Community-Acquired Pneumonia

Last verified: March 5, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Cough (productive or dry), fever, pleuritic chest pain, dyspnea1
  • Rigors and night sweats — common with bacterial etiologies
  • Elderly: may present atypically with confusion, falls, or functional decline without respiratory symptoms2

Exam Findings

  • Tachypnea, tachycardia
  • Focal crackles or bronchial breath sounds on auscultation
  • Dullness to percussion, increased tactile fremitus over affected area
  • Egophony (E-to-A change) → suggests consolidation
  • Pleural rub may indicate parapneumonic effusion1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Acute bronchitis
  • Influenza / COVID-19
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Heart failure with pulmonary edema
  • Lung malignancy
  • Eosinophilic pneumonia3

Immunocompromised

  • Pneumocystis jirovecii (PJP)
  • Fungal pneumonia (Histoplasma, Coccidioides)
  • Mycobacterial infection
  • Coxiella burnetii (Q fever) — travel/animal exposure3

Mimics

  • COPD or asthma exacerbation complicated by infection
  • Drug-induced pneumonitis (amiodarone, methotrexate)
  • Cryptogenic organizing pneumonia4

Diagnostic Tests

+

Chest X-Ray

  • Sn 73–92%, Sp 88–93% for lobar consolidation; PPV ~85%, NPV ~78%, +LR 6.1, -LR 0.255
  • Lateral decubitus views can identify layering effusions missed on standard PA/lateral

Procalcitonin

  • >0.25 ng/mL supports bacterial etiology: Sn 77%, Sp 79%, +LR 3.7, -LR 0.296
  • <0.1 ng/mL makes bacterial pneumonia unlikely

Lung Ultrasound (POCUS)

  • Sn 94%, Sp 96%, PPV 97%, NPV 93%, +LR 23.5, -LR 0.06 for consolidation7
  • Outperforms CXR in early disease and non-transportable patients

Microbiology

  • Sputum Gram stain/culture: Sn 50–60%, Sp ~80% — obtain before antibiotics1
  • Blood cultures: yield 5–14%; obtain in severe pneumonia
  • Legionella urinary antigen (serogroup 1): Sn 70–80%, Sp >99%, +LR >70, -LR 0.25
  • S. pneumoniae urinary antigen: Sn 60–75%, Sp 95%8

Treatment

+

Outpatient, no comorbidities1

  • Amoxicillin 1 g TID, OR
  • Doxycycline 100 mg BID, OR
  • Macrolide (azithromycin 500 mg day 1 → 250 mg days 2–5) if local pneumococcal resistance <25%

Outpatient with comorbidities (chronic lung/heart/liver/renal disease, diabetes, alcoholism, malignancy, asplenia)1

  • Amoxicillin-clavulanate OR cephalosporin (cefpodoxime, cefuroxime) PLUS macrolide or doxycycline, OR
  • Respiratory fluoroquinolone monotherapy (levofloxacin 750 mg daily or moxifloxacin 400 mg daily)

Inpatient, non-ICU1

  • Beta-lactam (ampicillin-sulbactam 1.5–3 g IV q6h, ceftriaxone 1–2 g IV daily, or cefotaxime 1–2 g IV q8h) PLUS macrolide or doxycycline, OR
  • Respiratory fluoroquinolone monotherapy

Inpatient, ICU1

  • Beta-lactam PLUS macrolide, OR beta-lactam PLUS respiratory fluoroquinolone
  • MRSA risk factors → add vancomycin or linezolid
  • Pseudomonas risk factors → piperacillin-tazobactam, cefepime, or meropenem PLUS fluoroquinolone or aminoglycoside

Duration1

  • Minimum 5 days; extend only if not clinically stable
  • Clinical stability = afebrile ≥48h, HR ≤100, RR ≤24, SBP ≥90, SpO2 ≥90% on room air

Expected Course

+
  • Defervescence within 48–72 hours of appropriate antibiotics9
  • Cough and fatigue may persist for weeks beyond clinical resolution
  • Radiographic clearing lags 4–12 weeks; slower in elderly, smokers, multilobar disease, COPD
  • Median hospital length of stay: 3–5 days
  • Failure to improve by 72h → reassess diagnosis; consider complications (empyema, lung abscess, ARDS), resistant organisms, or alternative diagnoses
  • 30-day mortality: 5–15% (hospitalized), 25–50% (ICU)1,9

Follow-up

+
  • Follow-up CXR at 6–12 weeks post-discharge for patients >50 y/o and smokers → rule out underlying malignancy1
  • Earlier reimaging if symptoms do not resolve as expected
  • Review pneumococcal and influenza vaccination status at discharge
  • Provide smoking cessation counseling
  • Post-discharge follow-up within 1–2 weeks to confirm clinical resolution1
  • Assess for cardiovascular complications (acute MI, new arrhythmia, worsening HF) — CAP significantly increases short-term CV risk10

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Without advanced imaging

  • Clinical diagnosis via history, vitals, and auscultation remains valid11
  • Lung ultrasound (if available) substitutes for CT
  • CURB-65 can risk-stratify without labs in some cases

Without subspecialty access

  • Most CAP managed effectively by generalists
  • Consult ID or pulmonology for non-resolving or recurrent pneumonia
  • Telemedicine can bridge access gaps

Formulary limitations1,11

  • Amoxicillin and doxycycline — widely available, inexpensive
  • Doxycycline substitutes for macrolides for atypical coverage
  • IV ampicillin + doxycycline as alternative if ceftriaxone unavailable
  • Penicillin G remains effective against susceptible pneumococci

The Lancet Infectious Diseases • February 14, 2026
Short-Course vs. Standard-Duration Antibiotic Therapy for Community-Acquired Pneumonia: Individual Patient Data Meta-Analysis
Study Design

Individual patient data meta-analysis of 12 RCTs (N=5,284). Compared 3–5 day vs. 7–14 day antibiotic courses for hospitalized adults with CAP. Primary outcome: clinical cure at 15–30 days.

Key Findings

Short-course therapy was noninferior to standard-duration therapy for clinical cure (87.3% vs. 88.1%; risk difference -0.8%, 95% CI -2.8% to 1.2%). No difference in 30-day mortality (3.1% vs. 3.4%). Short-course groups had lower rates of C. difficile infection (1.2% vs. 2.8%, p=0.003) and shorter hospital stays.

Bottom Line: 3–5 days of antibiotics is sufficient for most hospitalized CAP patients who reach clinical stability, with fewer adverse effects than longer courses.

ARDS Network: Low Tidal Volume Ventilation (ARMA Trial)

NEJM • 2000 • The Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome Network

Study Design

Multicenter, randomized controlled trial conducted across 10 university centers in the NHLBI ARDS Clinical Trials Network. Enrolled 861 patients with acute lung injury (ALI) or ARDS meeting the American-European Consensus Conference criteria. Patients were randomized to receive mechanical ventilation with either a traditional tidal volume of 12 mL/kg predicted body weight or a lower tidal volume of 6 mL/kg predicted body weight, with plateau pressure limits of 50 and 30 cm H2O, respectively.

Population

Adults with ALI/ARDS from diverse etiologies including pneumonia (35%), sepsis (22%), aspiration (15%), trauma (8%), and other causes (20%). Mean age was 51 years, and mean PaO2/FiO2 ratio at enrollment was approximately 130 mmHg. Patients receiving ventilation for more than 36 hours before meeting criteria were excluded.

Primary Endpoint

All-cause mortality before discharge home and breathing without assistance (assessed at 180 days). Secondary endpoints included ventilator-free days, organ failure-free days, and barotrauma rates.

Key Findings

The trial was stopped early by the Data Safety Monitoring Board after an interim analysis demonstrated a statistically significant mortality benefit. Mortality was 31.0% in the lower tidal volume group versus 39.8% in the traditional tidal volume group (absolute risk reduction 8.8%, relative risk reduction 22%, p=0.007, NNT=11). The lower tidal volume group also had significantly more ventilator-free days (mean 12 vs. 10 days, p=0.007) and more organ failure-free days. There was no significant difference in barotrauma rates between groups.

What Changed in Practice

This trial fundamentally transformed the approach to mechanical ventilation in ARDS and is considered one of the most important critical care trials ever conducted. Low tidal volume ventilation (6 mL/kg PBW) became the standard of care worldwide, effectively ending the era of high tidal volumes in ARDS management. The concept of "lung-protective ventilation" was cemented as a pillar of critical care practice.

What Critics Said

Critics argued that the control group's 12 mL/kg tidal volume was already higher than what many clinicians were using, potentially inflating the treatment effect. Some pointed out that the higher plateau pressures in the control group (rather than the tidal volume itself) might have been the primary driver of harm. The permissive hypercapnia required in the low tidal volume arm also raised concerns about CO2-related effects on intracranial pressure and hemodynamics.

Where It Stands Now

Low tidal volume ventilation remains the uncontested standard for ARDS management over two decades later. Subsequent studies have extended the concept of lung-protective ventilation to non-ARDS surgical patients. The 2023 ATS/ESICM/SCCM clinical practice guideline continues to recommend tidal volumes of 4–8 mL/kg PBW (with strong recommendation and moderate certainty). The concept of driving pressure (plateau minus PEEP) has emerged as potentially a more physiologically precise target than tidal volume alone, but low tidal volumes remain the foundation.

Cefepime vs. Piperacillin-Tazobactam in Suspected Sepsis: ACORN Extended Follow-up
RCT • Critical Care • Jan 8, 2026
+

Extended analysis of ACORN trial (N=2,511) confirmed no significant difference in 90-day mortality between cefepime and piperacillin-tazobactam in suspected sepsis. Bottom Line: Both remain reasonable first-line options for empiric sepsis coverage; monitor for cefepime neurotoxicity in renal impairment.
