Pericarditis and Pericardial Effusion

Last verified: March 11, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Chest pain: Sharp, pleuritic, improved by sitting up and leaning forward (>85-90% of cases)1
  • Dyspnea: Common with moderate to severe pericardial effusion2
  • Positional symptoms: Pain worsens when lying supine, improves when leaning forward1
  • Fever: May be present especially with infectious causes2
  • Fatigue and weakness: Non-specific symptoms reflecting hemodynamic compromise2
  • Palpitations: May occur with larger effusions or tamponade physiology2

Exam Findings

  • Pericardial friction rub: Superficial scratchy sound, best heard at left sternal border (≤33% of cases)1
  • Beck's triad (tamponade): Hypotension, jugular venous distension, muffled heart sounds3
  • Pulsus paradoxus: Systolic BP drop >10 mmHg with inspiration (suggests tamponade)3
  • Kussmaul sign: Paradoxical rise in JVP during inspiration3
  • Ewart sign: Dullness with bronchial breath sounds below left scapula (large effusion)3
  • Tachycardia: Compensatory response to decreased cardiac output2

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Myocardial infarction: ST elevation, troponin elevation, regional wall motion abnormalities4
  • Viral myocarditis: Elevated cardiac biomarkers with ventricular dysfunction4
  • Pleuritis: Unilateral chest pain, pleural friction rub1
  • Pneumonia: Fever, productive cough, lung consolidation1

Less Common

  • Aortic dissection: Tearing chest pain, pulse deficits, widened mediastinum4
  • Pulmonary embolism: Sudden dyspnea, hypoxemia, right heart strain4
  • Pneumothorax: Sudden chest pain, absent breath sounds1
  • Esophageal rupture: Post-vomiting chest pain, subcutaneous emphysema4

Mimics

  • Costochondritis: Chest wall tenderness, reproducible pain1
  • GERD: Burning chest pain, relation to meals1
  • Anxiety/panic disorder: Palpitations, shortness of breath, normal ECG1
  • Fibromyalgia: Widespread musculoskeletal pain1

Diagnostic Tests

+

Electrocardiogram

  • Diffuse concave ST elevation: Classic finding (up to 60% of cases)1
  • PR depression: Highly specific for pericarditis1
  • Low voltages: Suggests large pericardial effusion3
  • Electrical alternans: Classic finding in cardiac tamponade3

Echocardiography

  • Pericardial effusion detection: Primary method for confirming fluid around heart2
  • Tamponade signs: Right ventricular collapse, septal shift, respiratory variation3
  • Increased pericardial brightness: Non-specific marker of inflammation4

Laboratory Tests

  • C-reactive protein (CRP): Elevated in inflammatory pericarditis1
  • ESR: Inflammatory marker, useful for monitoring response1
  • Troponin: May be elevated if myocardial involvement (myopericarditis)4
  • Complete blood count: May show leukocytosis in infectious causes2

Advanced Imaging

  • Cardiac MRI: Pericardial late gadolinium enhancement, thickening >3mm5
  • CT scan: Better for detecting calcifications, pericardial thickening5
  • PET/CT: Can assess metabolic activity in chronic cases5

Treatment

+

Outpatient Management1,6

  • NSAIDs: Ibuprofen 600mg every 8 hours for 1-2 weeks OR aspirin 750-1000mg every 8 hours
  • Colchicine: 0.5mg daily (<70kg) or twice daily (≥70kg) for 3 months as adjunct
  • Gastroprotection: PPI recommended with NSAIDs
  • Activity restriction: Avoid physical activity beyond sedentary until symptoms resolve and CRP normalizes

Inpatient Non-ICU1,6

  • High-risk features: Fever, leukocytosis, large effusion, trauma, immunocompromised state
  • Same medical therapy: NSAIDs + colchicine as outpatient
  • Monitor for complications: Serial echocardiograms, clinical assessment
  • Specific etiology treatment: Antibiotics for bacterial, anti-TB therapy if indicated

ICU/Emergency Management3,7

  • Cardiac tamponade: Emergency pericardiocentesis or surgical drainage
  • Hemodynamic support: IV fluids, avoid positive pressure ventilation if possible
  • Pericardiocentesis: Echo or fluoroscopy-guided preferred approach
  • Surgical options: Pericardial window or pericardiectomy for refractory cases

Recurrent Pericarditis1,6

  • First recurrence: NSAIDs + colchicine for 6 months
  • Multiple recurrences: Consider low-dose corticosteroids (prednisone 0.2-0.5mg/kg/day)
  • Steroid-dependent: Consider immunosuppressive agents (azathioprine, methotrexate)
  • Refractory cases: IL-1 antagonists (anakinra, rilonacept) may be considered

Expected Course

+
  • Acute pericarditis: Most cases resolve within 1-2 weeks with appropriate treatment1
  • Recurrence rate: 15-30% after initial episode, up to 50% after first recurrence without colchicine6
  • Chronic pericarditis: Symptoms lasting >3 months, may require prolonged therapy6
  • Constrictive pericarditis: Rare complication (9% of acute cases), may require surgical intervention8
  • Tamponade: Life-threatening complication requiring immediate intervention3

Follow-up

+
  • Outpatient cases: Follow-up at 1 week to assess treatment response1
  • Symptom monitoring: Return if chest pain worsens, new dyspnea, or signs of tamponade1
  • CRP trending: Monitor inflammatory markers until normalization1
  • Echocardiogram: Repeat if symptoms worsen or large effusion present2
  • Athletes: Minimum 3-month activity restriction, cleared only after symptoms resolve and diagnostic tests normalize6
  • Recurrent cases: Regular cardiology follow-up, consider specialized pericarditis clinic5

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Basic Diagnostic Approach

  • Clinical diagnosis: Focus on characteristic chest pain pattern and physical exam
  • ECG: Most accessible test - look for ST elevation and PR depression
  • Chest X-ray: May show enlarged cardiac silhouette if large effusion

Treatment Modifications

  • Aspirin: More affordable alternative to other NSAIDs
  • Colchicine alternative: If unavailable, may use longer NSAID course
  • Monitor clinically: Focus on symptom improvement and vital signs

Cardiac Tamponade

Last verified: March 11, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Chest pain — vague, may be pleuritic or non-specific1
  • Shortness of breath and dyspnea on exertion1
  • Palpitations due to compensatory tachycardia1
  • Dizziness and syncope in severe cases1
  • Altered mental status due to decreased cardiac output1
  • Fatigue and weakness1

Exam Findings

  • Beck's triad — hypotension, jugular venous distension (JVD), and muffled heart sounds (present in minority of cases)1,2
  • Pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg (present in 66.6% of cases)3
  • Tachycardia — compensatory mechanism to maintain cardiac output1
  • Hypotension and signs of cardiogenic shock1
  • Elevated jugular venous pressure with prominent x descent1
  • Diminished heart sounds due to fluid accumulation1
  • Cool extremities and poor peripheral perfusion1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Constrictive pericarditis — differentiated by imaging and hemodynamics4
  • Right heart failure from other causes (pulmonary embolism, RV infarction)1
  • Cardiogenic shock from acute MI or decompensated heart failure1
  • Tension pneumothorax — may present with similar hemodynamics5

Less Common

  • Restrictive cardiomyopathy — may have similar filling patterns3
  • Large pleural effusions causing cardiac compression3
  • Severe asthma or COPD exacerbation with pulsus paradoxus3
  • Massive pulmonary embolism with acute right heart strain3

Mimics

  • Hypovolemic shock — may also cause pulsus paradoxus3
  • Septic shock with distributive hemodynamics1
  • Superior vena cava syndrome causing venous congestion1
  • Acute valvular regurgitation causing cardiogenic shock1

Diagnostic Tests

+

Electrocardiography

  • Sinus tachycardia — most common ECG finding1
  • Electrical alternans — beat-to-beat QRS amplitude variation, present in 17-23% of cases6
  • Low voltage QRS complexes due to pericardial fluid1
  • PR depression and diffuse ST changes (if concurrent pericarditis)1

Echocardiography1,7

  • Pericardial effusion — confirms presence and estimates size1
  • Right ventricular diastolic collapse — highly specific sign of tamponade1
  • Right atrial systolic collapse — early sign of tamponade physiology1
  • Ventricular septal shift and interdependence during respiration1
  • IVC distension with reduced respiratory variation7

Additional Imaging

  • Chest X-ray — enlarged cardiac silhouette ('water bottle' appearance) in large effusions1
  • CT chest — can detect pericardial effusion and assess for underlying pathology1
  • Cardiac MRI — detailed pericardial assessment, especially for constrictive vs. restrictive differentiation8

Treatment

+

Emergency Management1,9

  • Immediate pericardiocentesis — definitive treatment for hemodynamically significant tamponade1
  • IV fluid resuscitation — cautious volume expansion to improve preload1
  • Avoid diuretics — may worsen preload-dependent condition1
  • Inotropic support — dobutamine if hypotension persists after drainage1

Pericardiocentesis Technique9

  • Subxiphoid approach — preferred when feasible, lower risk of complications9
  • Echo or fluoroscopic guidance — reduces complications vs. blind approach9
  • Pigtail catheter placement — for continued drainage and monitoring9
  • Pericardial pressure monitoring — confirms resolution of tamponade physiology9

Post-Procedure Management1,9

  • Serial echocardiograms — monitor for reaccumulation1
  • Pericardial fluid analysis — cytology, culture, chemistry, PCR as indicated8
  • Treatment of underlying cause — malignancy, infection, autoimmune disease1
  • Surgical consultation — if recurrent or loculated effusions9

Expected Course

+
  • Immediate relief — rapid hemodynamic improvement after pericardiocentesis1
  • Recurrence risk — varies by etiology, highest with malignancy (up to 50%)1
  • Mortality — depends on underlying cause and rapidity of intervention1
  • Clinical context: Traumatic tamponade has highest acuity with rapid deterioration without intervention

Follow-up

+
  • Serial echocardiograms — at 24-48 hours, 1 week, and as clinically indicated1
  • Monitor for underlying etiology — oncology, rheumatology, infectious disease consultation as appropriate1
  • Activity restriction — until resolution of pericardial inflammation if concurrent pericarditis8
  • Surgical evaluation — if recurrent effusions or need for pericardial window/pericardiectomy9

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Clinical Assessment

  • Physical exam focus — assess for Beck's triad, pulsus paradoxus with manual BP cuff1
  • Basic ECG — look for tachycardia, low voltages, electrical alternans1
  • Point-of-care ultrasound — if available, assess for pericardial effusion and chamber collapse7

Management

  • Blind pericardiocentesis — subxiphoid approach if imaging guidance unavailable9
  • IV fluid challenge — cautious volume expansion while preparing for drainage1
  • Transfer considerations — arrange urgent transfer to facility with cardiothoracic surgery if available1

Osteomyelitis

Last verified: March 11, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Acute osteomyelitis: gradual onset over a few days, usually manifests within 2 weeks1
  • Local pain: dull pain with or without motion1
  • Back pain: most common symptom in vertebral osteomyelitis2
  • Constitutional symptoms: fever, chills, irritability, decreased appetite, lethargy, malaise1
  • Subacute presentation: generalized malaise, mild pain over several weeks with minimal fever1

Exam Findings

  • Local signs: erythema, swelling, warmth at site of infection1
  • Neurological impairment: occurs in approximately one-third of patients with vertebral osteomyelitis2
  • Probe-to-bone test: positive if bone is visible or encountered when ulcer is probed with sterile instrument3
  • Sinus tract: presence of fistulous tracts from skin to bone is diagnostic of chronic osteomyelitis4
  • Refusal to bear weight: in pediatric patients with limb involvement1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Cellulitis: soft tissue infection without bone involvement1
  • Septic arthritis: can occur if joint capsule is involved1
  • Fracture: especially stress fractures1
  • Diabetic foot ulcer: without underlying bone infection3

Less Common

  • Brodie abscess: subacute form of osteomyelitis typically through hematogenous spread1
  • Malignancy: primary bone tumor or metastatic disease3
  • Paget disease: can cause false-positive imaging findings4

Mimics

  • Arthritis: degenerative or inflammatory arthropathies4
  • Gout: crystal arthropathy4
  • SAPHO syndrome: synovitis, acne, pustulosis, hyperostosis, osteitis1

Diagnostic Tests

+

Laboratory Tests

  • ESR and CRP: often elevated but lack specificity; useful for monitoring response to therapy3
  • White blood cell count: often normal even in acute osteomyelitis3
  • Blood cultures: positive in hematogenous osteomyelitis; should be obtained in all suspected cases3
  • Bone biopsy with culture: gold standard for diagnosis, Sn 87%, Sp 93%3

Imaging Studies

  • Plain radiographs: first-line; 50-75% of bone matrix must be destroyed before lytic changes visible; Sn 70.4%, Sp 81.5%4
  • MRI: highly sensitive and specific; Sn 95.6%, Sp 80.7%; virtually excludes osteomyelitis if normal5
  • Three-phase bone scan: high sensitivity (83.6-94%); Sp reduced to 34% with hardware/recent surgery; Sp 70.6% overall6
  • CT: useful for sequestrum visualization and biopsy guidance; Sn 69.7%, Sp 90.2%4
  • 18F-FDG PET: Sn 85.1%, Sp 92.8%; excellent for chronic osteomyelitis4
  • 99mTc HMPAO WBC scan: Sn 87.3%, Sp 94.7%; comparable to MRI and PET5

Treatment

+

Acute Osteomyelitis - Outpatient7

  • Mild cases: oral antibiotics if pathogen identified and susceptible
  • Cephalexin 500mg PO QID for MSSA
  • Clindamycin 300-450mg PO TID for MSSA/MRSA (if susceptible)
  • Doxycycline 100mg PO BID for MSSA/MRSA (if susceptible)

Acute Osteomyelitis - Inpatient7

  • MSSA: Nafcillin 2g IV q4h or Cefazolin 2g IV q8h
  • MRSA: Vancomycin 15-20mg/kg IV q8-12h (target trough 15-20) or Linezolid 600mg IV/PO q12h
  • Gram-negative: based on culture and susceptibility
  • Duration: typically 6 weeks total; early switch to oral if clinically improving and CRP decreasing8

Chronic Osteomyelitis9

  • Surgical debridement: often required to remove sequestra and necrotic tissue
  • Rifampin adjunct: may improve cure rates, especially with prosthetic material
  • Oral therapy: acceptable alternative to IV with similar cure rates
  • Duration: 4-6 weeks; no evidence longer courses improve outcomes

Expected Course

+
  • Acute osteomyelitis: 70-80% cure rate with appropriate medical and surgical treatment1
  • Chronic osteomyelitis: recurrence rate 20-30%, up to 50% with P. aeruginosa1
  • Vertebral osteomyelitis: failure rates 10-30% in most clinical studies8
  • Complications: spinal deformity, paraplegia, sepsis if untreated2

Follow-up

+
  • Clinical assessment: improvement in pain, fever, local signs3
  • Laboratory monitoring: trending ESR and CRP; CRP <20mg/L suggests good response8
  • Imaging: repeat MRI if clinical deterioration or concern for complications4
  • Long-term follow-up: at least 1 year for high-risk patients or children with AHO10

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Clinical Diagnosis

  • Probe-to-bone test: positive if bone encountered when probing ulcer3
  • ESR and CRP: if available, useful for diagnosis and monitoring

Basic Imaging

  • Plain radiographs: first-line imaging, though insensitive early in disease
  • Ultrasound: can identify soft tissue abscesses and guide aspiration4

65 Trial — Permissive Hypotension in Critically Ill Elderly

JAMA • 2020 • Lamontagne F, et al.
Read on WikiJournalClub →

Clinical Question

In ICU patients aged ≥65 years with vasodilatory hypotension, what is the effect on 90 day mortality by reducing the vasopressor exposure through permissive hypotension of target MAP of 60-65 mmHg?

Study Design

Multicenter, pragmatic, open-label, randomized controlled trial conducted in 65 NHS ICUs in the UK. Enrollment from July 2017 to March 2019 with intention-to-treat analysis.

Population

Adults aged ≥65 years with vasodilatory hypotension despite fluid resuscitation, currently receiving vasopressors (≥0.1 µg/kg/min noradrenaline if used) with therapy likely requiring ≥6 additional hours. N=2,463 randomized (1,221 permissive hypotension, 1,242 usual care). Median age 75 years, 43% female, mean APACHE II score 20.9, 48% had septic shock.

Intervention

Permissive hypotension targeting MAP of 60-65 mmHg with trial-specific prompts/alarms on infusion pumps and medical notes. Choice of vasopressor left to treating clinician discretion.

Comparator / Control

Usual care without specific MAP target guidance.

Primary Endpoint

All-cause mortality at 90 days.

Key Findings

90-day mortality: 41% vs 43.8% (absolute risk difference −2.85; 95% CI, −6.75 to 1.05; P = 0.15; OR 0.89; 95% CI 0.76-1.04). Unadjusted comparison was not significant, but pre-specified covariate-adjusted analysis reached significance (OR 0.82; 95% CI 0.68-0.98). No difference in serious adverse events.

Strengths

Large multicenter pragmatic RCT with robust sample size, intention-to-treat analysis, and clinically relevant population. Addressed important question about MAP targets with potential to reduce vasopressor exposure and associated harms.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Open-label design, above-target MAPs achieved in permissive hypotension group (66.7 vs 72.6 mmHg), and complicated statistical interpretation as primary unadjusted analysis was non-significant while adjusted analysis reached significance despite authors appearing to prespecify no adjustments.

Practice Impact

As of August 2021, no guidelines had been published reflecting these results. The trial provided initial evidence that vasopressor use to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg might be reasonable but should not necessarily be the default strategy.

Why It Matters Now

This trial challenged the dogmatic MAP target of ≥65 mmHg derived from early sepsis studies and suggested that permissive hypotension may be safe in elderly critically ill patients, potentially reducing vasopressor-related complications while maintaining similar outcomes.
