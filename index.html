Based on: PubMed abstract
Placebo may be inferior compared to acetaminophen when given with morphine for the management of acute pain in the emergency department
Understanding optimal pain management combinations in emergency settings is crucial given the ongoing opioid crisis and need for effective multimodal analgesia.
Design: Prospective cohort study comparing patients who received intravenous acetaminophen with opioids versus those who received opioids alone • Outcome: Opioid requirement measured in Morphine Equivalent Units during Emergency Department stay
Population: Adult Emergency Department patients aged 18 years and older treated with opioids for painful conditions
Intervention: Intravenous acetaminophen plus opioids
Comparator: Opioids alone
Key Results
  • No significant difference in opioid consumption during ED stay between groups (10.0 MEU vs 10.0 MEU, p=0.53)
  • Trend toward decreased opioid requirement at 24h post-discharge (15.0 MEU vs 30.0 MEU, p=0.059)
  • Both groups achieved similar pain reduction (median NRS from 9.0 to 4.0) and patient satisfaction (>80%)
  • Nine minor adverse events recorded, equally distributed between groups
Clinical Interpretation
  • IV acetaminophen did not provide opioid-sparing effects during ED stay, contrary to expectations for multimodal analgesia
  • The trend toward reduced post-discharge opioid use suggests potential delayed benefits
  • Both approaches achieved similar pain control and patient satisfaction
Limitations
  • Single-center study
  • Non-randomized design
  • Relatively small sample size (116 patients)
Bottom Line: IV acetaminophen does not reduce opioid requirements during ED stay but may decrease post-discharge opioid use.
High confidence 📊 Informative

RECOVERY — Tocilizumab in COVID-19

Lancet • 2021 • RECOVERY Collaborative Group

Clinical Question

Does tocilizumab, an IL-6 receptor antagonist, reduce mortality and improve outcomes in hospitalized COVID-19 patients with hypoxia and evidence of systemic inflammation?

Study Design

Open-label, randomized controlled platform trial conducted across 131 UK hospitals. Patients randomized 1:1 to tocilizumab plus usual care versus usual care alone. Stratified by age, sex, level of respiratory support, and days since symptom onset. Unblinded design with blinded outcome assessment.

Population

Hospitalized COVID-19 patients with hypoxia (oxygen saturation <92% on air or receiving oxygen therapy) and evidence of systemic inflammation (CRP ≥75 mg/L). N=4,116 patients randomized between April 2020-January 2021. Mean age 63 years, 37% female, 26% receiving invasive mechanical ventilation, 41% non-invasive ventilation/high-flow oxygen.

Intervention

Tocilizumab 400-800mg IV (weight-based dosing: 8mg/kg, max 800mg) as single dose, with option for second dose if no clinical improvement. Given in addition to standard care including corticosteroids (82% received dexamethasone).

Comparator / Control

Usual care alone, which included corticosteroids in 82% of patients. No placebo given due to open-label design.

Primary Endpoint

28-day all-cause mortality

Key Findings

28-day mortality: 29% tocilizumab vs 33% usual care (RR 0.85, 95% CI 0.76-0.94, p=0.003). ARR = 4%, RRR = 13%, NNT = 25. Time to discharge: median 19 vs 28 days (rate ratio 1.22, 95% CI 1.12-1.33, p<0.0001). Progression to invasive mechanical ventilation, ECMO, or death: 33% vs 38% (RR 0.84, 95% CI 0.77-0.92). Benefit consistent across subgroups including corticosteroid use.

Strengths

Large sample size with adequate power. Pragmatic platform trial design allowing rapid recruitment. Conducted during peak pandemic with high event rates. Good baseline balance between groups. Pre-specified statistical analysis plan. Consistent benefit across multiple endpoints and subgroups.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Open-label design introducing potential bias. Single-dose strategy may not be optimal. Limited to patients with high CRP (≥75 mg/L). Conducted primarily in UK healthcare system limiting generalizability. No long-term follow-up beyond 28 days. Cost-effectiveness not assessed.

Practice Impact

Led to widespread adoption of tocilizumab in critically ill COVID-19 patients with systemic inflammation. Incorporated into WHO, NIH, and national treatment guidelines. Changed standard of care from corticosteroids alone to combination therapy with tocilizumab in appropriate patients. Influenced procurement and stocking decisions globally.

Why It Matters Now

While COVID-19 severity has decreased with vaccination and variants, tocilizumab remains important for severe cases. Established proof-of-concept for IL-6 inhibition in cytokine storm syndromes beyond COVID-19. Demonstrates value of platform trials for rapid evidence generation during health emergencies. Informs treatment of long COVID inflammatory complications and future pandemic preparedness strategies.

Hyponatremia

Last verified: March 13, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Neurological symptoms: Headaches, confusion, nausea, delirium1
  • Severe symptoms: Seizures, coma, altered mental status1
  • Chronic mild symptoms: Often asymptomatic or subtle cognitive impairment1
  • Gastrointestinal: Vomiting, nausea1

Exam Findings

  • Volume status assessment: Hypovolemic (dry mucous membranes, orthostasis), euvolemic (normal skin turgor), or hypervolemic (edema, elevated JVP)1
  • Neurological: Confusion, altered mental status, seizures in severe cases1
  • Signs of underlying condition: Heart failure, liver disease, adrenal insufficiency1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • SIADH: Most common cause of euvolemic hyponatremia1
  • Hypovolemic hyponatremia: GI losses (diarrhea, vomiting), diuretics, salt-wasting nephropathy1
  • Hypervolemic hyponatremia: CHF, liver cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, renal failure1
  • Drug-induced: Carbamazepine, SSRIs, chlorpropamide, cyclophosphamide1

Less Common

  • Cerebral salt wasting: CNS disorders (SAH, TBI, neurosurgery)1
  • Exercise-associated hyponatremia: Excessive fluid intake during endurance activities2
  • Endocrine causes: Hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency1
  • Psychogenic polydipsia: Psychiatric conditions with excessive water intake1

Mimics

  • Pseudohyponatremia: Hyperlipidemia, hyperproteinemia with normal osmolality3
  • Hyperglycemia: Osmotic shift causing factitious hyponatremia3
  • Mannitol/glycine: Osmotically active substances3

Diagnostic Tests

+

Initial Laboratory Tests1

  • Serum sodium: <135 mEq/L diagnostic; <125 mEq/L severe1
  • Serum osmolality: <280 mOsm/kg confirms hypotonic hyponatremia1
  • Urine osmolality: >100 mOsm/kg suggests impaired water excretion1
  • Urine sodium: >30 mEq/L suggests renal losses or SIADH1

Additional Tests1

  • TSH, cortisol: Rule out endocrine causes1
  • Fractional uric acid excretion: May help differentiate SIADH from cerebral salt wasting1
  • Volume status assessment: Clinical exam, CVP if available1

Treatment

+

Severe Symptomatic Hyponatremia (ICU)4,5

  • 3% hypertonic saline: 100-150 mL IV bolus q10min up to 3 boluses4
  • Target correction: 4-6 mEq/L increase in first 24 hours4
  • Monitor q2-4h: Frequent sodium checks to avoid overcorrection4
  • Desmopressin: Consider if correction rate >8 mEq/L/day to prevent ODS4

Moderate Symptomatic (Inpatient)4,5

  • 3% saline: 150 mL IV bolus, assess response4
  • Volume-specific therapy: Based on hypo/eu/hypervolemic classification4
  • Cause-specific treatment: Address underlying etiology4

Mild/Asymptomatic (Outpatient/Inpatient)4,5

  • Fluid restriction: 500-1000 mL/day initial approach4
  • Salt tablets: 1-2g daily to increase solute load4
  • Urea: 15-60g daily divided doses for SIADH4
  • Tolvaptan: 15mg daily (start low 3.75mg) for SIADH/CHF1

Volume-Specific Management1

  • Hypovolemic: Normal saline 0.5-1 mL/kg/hr + address losses1
  • Hypervolemic: Fluid restriction + loop diuretics + treat underlying condition1
  • Euvolemic (SIADH): Fluid restriction, urea, or vaptans1

Expected Course

+
  • Acute hyponatremia: Rapid correction possible without ODS risk if <48 hours duration1
  • Chronic hyponatremia: Gradual correction required; overcorrection risk for osmotic demyelination syndrome1
  • Correction rates: 4-6 mEq/L in 24h for chronic; up to 8 mEq/L acceptable in severe acute cases4
  • Clinical context: Most patients respond well to appropriate therapy targeting underlying cause

Follow-up

+
  • Inpatient monitoring: Sodium levels q4-6h during active treatment, then q12-24h4
  • Outpatient follow-up: Sodium check in 24-48h for mild cases, 1-2 weeks for stable chronic cases4
  • Medication review: Discontinue or adjust causative medications (diuretics, SSRIs, anticonvulsants)1
  • Underlying conditions: Treat CHF, cirrhosis, adrenal insufficiency, hypothyroidism1

Low-Resource Considerations

+

When 3% Saline Unavailable

  • Make hypertonic saline: Add 51g NaCl to 1L sterile water for 3% solution
  • Salt tablets + fluid restriction: 2-4g salt daily with 500mL water restriction
  • Normal saline: Use cautiously in hypovolemic hyponatremia only

Limited Monitoring

  • Clinical assessment: Monitor neurological status, symptoms improvement
  • Basic metabolic panel: If available, check q12-24h minimum
  • Urine specific gravity: Simple test to assess concentrating ability
