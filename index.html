Computed tomography angiography of the head and neck may inform emergency management in patients with suspected stroke
Optimizing CT angiography use in stroke evaluation can improve resource utilization while ensuring appropriate patient care.
Design: Retrospective cohort study of 1,445 ED patients at an academic Level 1 trauma center who underwent CTAHN in 2023 for 5 common neurologic presentations • Outcome: CTAHN findings that result in acute management change
Population: ED patients with acute neurologic symptoms (stroke code, headache, dizziness, altered mental status, vision changes)
Intervention: CT angiography of head and neck (CTAHN)
Key Results
  • 216 of 1,445 CTAHN studies (14.9%) resulted in acute management change
  • Highest yield for stroke codes (21.2%) and vision changes (14.7%), lowest for dizziness (4.0%)
  • For stroke codes: abnormal CT head (OR 2.84), focal neurologic deficit (OR 2.2), new symptoms (OR 2.39), maximal symptom severity at onset (OR 3.95), tobacco use (OR 2.02) predicted management change
  • NIHSS scores 10-14 had highest likelihood of management change
  • For headache: abnormal CT head (OR 4.98) and thunderclap presentation (OR 5.31) predicted management change
Clinical Interpretation
  • Clinical predictors can guide more selective CTAHN ordering
  • Isolated dizziness has very low yield and routine CTAHN may not be warranted
  • Abnormal noncontrast head CT significantly increases utility of subsequent CTA
Limitations
  • Single-center retrospective study
  • Limited to academic trauma center setting
Bottom Line: CTAHN is highest yield in stroke patients with focal deficits, abnormal head CT, or severe symptoms at onset, but low yield in isolated dizziness.

Placebo may be inferior compared to acetaminophen when given with morphine for the management of acute pain in the emergency department
Understanding optimal pain management combinations in emergency settings is crucial given the ongoing opioid crisis and need for effective multimodal analgesia.
Design: Prospective cohort study comparing patients who received intravenous acetaminophen with opioids versus those who received opioids alone • Outcome: Opioid requirement measured in Morphine Equivalent Units during Emergency Department stay
Population: Adult Emergency Department patients aged 18 years and older treated with opioids for painful conditions
Intervention: Intravenous acetaminophen plus opioids
Comparator: Opioids alone
Key Results
  • No significant difference in opioid consumption during ED stay between groups (10.0 MEU vs 10.0 MEU, p=0.53)
  • Trend toward decreased opioid requirement at 24h post-discharge (15.0 MEU vs 30.0 MEU, p=0.059)
  • Both groups achieved similar pain reduction (median NRS from 9.0 to 4.0) and patient satisfaction (>80%)
  • Nine minor adverse events recorded, equally distributed between groups
Clinical Interpretation
  • IV acetaminophen did not provide opioid-sparing effects during ED stay, contrary to expectations for multimodal analgesia
  • The trend toward reduced post-discharge opioid use suggests potential delayed benefits
  • Both approaches achieved similar pain control and patient satisfaction
Limitations
  • Single-center study
  • Non-randomized design
  • Relatively small sample size (116 patients)
Bottom Line: IV acetaminophen does not reduce opioid requirements during ED stay but may decrease post-discharge opioid use.

COMPASS — Rivaroxaban plus Aspirin in Stable CVD

NEJM • 2017 • Eikelboom JW et al.

Clinical Question

In patients with established stable atherosclerotic disease, is rivaroxaban plus aspirin more effective than aspirin alone in reducing cardiovascular death, stroke, or nonfatal MI?

Study Design

Randomized controlled trial with three-arm design comparing rivaroxaban 2.5mg BID plus aspirin, rivaroxaban 5mg BID alone, and aspirin alone. Trial was terminated early due to achievement of interim efficacy threshold at mean follow-up of 23 months.

Population

27,395 patients with established stable atherosclerotic disease were randomized. Note that 2,320 patients were excluded during the run-in phase, making the analytical population highly selected. See source for detailed inclusion/exclusion criteria.

Intervention

Rivaroxaban 2.5mg twice daily plus aspirin

Comparator / Control

Aspirin alone (with additional arm of rivaroxaban 5mg BID alone)

Primary Endpoint

Composite of cardiovascular death, stroke, or nonfatal MI

Key Findings

Rivaroxaban plus aspirin achieved 1.3% absolute risk reduction in primary outcome compared to aspirin alone, with 24% relative risk reduction. However, this was offset by 1.2% absolute increase in major bleeding. All-cause mortality was reduced by 0.7% with marginal significance. Net clinical benefit showed 1.2% absolute reduction in events favoring rivaroxaban plus aspirin.

Strengths

Large randomized trial addressing an important clinical question in stable atherosclerotic disease. Novel low-dose rivaroxaban strategy. Early termination for efficacy suggests robust signal.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Substantial number of patients (2,320) excluded during run-in phase, creating highly selected population. Increased major bleeding risk (primarily gastrointestinal) offset thrombotic benefits. Modified ISTH bleeding definition was broader than typical definitions.

Practice Impact

Established rivaroxaban plus aspirin as a legitimate therapeutic consideration in stable atherosclerotic disease, though requiring careful case-by-case risk-benefit assessment, particularly in patients whose high thrombotic risk substantially outweighs bleeding risk.

Why It Matters Now

Subsequent 2021 subgroup analysis confirmed benefits in symptomatic lower extremity PAD, validated by VOYAGER-PAD (2023). Current 2024 ACC/AHA/AACVPR guidelines now incorporate recommendations for antiplatelet therapy in PAD based partly on COMPASS findings, though full-intensity anticoagulation receives a Class 3/harm recommendation without other indications.

Syncope

Last verified: March 15, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Prodromal symptoms: dizziness, lightheadedness, diaphoresis, nausea, visual disturbances1
  • Patients describe events as fainting, blacking out, falling out, having a spell, or losing consciousness1
  • Episodes may occur suddenly and without warning1
  • Clinical context: Episodes are typically brief (seconds to minutes) with spontaneous recovery
  • Associated symptoms with vasovagal syncope: warmth, pallor, fatigue as usual aftermath2

Exam Findings

  • Complete loss of consciousness and postural tone followed by spontaneous recovery1
  • Clinical context: Normal neurologic examination after recovery
  • Associated findings with orthostatic syncope: lightheadedness, weakness in limbs, gait disturbances, cognitive impairment3
  • Clinical context: May have brief myoclonic jerks during unconsciousness (not true seizure activity)

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Vasovagal syncope: most common form; >85% of episodes in patients <40 years2
  • Cardiac syncope: estimated to be cause of 15% of syncopal events4
  • Orthostatic hypotension: classic, delayed, initial, and delayed blood pressure recovery forms3
  • Situational syncope: cough, micturition-associated2

Less Common

  • Carotid sinus syndrome: hypersensitivity with asystole ≥6s or SBP drop ≥50 mmHg2,5
  • Arrhythmogenic conditions: Brugada syndrome, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, ARVD, long QT syndrome6
  • Acute anemia or gastrointestinal bleeding6
  • Pulmonary embolism6

Mimics

  • Epileptic seizures: associated with hypersynchronous, rhythmic cortical neuron discharges7
  • Psychogenic non-epileptic spells: substance abuse, psychiatric disease6
  • Hypoglycemia6
  • Clinical context: Drop attacks, TIAs (usually without loss of consciousness)

Diagnostic Tests

+

Initial Evaluation1,5

  • History & physical examination: all patients should undergo complete evaluation including standing BP measurement5
  • 12-lead ECG: standard in all patients with syncope5
  • Orthostatic vital signs: lying, sitting, immediately standing, and after 3 minutes upright8

Specialized Testing1,3,5

  • Tilt table test: for suspected neurally mediated syncope, orthostatic hypotension evaluation9
  • Carotid sinus massage: patients >40 years with syncope of unknown origin compatible with reflex mechanism5
  • Echocardiogram: when known heart disease or data suggestive of structural heart disease5
  • External/implantable cardiac monitors: for suspected arrhythmic syncope8

Risk Stratification5

  • High-risk features: age >60, male sex, structural heart disease, brief prodrome, exertional syncope, family history of SCD <50 years8
  • Low-risk features: younger age, no cardiac disease, positional triggers, prolonged prodrome8

Treatment

+

Emergency Department/Inpatient1,5

  • High-risk patients: intensive diagnostic approach, urgent treatment, admission with monitoring up to 24 hours5
  • IV fluids if dehydrated; antibiotics if sepsis concern10
  • Structural heart disease: guideline-directed management and therapy (GDMT)11

Outpatient Management5,8

  • Reflex syncope: patient education, reassurance, trigger avoidance — cornerstone of treatment5
  • Severe reflex syncope: midodrine or fludrocortisone in young patients with low BP phenotype5
  • Counter-pressure maneuvers (including tilt training) in young patients with prodromes5
  • Long QT syndrome: beta-blockers first-line therapy (Class I)11

Device Therapy11

  • ICD reasonable in LQTS patients with arrhythmic syncope on beta-blockers (Class IIa)11
  • ICD not recommended in Brugada pattern with reflex syncope without other risk factors (Class III)11
  • Clinical context: Pacemaker therapy may be considered for carotid sinus syndrome with significant cardioinhibitory response

Expected Course

+
  • Syncope responsible for 1-3.5% of all ED visits and 6% of hospital admissions in US1
  • 30-40% lifetime prevalence; cardiac syncope carries 30% one-year mortality rate4
  • Vasovagal syncope: >85% of episodes in patients <40 years, >50% in geriatric patients2
  • Clinical context: Most vasovagal episodes are benign; cardiac causes have higher morbidity/mortality

Follow-up

+
  • Intermediate-risk patients: expert syncope opinion, can be managed safely in outpatient setting5
  • Risk stratification guides treatment; long-term prognosis based on underlying condition severity8
  • Athletes: cardiovascular assessment by experienced provider before resuming competitive sports11
  • Clinical context: Regular cardiology follow-up for structural heart disease; neurology consultation for suspected seizure disorders

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Essential Evaluation

  • Detailed history focusing on triggers, prodrome, witness account of event
  • Basic vital signs including orthostatic measurements
  • 12-lead ECG if available (essential for risk stratification)

Management

  • Patient education on trigger avoidance and hydration
  • Physical counter-pressure maneuvers training
  • Referral for high-risk features or recurrent episodes
