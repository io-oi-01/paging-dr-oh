Acute Respiratory Failure

Last verified: March 9, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Type 1 (Hypoxemic): Progressive dyspnea, fatigue, confusion, cyanosis1
  • Type 2 (Hypercapnic): Headache, somnolence, confusion, memory loss, impaired coordination2
  • Daytime somnolence and headaches (in chronic respiratory acidosis)2
  • Acute presentation: Sudden onset dyspnea, altered mental status1

Exam Findings

  • Respiratory signs: Tachypnea, accessory muscle use, paradoxical breathing1
  • Hypoxemic signs: Cyanosis, restlessness, tachycardia1
  • Hypercapnic signs: Altered mental status, asterixis, papilledema (if severe)2
  • Chronic signs: Polycythemia, pulmonary hypertension, heart failure2

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • COPD exacerbation (third most common etiology)1
  • Pneumonia (community-acquired, hospital-acquired)1
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)1
  • Cardiogenic pulmonary edema1

Less Common

  • Pulmonary embolism1
  • Pneumothorax1
  • Aspiration pneumonia1
  • Drug-induced respiratory depression (opioids, CNS depressants)2

Mimics

  • Neuromuscular disorders: Myasthenia gravis, muscular dystrophy, Guillain-Barré syndrome2
  • Central causes: Cerebrovascular accidents2
  • Obesity hypoventilation syndrome (Pickwickian syndrome)2

Diagnostic Tests

+

Arterial Blood Gas1,2

  • Type 1 RF: PaO₂ <60 mmHg, normal or decreased PaCO₂1
  • Type 2 RF: PaCO₂ >45 mmHg, pH <7.35, HCO₃⁻ >30 mmHg2
  • A-a gradient: Normal or increased depending on cause1

Chest Imaging1

  • Chest X-ray: Evaluate for infiltrates, pneumothorax, effusion1
  • CT chest: When chest X-ray inconclusive or complications suspected1

Laboratory Studies1

  • Complete blood count: Evaluate for infection, polycythemia1
  • Comprehensive metabolic panel: Assess acid-base status1
  • Cardiac biomarkers: Rule out myocardial infarction1

Treatment

+

Outpatient/Emergency Department3

  • Oxygen therapy: Target SpO₂ 88-92% in COPD, 94-98% in others3
  • Bronchodilators: Albuterol 2.5-5mg nebulized q20min x3, then q2-6h PRN3
  • Corticosteroids: Prednisolone 30-40mg daily x5 days for COPD exacerbation3

Inpatient Non-ICU3,4

  • Non-invasive ventilation (NIV): BiPAP for hypercapnic RF with pH ≤7.35, PaCO₂ >45 mmHg, RR >20-244
  • High-flow nasal cannula: 30-60 L/min for hypoxemic respiratory failure4
  • CPAP: 5-10 cm H₂O for cardiogenic pulmonary edema4

ICU Management4,5

  • Mechanical ventilation: Low tidal volume (6 mL/kg IBW) for ARDS5
  • PEEP: Titrate to optimize oxygenation while minimizing hemodynamic compromise5
  • Prone positioning: ≥16 hours/day for severe ARDS (PaO₂/FiO₂ <150)5
  • ECMO: Consider for refractory hypoxemia (PaO₂/FiO₂ <50, OI >40)5

Expected Course

+
  • Type 1 RF: Course depends on underlying etiology; ARDS mortality 35-46%1
  • Type 2 RF: Generally better prognosis with appropriate ventilatory support1
  • COVID-19 related: Up to 79% of hospitalized patients required mechanical ventilation early in pandemic1
  • Chronic complications: Prolonged mechanical ventilation leads to deconditioning, cognitive changes1

Follow-up

+
  • Acute phase: Close monitoring of ABG, respiratory status, hemodynamics1
  • Recovery phase: Gradual weaning of respiratory support, mobilization, nutrition optimization1
  • Post-discharge: Pulmonary rehabilitation for appropriate candidates1
  • Long-term: Treatment of underlying conditions (COPD, heart failure)1

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Basic Interventions

  • Pulse oximetry: Most cost-effective monitoring tool1
  • Simple oxygen delivery: Nasal cannula, simple face mask when advanced devices unavailable1
  • Position therapy: Semi-upright positioning, prone positioning when feasible1

Clinical context: Resource Allocation

  • Prioritize non-invasive ventilation when available to reduce intubation needs4
  • Early identification of patients suitable for comfort care to optimize resource utilization

Relevant Clinical Scores

qSOFA (Quick Sepsis-Related Organ Failure Assessment)

qSOFA (Quick Sepsis-Related Organ Failure Assessment) 0
Low risk. qSOFA negative. Continue standard monitoring.

Acute Cholecystitis

Last verified: March 9, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Right upper quadrant pain or epigastric pain lasting >6 hours1
  • Post-prandial pain triggered by fatty foods (“pork chops and gravy”)1
  • Nausea and vomiting with abdominal distention1
  • Referred pain to right scapular tip or mid-back1
  • Fever in acute cases1
  • Clinical context: Symptoms often begin 1-3 hours after fatty meals and may wax and wane initially

Exam Findings

  • Positive Murphy’s sign (cessation of inspiration during deep palpation of RUQ)1
  • Right upper quadrant tenderness with possible guarding1
  • Palpable gallbladder mass in complicated cases1
  • Fever and tachycardia in acute inflammation1
  • Clinical context: Murphy's sign may be absent in elderly patients or those on chronic pain medications

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Acute cholangitis — Charcot’s triad (fever, jaundice, RUQ pain)2
  • Acute pancreatitis — epigastric pain radiating to back, elevated lipase2
  • Peptic ulcer disease — epigastric pain, H. pylori association2
  • Hepatitis — elevated transaminases, tender hepatomegaly2

Less Common

  • Acute coronary syndrome — especially in elderly patients2
  • Pneumonia — right lower lobe with referred pain2
  • Kidney stones — right nephrolithiasis with colic2
  • Bowel obstruction — crampy pain, distention, nausea2

Mimics

  • Functional dyspepsia — chronic symptoms without inflammation2
  • GERD — burning epigastric pain, acid regurgitation2
  • Irritable bowel syndrome — chronic abdominal pain, altered bowel habits2
  • Sphincter of Oddi dysfunction — post-cholecystectomy pain2

Diagnostic Tests

+

Laboratory Studies1

  • CBC with differential: Leukocytosis (WBC >11,000/μL) with left shift1
  • C-reactive protein: Elevated ≥3 mg/dL supports acute inflammation3
  • Liver function tests: May show mild elevation; bilirubin >2 mg/dL suggests CBD stone1
  • Lipase/Amylase: Rule out concurrent pancreatitis1

Imaging Studies1,4

  • Right upper quadrant ultrasound: First-line imaging, readily available1
  • Gallbladder wall thickening >3mm, pericholecystic fluid, positive sonographic Murphy’s sign4
  • HIDA scan: If US negative but high suspicion; absence of GB filling diagnostic1
  • CT scan: If complications suspected or diagnosis unclear1

Treatment

+

Initial Management — All Settings1,5

  • NPO status and IV fluid resuscitation5
  • Analgesics: NSAIDs or opioids as needed for pain control5
  • Antiemetics: Ondansetron 4-8mg IV q8h PRN5

Antibiotic Therapy1,5

  • Grade I (Mild): Consider observation without antibiotics if no systemic signs5
  • Grade II (Moderate): Ceftriaxone 1-2g IV daily or Ampicillin-sulbactam 3g IV q6h5
  • Grade III (Severe): Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g IV q8h or Meropenem 1g IV q8h5

Surgical Management1,5

  • Early laparoscopic cholecystectomy (ELC): Within 72 hours of admission or 7-10 days from symptom onset5
  • Grade I-II: ELC preferred approach5
  • Grade III or high surgical risk: Percutaneous cholecystostomy as bridge to surgery5
  • Delayed surgery: If not candidate for ELC, delay ≥6 weeks after acute episode5

Expected Course

+
  • With appropriate treatment, most patients recover within 7-10 days
  • Without prompt treatment, may progress to gallbladder gangrene, empyema, or perforation
  • Clinical context: Recurrence rate is 10-30% if treated conservatively without cholecystectomy
  • Approximately 95% of acute cholecystitis cases are associated with gallstones

Follow-up

+
  • Post-surgical: Routine follow-up in 1-2 weeks for wound check and symptom resolution
  • Non-operative management: Close monitoring for complications, arrange elective cholecystectomy in 6-12 weeks
  • Elderly/high-risk patients: Consider percutaneous cholecystostomy with interval assessment for surgery candidacy
  • Recurrent symptoms: Urgent surgical consultation for possible complications

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Diagnostic Approach

  • Clinical diagnosis: Murphy’s sign + RUQ tenderness + fever/leukocytosis when imaging unavailable
  • Basic laboratory: CBC and basic metabolic panel if available
  • Point-of-care ultrasound: If available, look for gallstones and wall thickening

Treatment Options

  • Conservative management: IV fluids, pain control, broad-spectrum antibiotics when surgery unavailable
  • Open cholecystectomy: When laparoscopic capability not available
  • Drainage procedures: Percutaneous or surgical drainage for complicated cases

Delirium

Last verified: March 9, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Disturbance in attention: Reduced ability to direct, focus, sustain, or shift attention1
  • Altered awareness: Reduced orientation to the environment1
  • Fluctuating course: Symptoms develop over hours to days with marked variation in severity1
  • Sleep-wake cycle disruption: Nearly pathognomonic characteristic leading to "sundowning"2
  • Cognitive deficits: Memory impairment, disorientation, perceptual disturbances3
  • Motor subtypes: Hyperactive (agitated), hypoactive (somnolent), or mixed presentations4

Exam Findings

  • Inattention: Difficulty focusing or shifting attention during examination1
  • Disorganized thinking: Rambling, irrelevant conversation, unclear logical flow1
  • Altered level of consciousness: Either hypervigilant or lethargic1
  • Perceptual disturbances: Visual or auditory hallucinations, illusions1
  • Psychomotor agitation or retardation: May fluctuate between hyperactive and hypoactive states2

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Dementia: Chronic, progressive cognitive decline vs. acute, fluctuating course of delirium5
  • Depression: Personal withdrawal, slowed speech but usually oriented2
  • Psychosis: Primary psychiatric disorder without underlying medical cause2
  • Medication-induced sedation: Altered sensorium from sedatives, may mimic hypoactive delirium6

Less Common

  • Catatonia: Motor abnormalities with preserved consciousness1
  • Severe mood disorders: May present with psychotic features1
  • Toxic encephalopathy: From substances like anticholinergics (diphenhydramine)7

Mimics

  • Coma or stupor: Complete inability to respond, not classified as delirium per DSM-5-TR6
  • Acute encephalopathy: Generalized brain pathophysiology developing rapidly6
  • ICU psychosis (outdated term): Previously used misnomer for hyperactive ICU delirium3

Diagnostic Tests

+

Delirium Screening Tools1

  • Confusion Assessment Method (CAM): Sn 94-100%, Sp 90-95%1
  • CAM-ICU: For mechanically ventilated patients, Sn 95-100%, Sp 93-98%1
  • 4AT: Validated bedside screening tool1
  • Delirium Rating Scale-Revised-98 (DRS-R-98): For severity assessment1

Laboratory Assessment1

  • Comprehensive metabolic panel: Glucose, electrolytes, renal function1
  • Complete blood count: Infection markers, anemia1
  • Arterial blood gas: Hypoxemia, hypercapnia, acid-base status1
  • Urinalysis: Urinary tract infection1
  • Medication levels: When appropriate (digoxin, lithium)1

Neuroimaging1

  • Head CT: When focal neurological signs, trauma, or acute change1
  • MRI: For suspected stroke, encephalitis, or complex cases1
  • EEG: When seizures suspected or prolonged altered consciousness1

Treatment

+

Outpatient Management8

  • Address underlying causes: Medication review, treat infections, metabolic abnormalities8
  • Nonpharmacologic interventions: Environmental modifications, reorientation, family presence8
  • Avoid antipsychotics for prevention or routine treatment unless severe agitation8

Inpatient Non-ICU8

  • Multicomponent interventions: Hospital Elder Life Program (HELP) model8
  • Risk factor modification: Pain control, sleep hygiene, early mobilization8
  • Medication management: Discontinue deliriogenic medications, avoid benzodiazepines8
  • Antipsychotics if needed: Haloperidol 0.5-1 mg PO/IV q6h PRN severe agitation8
  • Monitor QTc: With antipsychotic use, especially haloperidol8

ICU Management9

  • ABCDEF Bundle: Assess, manage pain, minimize sedation, assess delirium, early mobility9
  • Sedation strategy: Dexmedetomidine preferred over propofol for delirium reduction9
  • Avoid benzodiazepines: Except for alcohol/sedative withdrawal9
  • Melatonin: 1-6 mg daily for sleep-wake cycle regulation9
  • Antipsychotics: Limited evidence for routine use in ICU delirium9

Expected Course

+
  • Duration: Most episodes resolve within days to weeks with treatment of underlying causes1
  • Mortality risk: Associated with increased morbidity and mortality1
  • Cognitive sequelae: May have persistent cognitive impairment, especially in elderly1
  • Functional decline: Risk of prolonged hospitalization and discharge to higher level of care2
  • Post-traumatic stress: Possible psychological trauma requiring assessment and support8

Follow-up

+
  • Delirium resolution assessment: Continue structured screening until resolved8
  • Medication reconciliation: Review and discontinue unnecessary psychotropic medications8
  • Cognitive assessment: Evaluate for persistent cognitive impairment8
  • Functional status: Physical therapy evaluation for mobility and safety8
  • Caregiver education: Signs of recurrence, prevention strategies8

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Clinical Assessment

  • Bedside cognitive testing: Simple attention tests (spell WORLD backwards)1
  • Family history: Baseline cognitive function from relatives1
  • Vital signs: Temperature, pulse, BP for infection or metabolic causes1

Management Priorities

  • Treat reversible causes: Infections, electrolyte abnormalities, hypoxia1
  • Environmental modifications: Adequate lighting, minimize noise, familiar objects1
  • Avoid restraints: Unless imminent safety risk8
  • Basic medication review: Discontinue non-essential medications1

IDEAL — Timing of Dialysis Initiation

NEJM • 2010 • Cooper BA, et al.
Read on WikiJournalClub →

Clinical Question

In patients with stage V CKD, does the initiation of early dialysis improve survival or outcomes?

Study Design

Multicenter, parallel-group, randomized, controlled trial conducted at 32 centers in Australia and New Zealand. Enrollment from 2000-2008 with mean follow-up of 3.6 years. Intention-to-treat analysis.

Population

828 patients with progressive CKD and estimated GFR between 10-15 ml/min/1.73 m² (using Cockcroft-Gault). Mean age 60.4 years, 71.5% white, 42.9% with diabetes, 38.9% with cardiovascular disease. Early dialysis arm n=404, late dialysis arm n=424.

Intervention

Early dialysis initiation when GFR 10-14 ml/min/1.73 m²

Comparator / Control

Late dialysis initiation when GFR 5-7 ml/min/1.73 m² or when GFR >7 ml/min at physician discretion for uremic symptoms or difficult electrolyte abnormalities

Primary Endpoint

All-cause mortality

Key Findings

No significant difference in all-cause mortality: 10.2 vs 9.8 events per 100 patient-years (HR 1.04; 95% CI 0.83-1.30; P=0.75). No significant differences in composite CV events (10.9 vs 8.8, HR 1.23; 95% CI 0.97-1.56; P=0.09), infectious events (12.4 vs 14.3, HR 0.87; 95% CI 0.70-1.08; P=0.20), or dialysis complications. Time to dialysis initiation was significantly shorter in early group: 1.80 vs 7.40 months (HR 2.09; 95% CI 1.81-2.41; P<0.001).

Strengths

First and only randomized controlled trial examining timing of dialysis initiation and mortality. Large sample size with robust intention-to-treat analysis. Pragmatic design allowing for clinical judgment in starting dialysis for symptoms.

Weaknesses / Limitations

No standardized assessment of creatinine was used. Used Cockcroft-Gault equation rather than MDRD. In practice, 76% of patients assigned to late dialysis started dialysis when GFR was ≥5-7 ml/min/1.73 m² due to symptoms, reducing the actual difference between groups (mean GFR at dialysis start: 12.0 vs 9.8 ml/min/1.73 m²).

Practice Impact

This trial supported current practice that dialysis should be initiated in symptomatic patients due to uremia or in asymptomatic patients with extremely low GFR (approximately 10 ml/min/1.73 m²). It challenged the practice of very early dialysis initiation in asymptomatic patients.

Why It Matters Now

IDEAL remains the definitive evidence against early dialysis initiation purely based on GFR thresholds. Current guidelines emphasize symptom-driven initiation, and this trial continues to inform the timing of renal replacement therapy decisions. The findings support a patient-centered approach rather than rigid GFR cutoffs for dialysis initiation.
