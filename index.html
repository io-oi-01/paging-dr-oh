Computed tomography angiography of the head and neck may inform emergency management in patients with suspected stroke
Understanding when CT angiography is most likely to change acute stroke management can improve resource utilization and reduce unnecessary imaging while ensuring appropriate care.
Design: Retrospective cohort study of 1,445 ED patients at an academic Level 1 trauma center who underwent CTAHN in 2023 for 5 common neurologic presentations • Outcome: CTAHN findings resulting in acute management change
Population: Emergency department patients with acute neurologic symptoms (stroke code, headache, dizziness, altered mental status, vision changes)
Intervention: CT angiography of head and neck (CTAHN)
Key Results
  • 216 of 1,445 CTAHN studies (14.9%) resulted in acute management change
  • Highest yield for stroke codes (21.2%) and vision changes (14.7%), lowest for dizziness (4.0%)
  • For stroke codes, predictors of management change included abnormal CT head (OR 2.84), focal neurologic deficit (OR 2.2), new vs recurring symptoms (OR 2.39), maximal symptom severity at onset (OR 3.95), and tobacco use (OR 2.02)
  • NIHSS scores of 10-14 had highest likelihood of CTAHN acute management change
  • For headache, abnormal CT head (OR 4.98) and thunderclap presentation (OR 5.31) predicted management change
Clinical Interpretation
  • These clinical predictors can help emergency physicians and neurologists make more informed decisions about when CTAHN is likely to be high-yield, potentially reducing unnecessary imaging while maintaining quality care
Limitations
  • Retrospective single-center design
  • Only univariate trends identified for some presentations due to small sample sizes
Bottom Line: CT angiography of head and neck has highest yield in stroke codes with abnormal CT, focal deficits, or new symptoms, and can be guided by specific clinical predictors.

Intervention: Intravenous acetaminophen plus opioids

  • Single-center study
  • Non-randomized design
  • Relatively small sample size (116 patients)

AFFIRM — Rate vs Rhythm Control in AF

NEJM • 2002 • Wyse DG, et al.

Clinical Question

Among patients with atrial fibrillation and a high risk of stroke or death, what are the effects of rate control versus rhythm control on mortality?

Study Design

Multicenter, parallel-group, randomized controlled trial conducted at 213 clinical sites. Median follow-up of 3.5 years with intention-to-treat analysis. N=4,060 patients randomized to rate-control (n=2,027) vs rhythm-control (n=2,033) strategies.

Population

Patients with nonvalvular atrial fibrillation. Key inclusion: age ≥65 years with AF likely to be recurrent, AF likely to cause illness/death, warranted long-term AF treatment, other stroke/death risk factors. Key exclusion: contraindication to anticoagulation, ineligible for ≥2 medications in either strategy. Mean age 70 years, 39% female, 23% had heart failure, 26% CAD, 51% hypertension.

Intervention

Rhythm-control strategy using antiarrhythmic agents (Class Ia: quinidine, procainamide, disopyramide; Class Ic: flecainide, propafenone, moricizine; Class III: amiodarone, sotalol, dofetilide) with cardioversion as needed. Warfarin anticoagulation could be stopped if sinus rhythm maintained ≥4 weeks.

Comparator / Control

Rate-control strategy targeting heart rate <80 bpm at rest and <110 bpm during activity using beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers (verapamil, diltiazem), and/or digoxin. Continuous warfarin anticoagulation (goal INR 2-3).

Primary Endpoint

All-cause mortality at 5 years.

Key Findings

5-year mortality: 25.9% (rate control) vs 26.7% (rhythm control), HR 1.15 (95% CI 0.99-1.34, P=0.08) - no significant difference with trend favoring rate control. Composite endpoint (death, disabling stroke, disabling anoxic encephalopathy, major bleeding, cardiac arrest): 32.7% vs 32.0% (P=0.33). Hospitalizations significantly higher with rhythm control: 73.0% vs 80.1% (P<0.001). Ischemic stroke rates similar: 5.5% vs 7.1% (P=0.79), ~1% annually in both groups.

Strengths

First and largest trial comparing rate vs rhythm control strategies. Large sample size with adequate power. Multicenter design enhancing generalizability. Intention-to-treat analysis. Appropriate median follow-up of 3.5 years.

Practice Impact

Challenged the assumption that rhythm control was superior to rate control in AF management. Established rate control as a reasonable first-line strategy for many patients with AF, particularly older patients without heart failure who showed particular benefit from rate control.

Why It Matters Now

AFFIRM's findings have been refined by subsequent trials. EAST-AFNET 4 (2020) showed early rhythm control (<1 year from diagnosis) reduces AF-related events. This led to updated 2023 ACC/AHA guidelines recommending rhythm control for recent-onset AF (<1 year) and emphasizing shared decision-making between rate vs rhythm strategies. Current practice balances AFFIRM's safety data for rate control with emerging evidence supporting early rhythm control in appropriate patients.

Adrenal Crisis

Last verified: March 16, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Nonspecific early symptoms: fatigue, weakness, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, back pain, diarrhea, dizziness1
  • Cardiovascular: hypotension, syncope1
  • Neurological progression: obtundation, metabolic encephalopathy1
  • Shock: rapid progression to shock1

Exam Findings

  • Vital signs: hypotension, fever2
  • Volume depletion: Clinical evidence of dehydration and volume loss2
  • Altered mental status: obtundation to metabolic encephalopathy1
  • Hyperpigmentation: diffuse and most prominent in sun-exposed areas (in primary adrenal insufficiency)2

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Septic shock: Similar presentation with hypotension and altered mental status
  • Hypovolemic shock: Dehydration, hypotension from other causes
  • Diabetic ketoacidosis: Nausea, vomiting, dehydration, electrolyte abnormalities
  • Acute gastroenteritis: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, dehydration

Less Common

  • Myocardial infarction: Chest pain, hypotension, shock
  • Pulmonary embolism: Sudden onset hypotension, collapse
  • Acute abdomen: Abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting
  • Drug overdose: Altered mental status, hypotension

Mimics

  • Hypothyroidism (myxedema coma): Altered mental status, hypothermia, hypotension
  • Hypoglycemia: Altered mental status, sweating, weakness
  • Psychiatric conditions: Fatigue, weakness, poor appetite
  • Chronic fatigue syndrome: Persistent fatigue, weakness

Diagnostic Tests

+

Laboratory Tests1,2

  • Basic metabolic panel: hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia2
  • Random serum cortisol: Cannot confirm or exclude diagnosis unless unequivocally elevated2
  • ACTH level: Elevation of ACTH with low cortisol is diagnostic of primary adrenal problem2
  • Plasma renin activity: Often elevated, indicative of mineralocorticoid deficiency2

Dynamic Testing3

  • Cosyntropin stimulation test: Diagnostic gold standard for primary adrenal insufficiency3
  • Standard dose: 250 μg for adults, 15 μg/kg for infants, 125 μg for children <2 years4
  • Interpretation: Peak cortisol levels below 500 nmol/L (18 μg/dL) at 30 or 60 minutes indicate adrenal insufficiency4

Treatment

+

Emergency Management - ICU1,5

  • Hydrocortisone IV: 100 mg IV or IM immediately, followed by 200 mg over next 24 hours1
  • Alternative dosing: 50 mg IV/IM every 6 hours1
  • Fluid resuscitation: 1 liter isotonic saline within first hour, then 4-6 liters in 24 hours5
  • Glucose support: Parenteral glucose therapy if hypoglycemic5

Inpatient Non-ICU5,6

  • Continuous infusion preferred: Expert consensus recommends continuous IV infusion as preferred mode6
  • Tapering: After 2-3 days, reduce to 100-150 mg over 24h, then gradually taper to maintenance7
  • Mineralocorticoid: No additional mineralocorticoid needed if hydrocortisone ≥100 mg/24h7

Outpatient Stress Dosing5

  • Sick day rule 1: Double routine oral glucocorticoid dose during fever or illness requiring bed rest5
  • Emergency injection: 100 mg IM hydrocortisone followed by 1 liter IV saline over 30 minutes8
  • Emotional stress: Additional 10 mg hydrocortisone for significant emotional stress1

Expected Course

+
  • Response timeline: Clinical improvement, especially blood pressure response, should be evident within 4-6 hours of hydrocortisone7
  • Mortality risk: Substantial mortality rate if not treated promptly1
  • Recovery: Most patients recover completely with appropriate treatment and supportive care
  • Recurrence risk: Approximately 8% of patients with adrenal insufficiency will experience crisis annually8

Follow-up

+
  • Endocrinology consultation: Involve endocrinologist as soon as possible for appropriate management guidance1
  • Regular appointments: Schedule follow-up with endocrinologist for monitoring and optimizing treatment1
  • Patient education: Educate about signs and symptoms of adrenal insufficiency for early recognition1
  • Steroid emergency card: Maintain easily accessible steroid emergency card with dosing information1

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Essential Equipment

  • Injectable hydrocortisone: Patients should carry vial of injectable hydrocortisone, needle and syringe at all times8
  • Medical alert jewelry: Medical alert bracelet or necklace is paramount for managing this condition1
  • Extra medication: Carry additional hydrocortisone tablets8

Alternative Treatment

  • Dexamethasone option: 4 mg initial bolus if undiagnosed patient (does not interfere with cortisol assays)9
  • IM route: When IV access difficult, intramuscular hydrocortisone can be used
  • Oral doubling: Double daily hydrocortisone dosage during intercurrent illness8
