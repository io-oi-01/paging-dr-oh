Acute Decompensated Heart Failure

Last verified: March 8, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Dyspnea and respiratory distress: overt respiratory distress in acute HF, significant dyspnea and respiratory decompensation from pulmonary edema1
  • Orthopnea: orthopnea present in patients with acute HF1
  • Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea: paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea in acute HF patients1
  • Anxiety and diaphoresis: anxiety and diaphoresis in patients with severe CHF or acutely decompensated HF1
  • Air hunger: patients frequently report air hunger, chest tightness2

Exam Findings

  • Tachycardia and tachypnea: tachycardia and tachypnea in acutely decompensated HF1
  • Pulmonary rales/crackles: classical finding of pulmonary rales translates to heart failure of moderate-to-severe intensity, absence of rales does not exclude pulmonary congestion1
  • Wheezing: wheezing may be present in acute decompensated heart failure1
  • Jugular venous distention: classical finding that must be assessed in all patients with HF1
  • Hepatojugular reflux: sustained increase in JVP >4 cm after applying pressure over the liver at 45° angle1
  • Frothy, blood-tinged sputum: as severity of pulmonary congestion increases1
  • Signs of poor perfusion: hypotension, reduced capillary refill, cold extremities, poor mentation, reduced urine output1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Pneumonia: interalveolar fluid produces crackles, most commonly in pneumonia2
  • Asthma exacerbation: acute presentations occur in asthma exacerbation2
  • Acute coronary syndrome: Clinical context: frequently precipitates or mimics acute heart failure

Less Common

  • Pulmonary embolism: acute presentations occur in pulmonary embolism2
  • Pneumothorax: conditions that limit airflow result in decreased breath sounds2
  • Pleural effusion: results in decreased breath sounds2

Mimics

  • Anxiety disorder: Clinical context: can present with dyspnea, tachycardia, and chest discomfort
  • Upper airway obstruction: inspiratory stridor typically indicates upper airway obstruction above vocal cords2
  • Anaphylaxis: wheezing from bronchoconstriction may occur in anaphylaxis2

Diagnostic Tests

+

Laboratory Tests1

  • BNP/NT-proBNP: Clinical context: elevated in volume overload and ventricular dysfunction
  • Creatinine/eGFR: 27.4% have mild renal dysfunction (GFR 60-89), 43.5% moderate (GFR 30-59), 13.1% severe (GFR 15-29)3
  • Electrolytes: Clinical context: assess for hyponatremia, hypokalemia from diuretics

Imaging1

  • Chest X-ray: chest radiographs help assess for signs of pulmonary congestion or edema in acute decompensated heart failure1
  • Echocardiography: Clinical context: assess ejection fraction, wall motion, valve function
  • ECG: Clinical context: identify arrhythmias, ischemia, conduction abnormalities

Risk Assessment Tools4

  • ADHERE CART Model: outcome predictions for acute decompensated heart failure4
  • AHA Get With Guidelines Score: outcome predictions for acute decompensated heart failure4
  • EFFECT Risk Score: outcome predictions for acute decompensated heart failure4

Treatment

+

Acute Stabilization (Inpatient)1,4,5

  • Ventilatory support: key component of therapy for acute decompensated heart failure without shock4
  • Loop diuretics IV: loop diuretics favored over thiazide diuretics, DOSE trial found no difference between bolus vs continuous IV4
  • Acetazolamide: ADVOR trial showed adding acetazolamide to loop diuretics increases successful decongestion within 3 days4

ICU Management (Cardiogenic Shock)6,9

  • Inotropes: consideration of milrinone or dobutamine with or without mechanical ventricular assist device4
  • Vasopressors + inotropes: combination of norepinephrine or epinephrine with inotropic agents when CPO/PAPi and BP decreased4
  • Milrinone: used in ICU for cardiac support in acute heart failure9
  • IABP: commonly used for temporary circulatory support as bridge therapy8

Chronic Management (Pre-discharge HFrEF/HFmrEF)4

  • ACE inhibitors/ARBs/ARNIs: early initiation in stabilized acute decompensated HFrEF before discharge offers benefits4
  • Beta-blockers: early initiation in stabilized patients before discharge4
  • MRAs: mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists early initiation offers benefits4
  • SGLT2 inhibitors: therapy before hospital discharge offers benefits4

Contraindications4

  • Avoid in HFpEF acute decompensation: medications not recommended for unstable patients with acutely decompensated HFpEF4
  • Avoid thiazolidinediones: contraindicated in acutely decompensated heart failure4
  • Avoid DPP-4 inhibitors: sitagliptin or saxagliptin contraindicated4

Expected Course

+
  • Progressive disease: HF is a progressive disease, as highlighted by ACC/AHA Stages A-D10
  • Recurrent hospitalizations: preventing recurrent hospitalizations essential to decrease morbidity and mortality1
  • EF trajectory matters: changes in EF over time more prognostic than baseline EF; patients progressing to HFrEF have worse prognosis4
  • Cardiorenal syndrome risk: 91% of ADHF patients have some degree of renal dysfunction on admission3

Follow-up

+
  • Weight monitoring: patient weight should be checked during each visit to track response and progression5
  • Medication optimization: all patients with current or prior HF should be considered for guideline-directed medical therapy10
  • Heart failure specialty team: guideline emphasizes care coordination with heart failure specialty team11
  • Trigger identification: identifying triggers including infection, medication noncompliance, NSAIDs, increased salt intake is essential1
  • Advanced HF referral: patients with advanced HF should be referred to HF team including palliative care12

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Clinical Assessment

  • Daily weights: Clinical context: most accessible monitoring tool for volume status
  • JVD assessment: JVD can be marker for fluid overload; patient at 45-degree angle for accurate assessment5

Basic Interventions

  • Salt restriction: salt and water restriction generally indicated for patients with HF8
  • Oral diuretics: Clinical context: when IV access limited, high-dose oral loop diuretics
  • Patient education: Clinical context: medication compliance, daily weights, symptom recognition

Relevant Clinical Scores

qSOFA (Quick Sepsis-Related Organ Failure Assessment)

qSOFA (Quick Sepsis-Related Organ Failure Assessment) 0
Low risk. qSOFA negative. Continue standard monitoring.

Asthma Exacerbation

Last verified: March 8, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Intermittent dyspnea, cough, and wheezing — hallmark symptoms that can be exacerbated by triggers ranging from allergens to viral infections1
  • Shortness of breath that can be frequently exacerbated by triggers1
  • Nocturnal cough — particularly occurring at night or in response to triggers like cold air or exercise; persistent cough following viral infection may suggest asthma2
  • Diurnal variation with symptoms being worse at night3
  • Poor school performance and excessive daytime fatigue due to disrupted sleep from nocturnal symptoms2

Exam Findings

  • Bilateral expiratory wheeze on auscultation during acute exacerbation3
  • Fine tremor in hands due to albuterol use and mild tachycardia; patients show respiratory distress, often sitting forward to splint open airways3
  • Tachypnea, hypoxia, prolonged expiratory phase, and accessory muscle use (subcostal, intercostal, supraclavicular retractions) during acute exacerbation2
  • Nasal flaring, tripod positioning, inability to speak in complete sentences, or grunting2
  • Silent chest in life-threatening asthma as air cannot enter or leave lungs, with signs of systemic hypoxia3
  • Altered mental status, lethargy, unresponsiveness, cyanosis indicating impending respiratory failure2

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Congestive heart failure — can present with wheezing; distinguished by history of orthopnea and paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, fine basal crackles, and characteristic chest X-ray features4
  • Chronic upper airway cough syndrome ('postnasal drip'), chronic sinusitis5
  • Inducible laryngeal obstruction (vocal cord dysfunction), upper airway cough syndrome6

Less Common

  • Gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), ACE inhibitor-induced cough5
  • Bronchiectasis, bronchiolitis obliterans — other chronic airway diseases that can mimic asthma4
  • Tracheobronchomalacia in patients with wheeze6

Mimics

  • COPD — especially in older patients with wheeze6
  • Obesity-related wheezing6
  • Clinical context: Pulmonary embolism, pneumothorax, pneumonia in patients presenting with acute dyspnea

Diagnostic Tests

+

Peak Flow Measurement7

  • Peak flow rate <50% without improvement despite aggressive therapy warrants hospital admission7
  • Peak expiratory flow below 50% needs immediate medical care8
  • Peak flow measurements indicate severe airway obstruction and constitute a medical emergency7

Spirometry and Bronchodilator Response9

  • Positive bronchodilator response: FEV1 increase >12% and >200 mL (preferably >15% and >400 mL) after 200-400 mcg albuterol9
  • Reversible obstruction via spirometry after bronchodilator is suggestive of asthma diagnosis10

Other Tests3

  • Pulse oximetry — useful for assessing severity; falling pO2 is a late finding indicating severely unwell or peri-arrest patient3
  • Chest radiograph may reveal hyperinflation but often cannot distinguish between asthma, COPD, and ACO4

Treatment

+

Acute Mild-to-Moderate Exacerbation (Outpatient)9

  • Albuterol (SABA): 4-10 puffs every 20 minutes within first hour for mild-to-moderate exacerbations9
  • After first hour: 4-10 puffs every 3-4 hours, potentially escalating to 6-10 puffs every 1-2 hours9
  • ICS-formoterol preferred: Early use prevents need for oral steroids and reduces severe exacerbations by 60-64%11,12
  • Systemic corticosteroids within one hour of emergency presentation significantly reduces hospitalization8

Severe Exacerbation/Status Asthmaticus (Inpatient)13

  • Standard therapy: β2-agonists and corticosteroids; refractory cases may require IV medications, continuous nebulizers, mechanical ventilation13
  • Correcting severe hypoxemia through oxygen application and repetitive short-acting beta2 agonists8
  • Pregnancy considerations: Aggressive management with SABA, oxygen, and early systemic corticosteroids; continue controller medications during labor9

Current GINA 2024 Recommendations11,12

  • All adults/adolescents should receive ICS-containing medication and not be treated with SABA alone12,9
  • Track 1 (preferred): as-needed combination low-dose ICS-formoterol; Track 2: SABA with separate ICS inhaler12
  • Low-dose budesonide/formoterol 80-160 mcg/4.5 mcg inhaled 1-2 times daily for symptoms1

Expected Course

+
  • Approximately 80-85% of asthma-related fatalities result from slow-onset exacerbations reflecting prolonged inadequate disease control13
  • Slow-onset exacerbations exhibit extensive airway inflammation and mucus plugging; sudden-onset involve predominant bronchospasm with clear airways13
  • Exacerbation rates 4-5 times higher in asthma-COPD overlap patients with more ED visits and hospitalizations4
  • Clinical context: Most patients with appropriate treatment show improvement within 1-2 hours; full recovery may take several days to weeks

Follow-up

+
  • Assess asthma severity, control, and exacerbation risk regularly during follow-up visits1
  • Every patient should receive written action plan explaining medication adjustment when symptoms worsen and when to seek medical help14
  • Measure lung function at diagnosis, 3-6 months after beginning ICS therapy, then periodically and more frequently in at-risk patients15
  • Patients should be sent home on oral prednisone after acute hospitalization8

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Diagnostic Alternatives15

  • Peak expiratory flow (PEF) emphasized as viable alternative to spirometry, especially in resource-limited environments11
  • Updated diagnostic flowchart allows flexibility in choosing initial lung function tests based on available resources14

Treatment Priorities8

  • Early institution of inhaled corticosteroids reduces exacerbations and decreases frequency of hospital admissions8
  • Clinical context: Focus on basic bronchodilators (albuterol) and systemic corticosteroids when advanced therapies unavailable

Cellulitis and Skin Soft Tissue Infections

Last verified: March 8, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Erythema with poorly demarcated borders1
  • Warmth over affected area1
  • Swelling and tenderness to palpation1
  • Pain at the affected site1
  • Fever and chills when systemic involvement2

Exam Findings

  • Poorly demarcated, expanding area of erythema1
  • Associated edema and tenderness1
  • Absence of purulent discharge or abscess1
  • Skin may appear pink to mildly erythematous (vs bright red in erysipelas)1
  • Evidence of portal of entry (trauma, insect bites, ulcers, surgical wounds)1
  • Clinical context: Lymphangitic streaking may be present in some cases
  • Clinical context: Regional lymphadenopathy may accompany infection

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Erysipelas - more superficial, bright red, well-demarcated borders, elevated1
  • Stasis dermatitis - bilateral, chronic, associated with venous insufficiency1
  • Venous insufficiency with inflammation1
  • Contact dermatitis - history of exposure, bilateral distribution pattern

Less Common

  • Deep vein thrombosis - unilateral swelling, may have erythema
  • Cutaneous abscess - purulent, fluctuant collection2
  • Septic bursitis - localized to bursal area with collection3

Mimics

  • MRSA skin infection - often misidentified as spider bite4
  • Necrotizing fasciitis - pain disproportionate to physical findings5
  • Periorbital cellulitis vs orbital cellulitis (around eyes)2
  • Chronic venous stasis dermatitis - bilateral, long-standing1

Diagnostic Tests

+

Laboratory Studies

  • Blood cultures - not routinely recommended for uncomplicated cellulitis6
  • Wound culture - consider in purulent infections or treatment failures6
  • Complete blood count - may show leukocytosis if systemic involvement2
  • C-reactive protein and ESR - may be elevated, useful for monitoring response3

Imaging Studies

  • Plain radiographs - limited value, may show soft tissue swelling5
  • CT scan - greater sensitivity than plain films for necrotizing infections5
  • MRI - most sensitive for deep infections and osteomyelitis3
  • Bedside ultrasound - can help identify abscesses and guide drainage3

Treatment

+

Outpatient - Mild Nonpurulent Cellulitis6

  • Penicillin VK 500 mg PO QID × 5 days
  • Cephalexin 500 mg PO QID × 5 days
  • Dicloxacillin 500 mg PO QID × 5 days
  • Clindamycin 300-400 mg PO TID × 5 days
  • Amoxicillin 500 mg PO TID for erysipelas1

Inpatient - Moderate Cellulitis with SIRS6

  • Ceftriaxone 1-2 g IV daily
  • Cefazolin 1-2 g IV every 8 hours
  • Clindamycin 600 mg IV every 8 hours
  • Penicillin G 2-4 million units IV every 4-6 hours

Severe/MRSA Risk6

  • Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV every 8-12 hours (target trough 15-20)
  • Linezolid 600 mg IV/PO every 12 hours
  • Daptomycin 4 mg/kg IV daily for skin infections
  • Ceftaroline 600 mg IV every 12 hours
  • MRSA coverage for: penetrating trauma, MRSA colonization, injection drug use, SIRS6

Purulent Infections6

  • Incision and drainage - primary therapy for abscesses
  • Trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole 1-2 DS tablets PO BID
  • Clindamycin 300-400 mg PO TID
  • Doxycycline 100 mg PO BID

Expected Course

+
  • 5-day treatment course recommended for uncomplicated cellulitis6
  • Clinical improvement expected within 48-72 hours of appropriate therapy
  • Resolution of erythema and swelling typically occurs over 7-10 days
  • Recurrence rate approximately 8-20% in patients with risk factors
  • Clinical context: Failure to respond within 48-72 hours should prompt reassessment

Follow-up

+
  • Clinical reassessment within 48-72 hours if outpatient treatment6
  • Address predisposing factors - diabetes, venous insufficiency, lymphedema6
  • Consider prophylactic antibiotics for patients with 3-4 episodes per year6
  • Prophylaxis options: penicillin or erythromycin twice daily × 4-52 weeks1
  • Benzathine penicillin G 1.2 million units IM every 2-4 weeks for prevention1

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Resource-Limited Settings

  • Penicillin remains first-line for streptococcal cellulitis1
  • Amoxicillin oral alternative when penicillin unavailable
  • Clinical assessment primary diagnostic tool when imaging unavailable
  • Local wound care and elevation important adjuncts

Prevention Strategies

  • Proper wound care and hygiene education
  • Management of predisposing conditions - diabetes, venous disease
  • Skin barrier protection and moisturization
  • Early recognition and treatment education for patients

AVERT — Apixaban for Cancer-Associated VTE Prophylaxis

NEJM • 2019 • Carrier M, et al.
Read on WikiJournalClub →

Clinical Question

In ambulatory patients with cancer who are initiating chemotherapy and who are at intermediate-to-high risk for venous thromboembolism, does apixaban reduce the risk of venous thromboembolism?

Study Design

Multicenter, double-blind, placebo-controlled, randomized trial conducted at 13 centers in Canada. Enrollment from 2014-2018 with mean follow-up of 183 days. Modified intention-to-treat analysis. No routine radiology screening performed.

Population

N=574 ambulatory patients with active malignancy. Inclusion criteria: age ≥18 years, newly diagnosed cancer or progression after remission, initiating chemotherapy with planned duration ≥3 months, Khorana score ≥2. Key exclusion criteria: increased bleeding risk, severe liver disease, certain cancer types (BCC, SCC, acute leukemia, MPN), life expectancy <6 months, CKD (GFR <30), platelet count <50,000. Mean age 61 years, 42% male.

Intervention

Apixaban 2.5 mg PO BID (n=291), with first dose administered within 24 hours of first chemotherapy dose.

Comparator / Control

Placebo (n=283)

Primary Endpoint

Objectively documented proximal DVT or PE at 180 days

Key Findings

VTE reduction: 4.2% vs 10.2% (HR 0.41; 95% CI 0.26-0.65; P<0.001; NNT=17). However, increased major bleeding: 3.5% vs 1.8% (HR 2.00; 95% CI 1.01-3.95; P=0.046; NNH=59). All-cause mortality: 12.2% vs 9.8% (HR 1.29; 95% CI 0.98-1.71). Major bleeding was predominantly associated with gastrointestinal or gynecologic cancers, with no fatal bleeding events.

Strengths

Double-blind, placebo-controlled design with objective VTE endpoints. Used validated Khorana risk score for patient selection. Demonstrated clear efficacy signal for VTE prevention. Good baseline characteristics balance between groups.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Low rate of renal insufficiency patients (creatinine clearance <50 ml/min). Did not include distal DVT in primary outcome, which may be treated with anticoagulation in cancer patients, potentially attenuating both VTE and bleeding outcomes. Bleeding events clustered in specific cancer types.

Practice Impact

This trial provided the first high-quality evidence supporting oral anticoagulation for primary VTE prevention in high-risk ambulatory cancer patients, representing a significant advance over parenteral options like low-molecular-weight heparin.

Why It Matters Now

AVERT established apixaban as an effective option for cancer-associated VTE prophylaxis, though the bleeding risk requires careful patient selection. The trial helped define which ambulatory cancer patients benefit most from thromboprophylaxis and contributed to evolving guidelines for cancer-associated thrombosis prevention.
