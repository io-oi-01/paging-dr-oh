Based on: PubMed abstract
Computed tomography angiography of the head and neck may inform emergency management in patients with suspected stroke
Understanding when CT angiography is most likely to change acute stroke management can improve resource utilization and reduce unnecessary imaging while ensuring appropriate care.
Design: Retrospective cohort study of 1,445 ED patients at an academic Level 1 trauma center who underwent CTAHN in 2023 for 5 common neurologic presentations • Outcome: CTAHN findings resulting in acute management change
Population: Emergency department patients with acute neurologic symptoms (stroke code, headache, dizziness, altered mental status, vision changes)
Intervention: CT angiography of head and neck (CTAHN)
Key Results
  • 216 of 1,445 CTAHN studies (14.9%) resulted in acute management change
  • Highest yield for stroke codes (21.2%) and vision changes (14.7%), lowest for dizziness (4.0%)
  • For stroke codes, predictors of management change included abnormal CT head (OR 2.84), focal neurologic deficit (OR 2.2), new vs recurring symptoms (OR 2.39), maximal symptom severity at onset (OR 3.95), and tobacco use (OR 2.02)
  • NIHSS scores of 10-14 had highest likelihood of CTAHN acute management change
  • For headache, abnormal CT head (OR 4.98) and thunderclap presentation (OR 5.31) predicted management change
Clinical Interpretation
  • These clinical predictors can help emergency physicians and neurologists make more informed decisions about when CTAHN is likely to be high-yield, potentially reducing unnecessary imaging while maintaining quality care
Limitations
  • Retrospective single-center design
  • Only univariate trends identified for some presentations due to small sample sizes
Bottom Line: CT angiography of head and neck has highest yield in stroke codes with abnormal CT, focal deficits, or new symptoms, and can be guided by specific clinical predictors.

HORIZONS-AMI — Bivalirudin in Primary PCI for STEMI

NEJM • 2008 • Stone et al. / HORIZONS-AMI Trial Group

Clinical Question

Does bivalirudin monotherapy improve net clinical outcomes compared to heparin plus glycoprotein IIb/IIIa inhibitors in patients with STEMI undergoing primary percutaneous coronary intervention?

Study Design

Multicenter, prospective, open-label, randomized controlled trial conducted at 123 centers in 11 countries. Patients were randomized 1:1 in an open-label fashion with independent clinical events committee adjudication of endpoints.

Population

3,602 patients presenting with STEMI within 12 hours of symptom onset undergoing primary PCI. Key exclusions included contraindications to anticoagulation, active bleeding, recent stroke, and planned use of warfarin. Mean age 60 years, 76% male.

Intervention

Bivalirudin monotherapy (0.75 mg/kg bolus, then 1.75 mg/kg/h during PCI, continued at 0.25 mg/kg/h for 4 hours post-procedure). Clopidogrel 600mg loading dose required.

Comparator / Control

Unfractionated heparin (60 U/kg bolus, target ACT 200-250 seconds) plus glycoprotein IIb/IIIa inhibitor (abciximab or double-bolus eptifibatide) per operator choice.

Primary Endpoint

Net adverse clinical events at 30 days (composite of major adverse cardiovascular events plus major bleeding not related to CABG).

Key Findings

Primary endpoint: bivalirudin 9.2% vs heparin+GPI 12.1% (ARR 2.9%, RRR 24%, RR 0.76, 95% CI 0.63-0.92, p=0.005, NNT=34). Major bleeding: 4.9% vs 8.3% (ARR 3.4%, RRR 41%, p<0.001, NNT=29). Acute stent thrombosis (≤24h): 1.3% vs 0.3% (p<0.001). 30-day mortality: 2.1% vs 3.1% (RR 0.66, 95% CI 0.44-1.00, p=0.047).

Strengths

Large, well-powered multicenter trial with rigorous endpoint adjudication. Pragmatic design reflecting real-world PCI practice. Clear demonstration of bleeding reduction with preserved ischemic efficacy. One-year mortality benefit sustained.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Open-label design introduced potential bias. Increased acute stent thrombosis raised safety concerns. Protocol mandated 4-hour post-PCI bivalirudin infusion may have contributed to bleeding reduction. Limited to STEMI population undergoing primary PCI.

Practice Impact

Established bivalirudin as preferred anticoagulant for primary PCI in STEMI patients, incorporated into AHA/ACC guidelines as Class I recommendation. Demonstrated importance of net clinical benefit (ischemic + bleeding outcomes) in antithrombotic trials.

Why It Matters Now

Remains foundational evidence for bivalirudin use in primary PCI, though recent trials (HEAT-PPCI, MATRIX) have challenged routine superiority over heparin alone. Current guidelines reflect more nuanced approach considering cost, bleeding risk, and institutional expertise with different anticoagulation strategies.

Hypoglycemia

Last verified: March 23, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Adrenergic symptoms: occur with glucose levels below 55 mg/dL — tachycardia, sweating, tremors1,10
  • Neuroglycopenic symptoms: occur with glucose levels below 48-50 mg/dL — mild mental status alteration to seizures and coma1,9,10
  • Whipple's triad (required for diagnosis): symptoms of hypoglycemia, plasma glucose <55 mg/dL, and resolution of symptoms after glucose correction1,2,9
  • Blunted adrenergic symptoms in individuals with frequent hypoglycemia episodes9
  • Focal neurological deficits that resolve with glucose correction may occur9

Exam Findings

  • Diaphoresis, hypotension, and bradycardia in patients with hypoglycemia10
  • Altered mental status ranging from confusion to coma2,10
  • Signs of sympathetic activation: tachycardia, hypertension (early), sweating10
  • Neurologic deficits may be present and reversible with glucose administration9

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Insulin or insulin secretagogue overdose in diabetes patients1,9
  • Medication-induced: sulfonylureas, meglitinides in diabetic patients9
  • Skipped meals or delayed eating in patients on glucose-lowering medications1
  • Increased physical activity without adequate carbohydrate intake1

Less Common

  • Insulinoma or other islet cell tumors1
  • Non-islet cell tumors producing IGF-21
  • Adrenal insufficiency or growth hormone deficiency1,9
  • Liver disease or kidney failure9
  • Postprandial hypoglycemia (reactive hypoglycemia)1
  • Post-bariatric surgery hypoglycemia6

Mimics

  • Anxiety or panic attacks with sympathetic symptoms1
  • Seizure disorders without hypoglycemia1
  • Factitious hypoglycemia from self-administered insulin or secretagogues4
  • Sepsis or critical illness with altered mental status9

Diagnostic Tests

+

Point-of-Care Testing

  • Capillary blood glucose: less reliable at extremely low levels, confirmation with laboratory glucose recommended16
  • Continuous glucose monitoring: should not be used for diagnosis due to poor accuracy at low glucose levels9

Laboratory Tests During Hypoglycemic Episode

  • Plasma glucose: definitive test, <55 mg/dL confirms hypoglycemia1,2
  • Insulin level: elevated in insulinoma or factitious hypoglycemia4,9
  • C-peptide: low with exogenous insulin, high with insulinoma or sulfonylurea use4,9
  • Beta-hydroxybutyrate: should be present during hypoglycemia; absence suggests hyperinsulinism9,16
  • Sulfonylurea/meglitinide screen: positive in factitious ingestion4,9

Provocative Tests

  • Glucagon stimulation test: 1 mg IV, rise in glucose >25 mg/dL suggests hyperinsulinemic hypoglycemia9
  • 72-hour fast: for suspected insulinoma in healthy-appearing patients1,9

Treatment

+

Conscious Patients (Outpatient)19,15

  • 15-15 rule: 15 grams fast-acting carbohydrates, recheck glucose in 15 minutes15,19
  • Fast-acting carbohydrates: glucose tablets, regular soda, fruit juice (avoid high fat/protein foods initially)19,27
  • Repeat treatment if glucose still <70 mg/dL after 15 minutes15,19
  • Follow-up snack: balanced meal with protein and carbohydrates after glucose normalizes15

Severe Hypoglycemia/Altered Mental Status7,11

  • Glucagon: 1 mg IM/SC or intranasal formulations (preferred for ease of use)7,11,29
  • Dextrose 50%: 50 mL IV (25g) in adults18
  • Dextrose 25%: 1-2 mL/kg IV in children, or dextrose 10%: 2.5-5 mL/kg IV16
  • Emergency medical services should be called for unresponsive patients15,19

Hospital Management11,25

  • IV dextrose bolus: then continuous infusion if persistent hypoglycemia16,18
  • Continuous dextrose infusion: 6-8 mg/kg/min for refractory hypoglycemia16
  • Monitor glucose every 15-20 minutes initially, then hourly16
  • Identify and treat underlying cause: medication adjustment, hormone replacement1,9

Expected Course

+
  • Conscious patients: symptoms typically resolve within 15 minutes of appropriate treatment15
  • Severe hypoglycemia: patients usually wake up within 15 minutes after glucagon injection15
  • Recurrent episodes require urgent medical evaluation and treatment plan modification13,19
  • Long-term outcomes: depend on underlying cause and frequency of episodes13

Follow-up

+
  • Review diabetes medications and adjust doses to prevent recurrence11,19
  • Diabetes education on hypoglycemia recognition and self-management13,19
  • Consider continuous glucose monitoring for patients with frequent episodes11,13
  • Glucagon prescription for high-risk patients (impaired awareness, history of severe episodes)11,19
  • Evaluate for underlying causes in non-diabetic patients1,9

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Emergency Treatment

  • Oral sugar solutions: honey, table sugar dissolved in water if commercial products unavailable15
  • Basic glucose monitoring: finger-stick glucose meters widely available16
  • Simple carbohydrates: crackers, bread, fruit if glucose tablets unavailable15

Prevention

  • Regular meal timing and patient education on medication-food coordination1
  • Family education on recognizing symptoms and basic emergency response15
  • Medication timing adjustments when meals are delayed or missed1
