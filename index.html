Invasive management for non-ST-segment elevation myocardial infarction in frail patients may not improve mortality
As the population ages, determining optimal treatment strategies for very elderly patients with NSTEMI becomes increasingly important for clinical decision-making.
Design: Retrospective observational cohort study with propensity score-adjusted analysis • Outcome: All-cause mortality at 45 days, 6 months, and long-term follow-up
Population: 967 consecutive patients aged ≥60 years admitted to intensive coronary care unit with NSTEMI between July 2019 and June 2024, stratified into 60-84 years and ≥85 years groups
Intervention: Invasive management including percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) and coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG)
Comparator: Conservative management without revascularization
Key Results
  • Patients ≥85 years had significantly higher mortality at all time points: 45 days (14.6% vs 5.9%, p<0.001), 6 months (19.7% vs 8.5%, p<0.001), and beyond 6 months (42.3% vs 17.9%, p<0.001)
  • Invasive management was less common in older patients (63.5% vs 75.1%, p<0.05)
  • In multivariable analysis, revascularization was independently associated with lower mortality (HR 0.565; 95% CI 0.38-0.84; p=0.002)
  • Propensity score-adjusted analyses showed lower mortality among patients undergoing revascularization, particularly in the 60-84 year group, with similar directional trends in patients ≥85 years
Clinical Interpretation
  • Contrary to the 2MM summary, this study actually suggests invasive management may benefit carefully selected very elderly NSTEMI patients
  • The findings support individualized treatment decisions rather than age-based exclusions
  • However, the absolute mortality remains high in the ≥85 age group regardless of treatment approach
Limitations
  • Retrospective observational design limits causal inference
  • Single-center study may limit generalizability
  • Selection bias inherent in who receives invasive vs conservative management despite propensity matching
Bottom Line: Invasive management appears beneficial for carefully selected patients ≥85 years with NSTEMI, supporting individualized rather than age-based treatment decisions.

DAPT Study — Duration of Dual Antiplatelet Therapy After PCI

NEJM • 2014 • Mauri L, et al.

Clinical Question

Among patients who have completed one year of dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT) after drug-eluting stent PCI, does continued DAPT reduce stent thrombosis and death, MI, or stroke without increasing bleeding?

Study Design

Multicenter, randomized, placebo-controlled trial conducted at 452 sites in North America, Europe, Australia, and New Zealand. Enrollment occurred from 2009-2011 with 30 months follow-up post-PCI (18 months after randomization). Analysis was intention-to-treat.

Population

9,961 compliant patients who tolerated 12 months of DAPT following drug-eluting stent PCI. Inclusion required age >18 years and undergoing PCI with stent (or had PCI with stent in prior 3 days) with no contraindications for DAPT. Key exclusion was prior bleeding on DAPT during the initial 12-month period.

Intervention

Continued thienopyridine therapy for an additional 18 months (totaling 30 months of DAPT) in 5,020 patients.

Comparator / Control

Placebo (aspirin monotherapy) in 4,941 patients after completing 12 months of DAPT.

Primary Endpoint

Three co-primary endpoints: stent thrombosis, composite of all-cause mortality/MI/stroke, and bleeding.

Key Findings

Continued DAPT reduced stent thrombosis (0.4% vs 1.4%; NNT 100) and the composite endpoint of death/MI/stroke (4.3% vs 5.9%; NNT 62), driven by MI reduction (2.1% vs 4.1%; NNT 50). However, bleeding increased (2.5% vs 1.6%; NNH 111). Unexpectedly, all-cause mortality increased (2.0% vs 1.5%; P=0.05), driven by non-cardiovascular deaths, particularly cancer-related deaths (0.62% vs 0.28%; P=0.02).

Strengths

Large sample size, robust multicenter design, placebo-controlled methodology, and enrolled only patients who had successfully completed 12 months of DAPT without bleeding complications, reducing confounding from early intolerance.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Study population excluded patients with prior bleeding on DAPT, likely underestimating true bleeding risk in all-comers. The unexpected increase in all-cause mortality, particularly cancer deaths, raised safety concerns requiring further investigation. Optimal patient selection criteria for extended DAPT remained unclear.

Practice Impact

Established that extended DAPT reduces ischemic events but increases bleeding risk. Led to development of risk stratification approaches and influenced 2016 ACC/AHA guidelines that recommend considering continuation beyond standard duration in selected patients with good DAPT tolerance, low bleeding risk, and high thrombotic risk.

Why It Matters Now

Remains foundational for DAPT duration decisions after PCI. The risk-benefit trade-offs identified in this trial prompted development of clinical decision tools and risk scores to individualize DAPT duration. The findings were corroborated by PEGASUS-TIMI 54, and current practice emphasizes personalized risk assessment rather than uniform extended therapy.

Hemophagocytic Lymphohistiocytosis

Last verified: May 12, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Fever: Prolonged fever ≥38.3°C (101°F) as part of systemic hyperinflammatory syndrome1
  • Constitutional symptoms: Malaise, weight loss, lethargy from systemic inflammation1
  • Respiratory distress: May occur due to pulmonary involvement and systemic inflammation1
  • Neurological symptoms: Including altered mental status, seizures, focal deficits from CNS involvement1
  • Cutaneous manifestations: Rash may be present in some patients2

Exam Findings

  • Hepatosplenomegaly: Organomegaly as hallmark finding from tissue infiltration and multiorgan involvement1
  • Lymphadenopathy: Less common than hepatosplenomegaly but may be present3
  • Pallor and petechiae: From cytopenias affecting all cell lines1
  • Jaundice: Bilirubin elevation common, fulminant liver failure can occur4
  • Mental status changes: Neurologic abnormalities including altered consciousness3

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Sepsis/Severe infection: Similar but not the same - requires differentiation from HLH4
  • Hematologic malignancies: Lymphoma, leukemia - most commonly sepsis or hematologic malignancy in adults1
  • Autoimmune disorders: SLE, rheumatoid arthritis, inflammatory bowel disease as triggers1
  • Macrophage activation syndrome (MAS): MAS refers to HLH caused by rheumatologic disease - subset of HLH4

Less Common

  • Primary immunodeficiencies: Chronic granulomatous disease, SCID - lack bleeding diathesis and hypopigmentation5
  • Drug-induced hyperinflammation: Post-chemotherapy, immunosuppressive agents6
  • Viral infections: EBV, HIV, CMV, influenza as common triggers7

Mimics

  • Adult Still's disease: Similar fever pattern and ferritin elevation8
  • Cytokine release syndrome: HLH reported in ~1% of CAR-T cell patients9
  • Kikuchi-Fujimoto disease: Can rarely trigger HLH with 20-42% mortality10

Diagnostic Tests

+

HLH-2004 Diagnostic Criteria (5 of 8 required)11

  • Fever: ≥38.3°C (101°F)11
  • Splenomegaly: Clinical or radiologic evidence11
  • Cytopenias (2+ cell lines): Hemoglobin <9 g/dL, platelets <100×10³/μL, neutrophils <1×10³/μL11
  • Hypertriglyceridemia and/or hypofibrinogenemia: Triglycerides ≥265 mg/dL, fibrinogen ≤150 mg/dL11
  • Hemophagocytosis: Observed in bone marrow in only 25% of HLH patients - not necessary for diagnosis12
  • Low/absent NK cell activity: By flow cytometry if available11
  • Hyperferritinemia: Ferritin >500 ng/mL (optimal cutoff ~3951 ng/mL, ferritin >10,000 highly suspicious with 96% specificity)13,14
  • Elevated soluble IL-2 receptor (sCD25): ≥2400 U/mL11

Laboratory Studies1

  • Complete blood count: Cytopenias affecting multiple cell lines1
  • Comprehensive metabolic panel: Elevated ALT in ~85% of patients, bilirubin elevation common4
  • Coagulation studies: Hyperfibrinolysis with low fibrinogen from activated macrophages4
  • Inflammatory markers: CRP universally elevated and correlates with disease severity4

Specialized Testing

  • Bone marrow biopsy: Hemophagocytosis hallmark but not sensitive - patients can have HLH without tissue evidence4
  • Genetic testing: Biallelic pathogenic variants in PRF1, STX11, STXBP2, UNC13D for familial HLH3
  • HScore calculator: Risk stratification model published 2014 - scoring 0-337 with higher scores indicating increased HLH probability12

Treatment

+

First-line Treatment (HLH-2004 Protocol)7,15

  • Dexamethasone: 10 mg/m² daily weeks 1-2, then 5 mg/m² weeks 3-4, 2.5 mg/m² weeks 5-6, 1.25 mg/m² week 7, taper week 815
  • Etoposide: 150 mg/m² twice weekly weeks 1-2, then once weekly (or 5 mg/kg per dose if <10 kg)15,16
  • Cyclosporine A: Targeting trough levels 200 μg/L15,17
  • Intrathecal therapy: Methotrexate + hydrocortisone for CNS involvement - dosed by age16

Adult-specific Modifications7

  • Moderate disease: Prednisolone 1-2 mg/kg or dexamethasone 5-10 mg/m² + IVIG 1.6 g/kg over 2-3 days7
  • Post-chemotherapy HLH: Corticosteroids + IVIG; use etoposide sparingly for bone marrow recovery7
  • Risk-adapted approach: Heterogeneity requires individualized treatment based on triggers, severity, comorbidities7,18

Supportive Care

  • Infection prophylaxis: Co-trimoxazole 480 mg PO 3x/week for 3 months post-treatment19
  • Antifungal prophylaxis: Per local high-risk protocols19
  • IVIG supplementation: Consider during therapy with prior approval from immunoglobulin panel19
  • Blood product support: For cytopenias as clinically indicated1

Definitive Treatment

  • Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation: Only cure for familial HLH - indicated for genetic disease, relapsing, or severe/persistent disease5,15
  • Timing considerations: Best outcomes when performed before accelerated phase - rule out accelerated phase first5

Expected Course

+
  • Without treatment: Life-threatening with median survival ~2 months in familial HLH1,16
  • With treatment: 5-year survival 61% in HLH-2004 study (55% in HLH-94)15,16
  • Post-transplant: 5-year survival 66% overall, 70% for verified familial HLH15
  • Prognostic factors: Higher ferritin values and failure to improve suggest worse outcomes4
  • Age differences: Primary disease presents in childhood, secondary disease typically in adults1

Follow-up

+
  • Response monitoring: Serial ferritin levels - in untreated HLH often increasing rapidly4
  • Organ function assessment: Weekly monitoring of hepatic, renal, hematologic parameters1
  • CNS surveillance: Continue intrathecal therapy until 1 week after resolution of CNS involvement16
  • Trigger identification: Always search for underlying causes - genetic, infection, malignancy, autoimmune20,16
  • Transplant evaluation: Early donor search for familial disease or severe/persistent cases17,15

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Clinical Diagnosis

  • Focus on clinical criteria: fever, splenomegaly, cytopenias when specialized tests unavailable1
  • Basic laboratory tests: CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel, coagulation studies4
  • Routine ferritin checking in critically ill patients given degree of elevation12

Treatment Modifications

  • Corticosteroids as first-line when etoposide unavailable or contraindicated7
  • IVIG may provide benefit in infection-associated cases7
  • Aggressive treatment of underlying infections and triggers16

Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation

Last verified: May 12, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Bleeding: hemorrhage may be the presenting symptom due to consumption of clotting factors and platelets1
  • Thrombotic symptoms: dyspnea, neurologic changes, acute organ dysfunction from microvascular thrombosis1
  • Purpura fulminans: fever, hemorrhage from multiple sites, hypotension in acute infectious form2
  • Obstetric manifestations: sudden dyspnea, dysphoria, hypotension, cardiovascular collapse during peripartum period3

Exam Findings

  • Petechial rash: may rapidly progress to larger ecchymotic areas and hemorrhagic bullae2
  • Skin necrosis: hard eschars characteristic of purpura fulminans2
  • Multiple organ dysfunction: compromised blood flow to organs from micro- and macrovascular clotting1
  • Acute bleeding: from mucosal surfaces, surgical sites, or other locations1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Liver failure: may have similar coagulopathy but different underlying pathophysiology1
  • Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura (TTP): microangiopathic hemolytic anemia with thrombocytopenia4
  • Hemolytic uremic syndrome: similar presentation with thrombocytopenia and organ dysfunction4

Less Common

  • Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia: thrombocytopenia with thrombotic tendency4
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome: can cause thrombotic complications but different mechanism4

Mimics

  • Acute leukemia: may present with bleeding and abnormal coagulation studies4
  • Vitamin K deficiency: prolonged PT but different fibrinogen and D-dimer patterns4

Diagnostic Tests

+

ISTH DIC Score (2025 Updated)5

  • Platelet count: >100×10⁹/L (0 points), 50-100×10⁹/L (1 point), <50×10⁹/L (2 points)5
  • D-dimer/FDP: not elevated (0 points), moderately elevated (2 points), strongly elevated (3 points)5
  • PT prolongation: <3 sec (0 points), 3-6 sec (1 point), >6 sec (2 points)5
  • Fibrinogen: >1 g/L (0 points), <1 g/L (1 point)5
  • Score interpretation: ≥5 compatible with overt DIC, <5 suggests non-overt DIC5

Laboratory Tests

  • D-dimer: elevated in both acute and chronic DIC, high sensitivity but low specificity6
  • PT/PTT: elevated in acute DIC due to consumption of coagulation factors6
  • Fibrinogen: may be low or normal depending on production vs consumption balance6
  • Antithrombin III, Protein C/S: may help confirm purpura fulminans diagnosis2

Treatment

+

Primary Management7

  • Treat underlying condition: cornerstone of DIC management - sepsis, trauma, malignancy, obstetric complications7
  • Supportive care: maintain hemodynamic stability, organ support as needed1

Bleeding DIC Management7

  • Platelets: transfuse if count <50×10⁹/L in bleeding patients or those at high bleeding risk7
  • Fresh frozen plasma: for bleeding patients with prolonged PT/PTT, not based on lab values alone7
  • Fibrinogen concentrate/cryoprecipitate: for severe hypofibrinogenemia <1 g/L despite FFP7

Thrombotic DIC Management7

  • Therapeutic heparin: for arterial/venous thromboembolism, severe purpura fulminans with ischemia7
  • UFH continuous infusion: 10 μg/kg/h, monitor clinically for bleeding rather than APTT targets7
  • VTE prophylaxis: prophylactic LMWH or heparin in critically ill, non-bleeding patients7

Specific Therapies7

  • Heparin in consumptive coagulopathy: indicated for managing acute and chronic DIC8
  • Antifibrinolytics contraindicated: generally avoid unless primary hyperfibrinolytic DIC with severe bleeding7

Expected Course

+
  • Acute DIC: rapid consumption of clotting factors leading to severe bleeding and organ dysfunction6
  • Chronic DIC: production typically keeps pace with consumption, more prone to thrombotic events6
  • Mortality correlation: ISTH DIC score strongly predicts mortality, particularly in sepsis9
  • Sepsis-associated DIC: mortality rates 20-50%, most common cause (~47% incidence)10

Follow-up

+
  • Daily monitoring: repeat DIC scoring daily to characterize severity and course5
  • Laboratory trending: monitor platelet count, coagulation studies, fibrinogen, D-dimer7
  • Clinical assessment: watch for new bleeding, thrombotic events, organ dysfunction7
  • Underlying condition response: monitor treatment response of precipitating cause7

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Essential Tests

  • Basic coagulation panel: PT, PTT, platelet count - should be available daily in most hospitals5
  • Peripheral blood smear: Leishman stain to assess for schistocytes and platelet morphology11
  • Paracoagulation test: protamine sulfate test for fibrin degradation products when D-dimer unavailable11

Modified Scoring

  • Clinical context: Modified scoring systems have been evaluated when certain assays unavailable, but original ISTH system remains international standard5
