Smallpox therapy tecovirimat not efficacious in treating clade II mpox
Tecovirimat was approved for smallpox under FDA Animal Rule based on animal models, but human efficacy data for mpox was lacking until this trial.
Design: Phase 3, international, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial • Outcome: Clinical resolution assessed in time-to-event analysis in participants with active skin or mucosal lesions
Population: Adults with laboratory-confirmed clade II mpox (344 participants with confirmed infection, 336 with active skin or mucosal lesions in primary analysis)
Intervention: Oral tecovirimat for 14 days
Comparator: Placebo for 14 days
Key Results
  • By day 29, estimated cumulative incidence of clinical resolution was 83% with tecovirimat vs 84% with placebo
  • Competing-risks hazard ratio for clinical resolution was 0.98 (95% CI 0.74-1.31, P=0.89)
  • No substantial difference in pain reduction among participants with severe pain (difference 0.1 point, 95% CI -0.8 to 1.0)
  • No difference in complete lesion healing (HR 0.97, 95% CI 0.75-1.26) or viral DNA clearance
  • Similar adverse events grade 3+ (4% tecovirimat vs 3% placebo)
Clinical Interpretation
  • This well-designed trial definitively shows tecovirimat provides no benefit for clade II mpox in humans despite animal model efficacy
  • The high resolution rates in both groups suggest most participants had self-limiting disease
  • Questions the utility of tecovirimat for routine mpox treatment and highlights limitations of animal rule approvals
Limitations
  • Limited to clade II mpox, results may not apply to other clades
  • Most participants likely had mild disease given high resolution rates in both groups
Bottom Line: Tecovirimat shows no clinical benefit for clade II mpox treatment compared to placebo in this definitive phase 3 trial.
High confidence 📊 Practice-changing

  • Retrospective single-center design
  • Only univariate trends identified for some presentations due to small sample sizes
Bottom Line: CT angiography of head and neck has highest yield in stroke codes with abnormal CT, focal deficits, or new symptoms, and can be guided by specific clinical predictors.

OSCAR — High-Frequency Oscillatory Ventilation in Adults

NEJM • 2013 • Young et al.

Clinical Question

Does high-frequency oscillatory ventilation (HFOV) reduce mortality compared to conventional ventilation in adults with moderate-to-severe ARDS?

Study Design

Multicenter, randomized, controlled, unblinded trial conducted across 29 ICUs in the UK. Patients were randomized 1:1 to HFOV vs conventional ventilation within 48 hours of meeting ARDS criteria. Primary analysis was intention-to-treat.

Population

Adults with moderate-to-severe ARDS (PaO2/FiO2 ≤200 mmHg) requiring mechanical ventilation. Key exclusions: pregnancy, moribund patients, contraindications to HFOV. N=795 patients randomized (398 HFOV, 397 conventional ventilation).

Intervention

High-frequency oscillatory ventilation using standardized protocol with mean airway pressure set 5-8 cmH2O above conventional ventilation, frequency 10 Hz initially, amplitude targeting pH >7.25 and adequate chest wall vibration.

Comparator / Control

Conventional mechanical ventilation using lung-protective strategy with tidal volumes 6-8 mL/kg predicted body weight, plateau pressure ≤30 cmH2O, and PEEP per FiO2 table.

Primary Endpoint

All-cause mortality at hospital discharge censored at 180 days.

Key Findings

HFOV showed higher mortality: 166/398 (41.7%) vs 163/397 (41.1%) in conventional ventilation group. ARR: -0.6% (favoring conventional ventilation). RR: 1.01 (95% CI: 0.87-1.19), p=0.85. No significant difference in 30-day mortality: 142/398 (35.7%) vs 131/397 (33.0%), RR: 1.08 (95% CI: 0.90-1.30). HFOV patients had more treatment failures requiring rescue therapies.

Strengths

Large multicenter RCT with adequate power, appropriate lung-protective conventional ventilation comparator, standardized HFOV protocol, intention-to-treat analysis, and robust primary endpoint of mortality.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Unblinded design, higher than expected mortality in both groups, some crossover between groups, potential for learning curve effects with HFOV, and inclusion of patients with varying ARDS severity.

Practice Impact

Along with the concurrent OSCILLATE trial, OSCAR definitively established that HFOV does not improve outcomes in adult ARDS and may cause harm. Led to abandonment of HFOV as rescue therapy in most adult ARDS protocols.

Why It Matters Now

Represents important negative trial that prevented widespread adoption of potentially harmful therapy. Reinforced importance of lung-protective conventional ventilation as standard of care. Highlights how promising physiologic rationale doesn't always translate to clinical benefit in complex critical care interventions.

  • Abdominal distension: Progressive distension as bowel accumulates gas and fluid proximal to obstruction1
  • Obstipation: Cessation of flatus and stool passage in complete obstruction1
  • Nausea: Often accompanied by loss of appetite2

Exam Findings

  • Abdominal distension: Visible distension with tympany to percussion3
  • High-pitched bowel sounds: Classic hyperactive, high-pitched sounds initially, may become absent later3
  • Tenderness: Diffuse tenderness; peritoneal signs suggest strangulation or perforation4
  • Signs of complications: Fever, tachycardia, leukocytosis, metabolic acidosis suggest bowel compromise4
  • Hernia evaluation: Examine for external hernias as potential cause4

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Adhesive small bowel obstruction: Most common cause (60-75% of cases), especially after previous abdominal surgery1,5
  • Hernias: Inguinal, umbilical, incisional hernias with incarceration1,5
  • Malignancy: Third most common cause; primary or metastatic tumors (5-20% of cases)1

Less Common

  • Volvulus: Small bowel twisting causing closed-loop obstruction1
  • Gallstone ileus: Gallstone impaction at ileocecal valve; Rigler triad on imaging1
  • Endometriosis: Particularly in women of reproductive age1
  • Inflammatory bowel disease: Crohn disease causing benign strictures5

Mimics

  • Paralytic ileus: Functional obstruction; absent bowel sounds vs hyperactive in early SBO6
  • Large bowel obstruction: Pain, distention, minimal flatus, but different imaging pattern7
  • Pseudo-obstruction: Bowel dilation without mechanical obstruction8
  • Superior mesenteric artery syndrome: Rare proximal SBO with epigastric pain, nausea, vomiting9

Diagnostic Tests

+

Imaging Studies

  • CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast: Gold standard; identifies transition point, ischemia, perforation1,2
  • Plain abdominal radiographs: Sn 70-86% for high-grade obstruction, less sensitive for partial obstruction10
  • CT for ischemia/strangulation: Sn 85-100% for detecting ischemia confirmed at surgery10
  • Ultrasound: Alternative to CT but with logistical limitations4

Laboratory Studies

  • Complete blood count: Evaluate for leukocytosis suggesting complications3,4
  • Comprehensive metabolic panel: Assess electrolyte imbalances, renal function3,4
  • Serum lactate: Elevated levels may indicate bowel ischemia3,8
  • Arterial blood gas: Clinical context: Evaluate for metabolic acidosis in suspected strangulation

Treatment

+

Initial Management (All Settings)1,2

  • NPO and nasogastric decompression: Immediate bowel rest with NG tube placement
  • IV fluid resuscitation: Crystalloids to correct dehydration and electrolyte abnormalities
  • Electrolyte correction: Address hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis
  • Urinary catheter: Clinical context: Monitor urine output during resuscitation

Non-operative Management (Stable Patients)4,11

  • Water-soluble contrast (WSC): Via NG tube; reduces hospital stay by ~2 days
  • Conservative management duration: 80% of adhesive SBO resolve; 60-85% without surgery
  • Serial monitoring: Watch for signs of deterioration requiring surgery

Surgical Management (Inpatient/ICU)1,4

  • Immediate surgery indications: Strangulation, ischemia, perforation, peritonitis
  • Failed conservative management: Surgery after unsuccessful non-operative trial
  • Laparoscopic approach: When technically feasible; may reduce morbidity vs open surgery
  • Adhesiolysis: Surgical division of fibrous bands causing obstruction

Expected Course

+
  • Non-operative resolution: ~80% of adhesive SBO resolve with conservative management4,11
  • Recurrence risk: Up to 25% with chronic pain experience recurrent episodes4
  • Complications: Bowel ischemia, necrosis, perforation, peritonitis in severe cases1
  • Mortality factors: Clinical context: Higher in elderly, delayed diagnosis, bowel compromise

Follow-up

+
  • Serial imaging: Follow-up abdominal radiographs to confirm resolution12
  • Return of bowel function: Clinical context: Passage of flatus and stool indicates resolution
  • Gradual diet advancement: Clinical context: Clear liquids → full liquids → regular diet as tolerated
  • Long-term monitoring: Watch for chronic abdominal pain and recurrent episodes4

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Diagnostic Approaches

  • Plain radiographs: Sn 70-86%; remains important due to widespread availability and low cost10
  • Ultrasound: Alternative to CT when available; operator-dependent4
  • Clinical assessment: Careful history and physical examination remain essential4

Treatment Modifications

  • Conservative management: Bowel rest, fluid repletion, NG decompression as initial treatment11
  • Oral contrast alternative: Clinical context: Water-soluble contrast by mouth if NG tube unavailable
  • Early surgical consultation: Clinical context: Lower threshold for surgery when advanced imaging unavailable
