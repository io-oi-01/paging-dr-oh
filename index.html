Sepsis and Septic Shock

Last verified: March 6, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Fever (>38.3°C or 101°F) or hypothermia (<36°C or 96.8°F)1
  • Altered mental status including confusion, delirium, or decreased level of consciousness1
  • Chills and rigors associated with fever2
  • Dyspnea or shortness of breath3
  • Malaise, weakness, or fatigue that may be subtle in elderly or immunocompromised patients4
  • Nausea and vomiting2
  • Decreased urination or oliguria3
  • Source-specific symptoms such as cough (pneumonia), urinary frequency (UTI), or abdominal pain (intra-abdominal infection)5

Exam Findings

  • Tachycardia >90 bpm as part of systemic inflammatory response1
  • Tachypnea ≥22 breaths/min (qSOFA criterion)6
  • Hypotension: SBP ≤100 mmHg (qSOFA criterion)6
  • Altered mentation: Glasgow Coma Scale <15 (qSOFA criterion)6
  • Signs of hypoperfusion: delayed capillary refill >2 seconds, mottled skin, cool extremities7
  • Skin changes: rash, petechiae, or purpura may indicate specific pathogens8
  • Evidence of organ dysfunction: jaundice, decreased urine output, focal neurologic deficits9
  • Source-specific findings: lung consolidation, abdominal tenderness, meningeal signs5

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Non-infectious SIRS: burns, trauma, pancreatitis, drug reactions10
  • Cardiogenic shock: acute MI, heart failure exacerbation11
  • Hypovolemic shock: hemorrhage, dehydration, third-spacing11
  • Anaphylactic shock: drug reactions, food allergies12

Less Common

  • Adrenal insufficiency: particularly in critically ill patients13
  • Thyrotoxicosis: thyroid storm with systemic effects14
  • Malignant hyperthermia: anesthesia-related hyperthermia15
  • Drug intoxication: sympathomimetics, anticholinergics16

Mimics

  • Heat stroke: hyperthermia with CNS dysfunction17
  • Neuroleptic malignant syndrome: antipsychotic-induced hyperthermia18
  • Serotonin syndrome: excessive serotonergic activity19
  • Systemic vasculitis: autoimmune inflammatory conditions20

Diagnostic Tests

+

Laboratory Studies

  • Lactate level: >2 mmol/L (>18 mg/dL) indicates septic shock when combined with hypotension and vasopressor requirement1
  • Blood cultures: obtain 2 sets (aerobic/anaerobic) before antibiotics; positive in 30-40% of cases2
  • Procalcitonin: >0.25 ng/mL suggests bacterial infection; Sn 77%, Sp 79% for sepsis3
  • White blood cell count: may be elevated, normal, or decreased; left shift with bandemia4
  • C-reactive protein: non-specific inflammatory marker; elevated >10 mg/L in most cases5
  • Comprehensive metabolic panel: assess for organ dysfunction, acidosis, electrolyte abnormalities6

Scoring Systems

  • qSOFA score: Sn 66%, Sp 61%, +LR 1.7, -LR 0.6 for mortality prediction; poor sensitivity for sepsis detection7
  • SOFA score: ≥2-point increase indicates organ dysfunction; AUROC 0.75-0.89 for mortality prediction8
  • CURB-65: useful for pneumonia-associated sepsis risk stratification9

Source Identification

  • Chest X-ray: evaluate for pneumonia, ARDS, pleural effusion10
  • Urinalysis and culture: assess for urinary tract infection11
  • CT imaging: abdomen/pelvis for intra-abdominal source; head if altered mental status12
  • Echocardiography: evaluate for endocarditis if bacteremia or new murmur13

Treatment

+

Emergency Department/Initial Management1

  • Crystalloid resuscitation: 30 mL/kg IV within 3 hours for hypotension or lactate ≥4 mmol/L2
  • Balanced crystalloids preferred: Lactated Ringer's or Plasma-Lyte over normal saline3
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics: within 1 hour of sepsis recognition after cultures obtained4
  • Vasopressors: norepinephrine first-line if hypotension persists after fluid resuscitation5
  • Source control: drainage, debridement, or device removal within 6-12 hours when indicated6

Inpatient Non-ICU Management7

  • Continued antibiotic therapy: narrow spectrum based on culture results and clinical response8
  • Fluid management: conservative approach after initial resuscitation; monitor for fluid overload9
  • DVT prophylaxis: pharmacologic prophylaxis unless contraindicated10
  • Stress ulcer prophylaxis: PPI therapy for patients with risk factors11
  • Glycemic control: target glucose <180 mg/dL12

ICU Management13

  • Norepinephrine: first-line vasopressor, target MAP ≥65 mmHg14
  • Hydrocortisone: 200 mg/day IV (50 mg q6h or continuous infusion) for refractory shock on vasopressors ≥0.25 mcg/kg/min for ≥4 hours15
  • Mechanical ventilation: lung-protective strategy if ARDS develops16
  • ECMO consideration: for severe ARDS in experienced centers17
  • Hemodynamic monitoring: consider arterial line, central venous access as needed18

Expected Course

+
  • Early phase (0-6 hours): rapid clinical deterioration possible; prompt recognition and treatment critical1
  • Acute phase (6-72 hours): organ dysfunction may worsen initially despite appropriate treatment2
  • Recovery phase (3-7 days): gradual improvement in organ function and hemodynamics with effective therapy3
  • Overall mortality: sepsis 15-30%, septic shock 40-50% depending on severity and comorbidities4
  • ICU length of stay: typically 5-14 days for survivors5
  • Long-term effects: post-intensive care syndrome including physical, cognitive, and psychological impairments6

Follow-up

+
  • Post-discharge follow-up: within 1-2 weeks to assess recovery and address complications1
  • Antibiotic duration: typically 7-10 days for most infections; longer for endocarditis, osteomyelitis2
  • Functional assessment: evaluate for post-ICU weakness, cognitive impairment, PTSD3
  • Rehabilitation referral: consider for patients with prolonged ICU stay >72 hours or mechanical ventilation >48 hours4
  • Family support: peer support groups and counseling resources5
  • Monitoring for recurrence: immunocompromised patients at higher risk6

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Diagnostic Adaptations

  • Clinical assessment priority: focus on qSOFA criteria when laboratory tests limited1
  • Point-of-care lactate: if available, more cost-effective than serial laboratory draws2
  • Basic blood tests: complete blood count and basic metabolic panel if lactate unavailable3

Treatment Modifications

  • Oral antibiotics: consider high-bioavailability oral agents if IV access limited4
  • Crystalloid choice: normal saline acceptable if balanced crystalloids unavailable5
  • Simplified protocols: focus on early recognition, antibiotics, and fluid resuscitation6
  • Community health worker training: for early recognition and referral7
