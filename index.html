Pneumothorax

Last verified: March 11, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Acute onset unilateral chest pain — sharp, pleuritic nature1
  • Dyspnea and shortness of breath1
  • Chest tightness and fatigue2
  • Tachycardia and cyanosis if significant lung collapse present2

Exam Findings

  • Diminished or absent breath sounds on affected side1
  • Hyperresonance to percussion ipsilaterally1
  • Decreased tactile fremitus over pneumothorax1
  • Tachypnea and tachycardia if tension pneumothorax3
  • Mediastinal shift and jugular venous distension in tension pneumothorax3

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Myocardial infarction — chest pain with ECG changes3
  • Pulmonary embolism — dyspnea with risk factors3
  • Pleural effusion — dullness to percussion vs hyperresonance1

Less Common

  • Pericarditis — positional chest pain with pericardial rub3
  • Aortic dissection — tearing chest pain radiating to back3
  • Esophageal rupture — chest pain with subcutaneous emphysema1

Mimics

  • Large emphysematous bullae — can mimic pneumothorax on imaging5
  • Skin fold artifact on chest X-ray — lung markings visible beyond fold1
  • Massive pulmonary embolism — cardiovascular collapse3

Diagnostic Tests

+

Chest Radiography1,4

  • Upright chest X-ray: Sn 46%, Sp 100% for pneumothorax detection11
  • Supine chest X-ray: Sn 31.8%, Sp 100% in trauma patients17
  • Deep sulcus sign in supine films suggests pneumothorax13

Point-of-Care Ultrasound (POCUS)11,12

  • Lung sliding assessment: Sn 87%, Sp 99% for pneumothorax detection11
  • Absence of lung sliding + A-lines: Sn 95%, Sp 94% for occult pneumothorax18
  • B-lines presence rules out pneumothorax with high NPV19
  • Lung point sign: Sp 100%, PPV 100% when present18,19

Computed Tomography1,4

  • Gold standard for pneumothorax detection and sizing16
  • Indicated for: recurrent pneumothorax, persistent air leak >4 days, surgical planning1
  • Size concordance with ultrasound within 1.9—2.3 cm18

Treatment

+

Small Primary Spontaneous Pneumothorax (<2 cm rim)1,4

  • Conservative management with observation and supplemental oxygen1
  • Serial chest X-rays at 4—6 hours to assess progression1
  • Discharge criteria: stable size, room air tolerance, adequate follow-up1

Large Primary Spontaneous Pneumothorax (≥2 cm rim)1,4

  • Needle aspiration as first-line intervention per BTS guidelines1
  • Small-bore chest tube (pigtail catheter) if aspiration fails1
  • Large-bore chest tube for significant respiratory distress1

Secondary Spontaneous Pneumothorax1,2

  • Chest tube drainage indicated for most cases due to underlying lung disease1
  • Avoid simple aspiration in patients with COPD or significant comorbidities1
  • Consider pleurodesis for recurrent episodes1

Tension Pneumothorax (ICU/Emergency)3

  • Immediate needle decompression 2nd intercostal space, mid-clavicular line3
  • 14-gauge angiocatheter or longer needle based on chest wall thickness6
  • Followed by definitive chest tube placement3

Surgical Management1,4

  • VATS bullectomy + pleurodesis for recurrent pneumothorax1
  • Open thoracotomy if VATS contraindicated or unsuccessful1
  • Indications: ≥2 recurrences, persistent air leak >7 days, bilateral pneumothorax1

Expected Course

+
  • Spontaneous resolution at rate of 1.25% of pleural space per day without intervention1
  • Primary pneumothorax recurrence: 30% after first episode, 60% after second1
  • Complete resolution typically within 2—4 weeks with appropriate management1
  • Clinical context: Oxygen supplementation can increase reabsorption rate by up to 4-fold

Follow-up

+
  • Chest X-ray at 2—4 weeks to confirm complete resolution1
  • Activity restrictions: avoid air travel for 6 weeks, diving permanently contraindicated1
  • Smoking cessation counseling essential for prevention1
  • Return precautions: worsening dyspnea, chest pain, signs of recurrence1

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Diagnostic Alternatives

  • Clinical diagnosis based on physical exam when imaging unavailable7
  • Needle aspiration both diagnostic and therapeutic1
  • Improvised chest seal using occlusive dressing for tension pneumothorax6

Treatment Modifications

  • Heimlich valve for ambulatory management when available1
  • Water seal drainage using improvised system if commercial unavailable1
  • Extended observation with serial clinical assessments1

Atrial Fibrillation

Last verified: March 11, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Palpitations - sensation of rapid, irregular, or skipping heartbeat1
  • Chest pain1
  • Shortness of breath1
  • Fatigue and generalized weakness1
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness1
  • Nausea1
  • Diaphoresis (severe sweating)1
  • Exercise intolerance2
  • Clinical context: Asymptomatic presentation - may be detected on routine examination or ECG1

Exam Findings

  • Irregularly irregular pulse1
  • Tachycardia - heart rate often > 100 bpm (100-175 bpm typical)3
  • Signs of heart failure - if present may include peripheral edema, jugular venous distension1
  • Variable pulse intensity due to irregular ventricular filling1
  • Clinical context: Signs of stroke or TIA if thromboembolic event has occurred1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Atrial flutter - regular sawtooth pattern on ECG2
  • Multifocal atrial tachycardia (MAT) - irregular but with visible P waves2
  • Sinus tachycardia with frequent PACs2
  • AVNRT (atrioventricular nodal reentrant tachycardia)2

Less Common

  • AVRT (atrioventricular reentrant tachycardia)2
  • Ventricular tachycardia with irregular conduction2
  • Ashman phenomenon - wide QRS in AF due to aberrant conduction4

Mimics

  • Artifact on ECG monitoring2
  • Frequent premature ventricular contractions2
  • Sinus arrhythmia - respiratory variation in healthy individuals2

Diagnostic Tests

+

Electrocardiographic Tests

  • 12-lead ECG: Gold standard - shows absence of P waves, irregularly irregular R-R intervals, narrow QRS complexes (< 120ms unless aberrancy)1,5
  • Holter monitor: 24-48 hours - useful for paroxysmal AF detection1
  • Event monitor/Loop recorder: Extended monitoring for infrequent episodes1
  • Fibrillatory waves: May be fine (< 0.5mm) or coarse (> 0.5mm amplitude)5

Laboratory Tests

  • Basic metabolic panel: Sodium, potassium, creatinine, eGFR1
  • Thyroid function: TSH, T4 - rule out hyperthyroidism1
  • Complete blood count: Hemoglobin assessment1
  • Liver enzymes: Baseline for anticoagulation therapy1
  • Coagulation studies: PT/INR, aPTT1

Imaging Studies

  • Transthoracic echocardiogram: Assess valve disease, LV function, left atrial size1
  • Chest X-ray: Evaluate for heart failure, pulmonary disease1
  • Transesophageal echocardiogram: Rule out atrial thrombus before cardioversion1

Treatment

+

Stroke Prevention - Outpatient6,7

  • CHA₂DS₂-VASc score assessment for thromboembolism risk6
  • Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) preferred: apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban6
  • Warfarin: Alternative when DOACs contraindicated or mechanical heart valve6
  • Anticoagulation recommended for CHA₂DS₂-VA ≥ 1 (excluding female sex as risk factor)6

Rate Control - Outpatient6

  • Beta-blockers: Metoprolol, atenolol (any EF) - first-line6
  • Calcium channel blockers: Diltiazem, verapamil (LVEF > 40% only)6
  • Digoxin: Any EF, useful in heart failure patients6
  • Target heart rate: < 110 bpm at rest, individualize based on symptoms1

Rhythm Control - Outpatient6

  • Electrical cardioversion: For symptom relief and diagnostic purposes6
  • Catheter ablation: Expanded indications, consider in suitable patients within 12 months6
  • Antiarrhythmic drugs: Amiodarone, sotalol, flecainide (structure-dependent)1
  • Rhythm control recommended for symptom reduction and quality of life improvement6

Emergency/Inpatient Management1

  • Hemodynamic instability: Immediate electrical cardioversion1
  • Rate control: IV metoprolol, diltiazem, or digoxin1
  • Anticoagulation: Heparin bridge if cardioversion planned within 48 hours1
  • Monitor for complications: Stroke, heart failure, thromboembolism1

Expected Course

+
  • Paroxysmal AF: Episodes < 7 days, may progress to persistent1
  • Persistent AF: Episodes > 7 days, often requires intervention1
  • Permanent AF: Accepted arrhythmia, focus on rate control and anticoagulation1
  • Stroke risk: 5-fold increased risk, leading cardiac cause of stroke1
  • Heart failure development: May occur due to tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy1

Follow-up

+
  • Anticoagulation monitoring: INR if on warfarin, renal function for DOACs1
  • Rhythm monitoring: Periodic ECGs or event monitors1
  • Symptom assessment: Modified EHRA score for symptom burden6
  • Echocardiography: Annual or with clinical change1
  • Risk factor modification: Blood pressure control, weight management, OSA treatment6

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Basic Diagnosis

  • Pulse palpation: Screen for irregularly irregular rhythm1
  • Basic ECG: Single-lead ECG devices for detection1
  • Clinical assessment: Focus on stroke risk factors1

Essential Treatment

  • Aspirin: If anticoagulation unavailable (suboptimal stroke prevention)1
  • Rate control: Beta-blockers or digoxin1
  • Risk factor modification: Blood pressure control, lifestyle changes1

Relevant Clinical Scores

CHA₂DS₂-VASc Score

CHA₂DS₂-VASc Score 0
0 points: Low risk. Anticoagulation generally not recommended.

HAS-BLED Bleeding Risk Score

HAS-BLED Bleeding Risk Score 0
Low bleeding risk. Proceed with anticoagulation if indicated.

Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia

Last verified: March 11, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • HIT is generally not associated with bleeding1
  • Clinical context: Patients may be asymptomatic if no thrombotic complications occur
  • Symptoms related to thrombotic complications: chest pain, shortness of breath, limb pain or swelling1

Exam Findings

  • A fall in platelet counts and a hypercoagulable state characterize HIT1
  • Most patients have platelet count ranging from 60,000 to 80,000/mm³2
  • Patients who experience HIT may develop thromboembolic complications that are associated with significant morbidity and mortality1
  • Clinical context: Signs of thrombosis including limb ischemia, organ infarction, or pulmonary embolism may be present

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Type I HIT (HAT) — non-immune mediated thrombocytopenia1
  • Immune thrombocytopenia (ITP)1
  • Disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)1
  • Drug-induced thrombocytopenia from other medications1

Less Common

  • Liver disease1
  • Myelosuppression1
  • Hemodilution1
  • Clinical context: Pseudothrombocytopenia from EDTA-induced platelet clumping

Mimics

  • Antiphospholipid antibody syndrome (APS) — thrombocytopenia with thrombotic events3
  • Thrombotic microangiopathy3
  • Paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria (PNH)3

Diagnostic Tests

+

Clinical Scoring Systems

  • 4Ts Score: NPV for low probability score 0.998 (95% CI 0.970-1.000), PPV for intermediate probability 0.14 (0.09-0.22), PPV for high probability 0.64 (0.40-0.82)4
  • Score of 3 or less effectively rules out HIT; further testing is not required5

Laboratory Tests

  • Immunoassays (PF4/heparin ELISA): Widely available, sensitive and simple to perform but yield frequent false-positive results6
  • Functional assays (Serotonin Release Assay): Much greater specificity but require radioisotope and reactive donor platelets6
  • Score ≥ 4 warrants PF4-heparin antibody testing; if positive, functional assay should be performed5

Treatment

+

Acute Management7

  • Immediate heparin discontinuation: The 2 key principles are (1) avoid heparin and (2) use nonheparin anticoagulation7
  • Score ≥ 4 warrants immediate discontinuation of heparin and initiation of non-heparin anticoagulant5

Non-Heparin Anticoagulants

  • Argatroban: 2 mcg/kg/min as continuous IV infusion; metabolized by liver, dose should be reduced in hepatic impairment8,2
  • Bivalirudin: 0.75 mg/kg IV bolus followed by 1.75 mg/kg/h maintenance infusion9
  • Fondaparinux: Clinical context: Alternative option, typical dosing 7.5 mg subcutaneous daily
  • DOACs: Rivaroxaban for treatment of acute HIT as initial therapy in hemodynamically stable patients10

Special Considerations

  • ASH guideline: therapeutic intensity anticoagulation for high-probability 4T score (6-8) or intermediate-probability (4-5) with indication for anticoagulation1
  • Warfarin should be avoided during acute episode2
  • Clinical context: Platelet transfusions are contraindicated and may worsen thrombosis

Expected Course

+
  • Onset typically occurs 5-14 days after start of heparin therapy, may occur after heparin cessation1
  • May develop sooner if prior heparin exposure within preceding 3 months2
  • Initial 5-10% daily risk of thrombosis, amputation, or death without treatment4
  • High morbidity (60-90%) and mortality rates (6-30%); even with early recognition, morbidity 7.4% and mortality 1.1%11

Follow-up

+
  • Clinical context: Platelet count monitoring until recovery to normal levels
  • Clinical context: Continue non-heparin anticoagulation until platelet count recovery and resolution of thrombotic complications
  • Patient education on heparin avoidance and medical alert bracelet for 3 months7
  • Clinical context: Long-term anticoagulation duration depends on presence and type of thrombotic complications

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Limited Laboratory Access

  • Clinical context: Rely on 4Ts score for clinical decision-making when laboratory tests unavailable
  • Clinical context: Consider empiric treatment with non-heparin anticoagulant if high clinical suspicion

Alternative Anticoagulants

  • Clinical context: Fondaparinux may be more readily available than argatroban or bivalirudin
  • Clinical context: DOACs may be considered if available and patient is hemodynamically stable

INTERACT2 — Intensive BP Lowering in Acute ICH

NEJM • 2013 • Anderson CS, Heeley E, Huang Y, et al.
Read on WikiJournalClub →

Clinical Question

Does intensive blood pressure lowering to <140 mmHg systolic within 6 hours of acute intracerebral hemorrhage improve functional outcomes compared to standard care?

Study Design

Randomized, controlled, open-label trial with blinded outcome assessment. International multicenter study across 144 hospitals in 21 countries. Patients randomized 1:1 to intensive vs. guideline-recommended BP management.

Population

2,839 patients with acute spontaneous ICH within 6 hours of onset, elevated systolic BP (150-220 mmHg), no definite indication/contraindication for intensive BP lowering. Excluded: structural causes, deep coma, massive hematoma with poor prognosis.

Intervention

Intensive BP lowering: target SBP <140 mmHg within 1 hour and maintained for 7 days using IV antihypertensive agents (nicardipine, labetalol, enalaprilat, hydralazine per local protocols).

Comparator / Control

Guideline-recommended BP management: target SBP <180 mmHg using same antihypertensive agents. More conservative approach following 2007 AHA/ASA guidelines.

Primary Endpoint

Death or major disability at 90 days, defined as modified Rankin Scale (mRS) scores 3-6. Secondary endpoints included ordinal analysis of mRS, mortality, serious adverse events.

Key Findings

Primary endpoint: 52.0% intensive vs. 55.6% guideline group (OR 0.87, 95% CI 0.75-1.01, p=0.06). Ordinal analysis of mRS showed significant improvement (OR 0.87, 95% CI 0.77-1.00, p=0.04). ARR = 3.6%, RRR = 6.5%, NNT = 28. No increase in serious adverse events (23.3% vs. 23.6%, p=0.83).

Strengths

Large international sample size, pragmatic design reflecting real-world practice, excellent follow-up (99.6%), pre-specified ordinal analysis, consistent treatment effect across subgroups, safety demonstrated with no excess adverse events.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Primary endpoint narrowly missed statistical significance (p=0.06), open-label design, modest treatment effect size, predominantly Asian population (88%), relatively small hematomas (median 11 mL), short-term follow-up only.

Practice Impact

Shifted clinical practice toward more aggressive BP management in acute ICH. Influenced 2015 AHA/ASA stroke guidelines to consider SBP target <140 mmHg as reasonable. Changed emergency department and stroke unit protocols for ICH management globally.

Why It Matters Now

Remains cornerstone evidence for acute ICH BP management. Subsequent meta-analyses and real-world studies have generally supported intensive BP lowering. ATACH-2 trial results created some controversy, but INTERACT2 findings still widely accepted and implemented in current guidelines.
