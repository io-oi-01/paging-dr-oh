Based on: PubMed abstract
Placebo may be inferior compared to acetaminophen when given with morphine for the management of acute pain in the emergency department
Understanding optimal pain management combinations in emergency settings is crucial given the ongoing opioid crisis and need for effective multimodal analgesia.
Design: Prospective cohort study comparing patients who received intravenous acetaminophen with opioids versus those who received opioids alone • Outcome: Opioid requirement measured in Morphine Equivalent Units during Emergency Department stay
Population: Adult Emergency Department patients aged 18 years and older treated with opioids for painful conditions
Intervention: Intravenous acetaminophen plus opioids
Comparator: Opioids alone
Key Results
  • No significant difference in opioid consumption during ED stay between groups (10.0 MEU vs 10.0 MEU, p=0.53)
  • Trend toward decreased opioid requirement at 24h post-discharge (15.0 MEU vs 30.0 MEU, p=0.059)
  • Both groups achieved similar pain reduction (median NRS from 9.0 to 4.0) and patient satisfaction (>80%)
  • Nine minor adverse events recorded, equally distributed between groups
Clinical Interpretation
  • IV acetaminophen did not provide opioid-sparing effects during ED stay, contrary to expectations for multimodal analgesia
  • The trend toward reduced post-discharge opioid use suggests potential delayed benefits
  • Both approaches achieved similar pain control and patient satisfaction
Limitations
  • Single-center study
  • Non-randomized design
  • Relatively small sample size (116 patients)
Bottom Line: IV acetaminophen does not reduce opioid requirements during ED stay but may decrease post-discharge opioid use.
High confidence 📊 Informative

SURPASS-2 — Tirzepatide vs Semaglutide in T2DM

NEJM • 2021 • Frías et al.

Clinical Question

Is tirzepatide superior to semaglutide 1 mg for glycemic control and weight reduction in adults with type 2 diabetes inadequately controlled on metformin?

Study Design

Phase 3, randomized, open-label, active-controlled trial conducted at 122 sites across 6 countries. Participants were randomized 1:1:1:1 to tirzepatide 5 mg, 10 mg, 15 mg, or semaglutide 1 mg once weekly for 40 weeks.

Population

Adults with T2DM on stable metformin ≥1500 mg/day, HbA1c 7.0-10.5%, BMI ≥25 kg/m². Key exclusions: T1DM, recent cardiovascular events, severe renal impairment. N=1,879 randomized and treated.

Intervention

Tirzepatide 5 mg, 10 mg, or 15 mg subcutaneous injection once weekly (dual GIP/GLP-1 receptor agonist)

Comparator / Control

Semaglutide 1 mg subcutaneous injection once weekly (GLP-1 receptor agonist)

Primary Endpoint

Change in HbA1c from baseline to week 40 (non-inferiority margin 0.4%, superiority margin 0%)

Key Findings

HbA1c reduction from baseline: Tirzepatide 5mg -2.01%, 10mg -2.24%, 15mg -2.30% vs Semaglutide -1.86%. Treatment differences: 5mg -0.15% (95% CI -0.29 to -0.02, p=0.03), 10mg -0.38% (95% CI -0.51 to -0.24, p<0.001), 15mg -0.44% (95% CI -0.58 to -0.31, p<0.001). Weight loss: Tirzepatide 15mg -11.2 kg vs Semaglutide -5.7 kg, difference -5.5 kg (95% CI -6.7 to -4.2, p<0.001). HbA1c <7%: Tirzepatide 15mg 86% vs Semaglutide 79% (p=0.02). Nausea rates similar across groups (12-18%).

Strengths

Large sample size, active comparator design, dose-ranging evaluation, robust statistical analysis with multiple endpoints, good completion rates (>85%), international multicenter design enhancing generalizability

Weaknesses / Limitations

Open-label design introducing potential bias, relatively short 40-week duration, predominantly white population (70%), industry-sponsored, lack of cardiovascular outcome data, higher discontinuation in highest tirzepatide dose group

Practice Impact

Established tirzepatide as superior to semaglutide for both glycemic control and weight loss, leading to FDA approval in 2022. Changed T2DM treatment algorithms by providing a more effective option for patients requiring intensive glucose lowering and weight management.

Why It Matters Now

Represents the most effective diabetes medication for dual glucose and weight control. Subsequent SURPASS trials confirmed cardiovascular safety. Growing real-world experience supports trial findings. Cost and access remain barriers, but clinical efficacy has established tirzepatide as preferred option for appropriate patients.

Diabetic Ketoacidosis

Last verified: March 12, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Nausea and vomiting — frequently accompany the metabolic decompensation1
  • Polyuria and polydipsia — secondary to hyperglycemia and osmotic diuresis1
  • Abdominal pain — often severe and can mimic surgical conditions1
  • Altered mental status — ranging from confusion to coma1
  • Fruity breath odor — due to acetone, a volatile ketone body1
  • Weight loss — from fluid losses and catabolism1

Exam Findings

  • Kussmaul respirations — deep, labored breathing as compensatory response to acidosis1
  • Dehydration signs — dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor, hypotension1
  • Tachycardia — compensatory response to volume depletion1
  • Hypotension — may progress to shock in severe cases1
  • Altered mental status — confusion, lethargy, or coma (severity correlates with osmolality)1
  • Ketotic breath odor — characteristic fruity smell1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Hyperglycemic hyperosmolar state (HHS) — extreme hyperglycemia without significant ketosis2
  • Sepsis — can precipitate DKA and shares similar presentation2
  • Pneumonia — common precipitant and mimics DKA symptoms2
  • Urinary tract infection — frequent trigger for metabolic decompensation2

Less Common

  • Alcoholic ketoacidosis — glucose levels rarely >250 mg/dL, β-hydroxybutyrate:acetoacetate ratio up to 8:13
  • Starvation ketoacidosis — dietary intake <500 kcal/day, normal or low glucose2
  • Salicylate poisoning — mixed acid-base disorder with respiratory alkalosis and metabolic acidosis4
  • Methanol or ethylene glycol ingestion — elevated osmolar gap4

Mimics

  • Pancreatitis — abdominal pain and can precipitate DKA2
  • Myocardial infarction — can present with metabolic decompensation2
  • Lactic acidosis — high anion gap without ketonemia4
  • Uremia — metabolic acidosis in chronic kidney disease4

Diagnostic Tests

+

Laboratory Criteria (2024 Consensus)5

  • Glucose: ≥200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L) OR prior diabetes history irrespective of glucose5
  • Ketones: β-hydroxybutyrate ≥3.0 mmol/L (venous/capillary) OR urine ketones 2+ or greater5
  • Metabolic acidosis: pH <7.3 AND/OR bicarbonate <18 mmol/L5

Blood Gas Analysis1

  • Arterial pH: <7.3 (severe <7.0, moderate 7.0-7.24, mild 7.25-7.30)1
  • Bicarbonate: <18 mEq/L (severe <10, moderate 10-15, mild 15-18)1
  • Anion gap: Usually >12 mEq/L (Note: 2024 guidelines removed anion gap as diagnostic criterion)5

Additional Testing1

  • Basic metabolic panel: assess electrolytes, renal function, glucose1
  • Serum osmolality: typically elevated due to hyperglycemia and dehydration1
  • Complete blood count: may show leukocytosis even without infection1
  • Urinalysis: ketones, glucose, signs of infection1

Treatment

+

Fluid Resuscitation (2024 Guidelines)5

  • Initial fluid: 0.9% NaCl or balanced electrolyte solutions at 15-20 mL/kg/h5
  • Goal: Replace 50% of estimated fluid deficit (typically 6L) in first 8-12 hours5
  • Dextrose addition: Add when glucose <250 mg/dL to prevent hypoglycemia5
  • Clinical context: Balanced electrolyte solutions may resolve DKA 5.36 hours faster than 0.9% saline5

Insulin Therapy1,5

  • Initial dose: Regular insulin IV infusion 0.1 units/kg/hour1
  • Pediatric dosing: 0.1 units/kg/hour continuous infusion (no bolus doses)1
  • Glucose monitoring: Check every 1-2 hours initially5
  • Transition to subcutaneous: Continue IV insulin 1-2 hours after starting subcutaneous insulin5

Electrolyte Management1,5

  • Potassium replacement: Monitor closely, replace as needed (typically depleted despite normal/high serum levels)1
  • Phosphate: Consider replacement if severely low1
  • Bicarbonate: Only if pH <6.9 per ADA guidelines (not routinely recommended)4
  • Monitoring frequency: Check electrolytes every 2-4 hours until stable5

ICU vs Ward Management1,5

  • ICU indications: Severe DKA (pH <7.0), altered mental status, hemodynamic instability1
  • Step-down unit: Moderate DKA (pH 7.0-7.24) with stable vital signs1
  • General ward: Mild DKA (pH 7.25-7.30) in alert, stable patients1

Expected Course

+
  • Resolution criteria: Glucose <200 mg/dL, bicarbonate ≥18 mEq/L, venous pH >7.35
  • Time to resolution: Typically 12-24 hours with appropriate treatment1
  • Mortality rate: >5% in elderly patients and those with severe comorbidities1
  • Prognosis factors: Age extremes, coma, hypotension, and severe comorbidities worsen prognosis1

Follow-up

+
  • Diabetes education: Enhanced patient education and improved access to medical care reduce recurrence1
  • Medication adherence: Address barriers to insulin therapy compliance1
  • Sick day management: Educate on sick day rules and when to seek medical attention5
  • Regular monitoring: Establish regular follow-up with endocrinology or primary care5

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Essential Interventions1

  • Fluid resuscitation: 0.9% normal saline if balanced solutions unavailable5
  • Regular insulin: Can use subcutaneous insulin in milder cases if IV pumps unavailable1
  • Urine ketones: Use dipstick testing if serum β-hydroxybutyrate unavailable1
  • Basic monitoring: Glucose, electrolytes, and pH at minimum1
