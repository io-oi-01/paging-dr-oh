VADT — Intensive Glucose Control in T2DM Veterans

NEJM • 2009 • Duckworth et al./VADT Investigators

Clinical Question

Does intensive glucose control reduce major cardiovascular events and mortality in patients with advanced type 2 diabetes mellitus compared to standard glucose control?

Study Design

Multicenter, randomized, open-label, controlled trial conducted at 20 VA medical centers. Participants were randomized 1:1 to intensive vs standard glucose control with median follow-up of 5.6 years. Primary care physicians were aware of treatment assignments but outcome assessors were blinded to treatment allocation.

Population

N=1,791 military veterans with type 2 diabetes (mean age 60 years, 97% male, mean diabetes duration 11.5 years, baseline HbA1c 9.4%). Key inclusion: suboptimally controlled T2DM on maximum tolerated oral agents or insulin. Exclusion: advanced CVD, life expectancy <7 years, frequent severe hypoglycemia.

Intervention

Intensive glucose control targeting HbA1c <6.0% using combination of insulin, metformin, rosiglitazone, and other agents as needed. Achieved median HbA1c of 6.9%.

Comparator / Control

Standard glucose control with less aggressive targets, achieving median HbA1c of 8.4% (separation of 1.5% maintained throughout study).

Primary Endpoint

Time to first occurrence of major cardiovascular event (composite of MI, stroke, cardiovascular death, new/worsening CHF, surgical intervention for cardiac/cerebrovascular/peripheral vascular disease, inoperable coronary disease, amputation for ischemic gangrene).

Key Findings

Primary endpoint occurred in 264 intensive group vs 235 standard group patients. HR 0.88 (95% CI 0.74-1.05, p=0.14). ARR = 3.1%, RRR = 12%, NNT = 32. All-cause mortality: HR 1.07 (95% CI 0.81-1.42, p=0.62). CV mortality: HR 1.32 (95% CI 0.81-2.14, p=0.26). Severe hypoglycemia was significantly higher in intensive group (21.2% vs 9.9%, p<0.001).

Strengths

Large sample size, excellent separation of HbA1c levels (1.5%), long follow-up duration, robust composite endpoint, intention-to-treat analysis, representative of real-world diabetes patients with established disease.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Open-label design, male-predominant veteran population limiting generalizability, relatively older patients with long diabetes duration, study may have been underpowered for mortality endpoints, high rate of severe hypoglycemia in intensive group.

Practice Impact

Challenged the paradigm that intensive glycemic control always reduces cardiovascular events. Led to more individualized HbA1c targets, particularly in older patients with established diabetes and cardiovascular risk factors. Influenced guidelines to recommend less stringent targets (7-8%) in certain high-risk populations.

Why It Matters Now

Along with ACCORD and ADVANCE trials, established the concept of individualized glycemic targets based on patient characteristics. Remains relevant for understanding that intensive control may not benefit all patients and reinforced focus on avoiding hypoglycemia. Current guidelines still reference VADT when recommending less stringent targets for older adults with complex comorbidities.

Lower GI Bleeding

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Hematochezia: Bright red blood per rectum — most common presentation in moderate to massive bleeding1
  • Melena: Dark, tarry stools indicating slower bleeding or more proximal source1
  • Hematemesis: Less common, may indicate rapid upper GI bleeding with lower presentation1
  • Fatigue, dyspnea, palpitations: Symptoms of anemia in occult or chronic bleeding1
  • Abdominal pain: May be absent in diverticular bleeding (painless hematochezia characteristic)1

Exam Findings

  • Hemodynamic instability: SBP ≤ 90 mmHg, HR ≥ 100/min in massive bleeding1
  • Pallor and signs of anemia: Pale conjunctivae, tachycardia1
  • Low urine output: Oliguria in severe hemorrhage1
  • Rectal examination findings: Blood on digital rectal exam, hemorrhoids, masses1
  • Abdominal examination: Assessment for tenderness, masses, distention, signs of cirrhosis5

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Diverticular disease: Accounts for >40% of lower GI bleeds, painless hematochezia1
  • Ischemic colitis: Occurs in 20% of lower GI bleeds, more prevalent in elderly1
  • Hemorrhoids: Internal/external, bleeding with or without defecation5
  • Angiodysplasia: Most frequent cause of small bowel bleeding in patients >60 years8
  • Inflammatory bowel disease: Ulcerative colitis, Crohn's disease with chronic inflammation5

Less Common

  • Colorectal neoplasia: Cancer represents ~3.4% of cases but serious cause to rule out5
  • Post-polypectomy bleeding: Iatrogenic cause following endoscopic procedures1
  • Infectious colitis: Various pathogens causing mucosal inflammation1
  • Radiation proctitis: Following pelvic radiation therapy1
  • NSAID-induced ulcers: Medication-related mucosal injury1

Mimics

  • Upper GI bleeding: ~33% of presumed lower GI bleeds with heavy bleeding are actually upper GI source1
  • Small bowel bleeding: 0.7-9% of lower GI bleeds originate from small intestine1
  • Hemosuccus pancreaticus: Rare cause (~1/1,500 GI bleeds) from pancreatic duct bleeding9

Diagnostic Tests

+

Initial Laboratory Assessment

  • Complete blood count: Hemoglobin ≤ 6 g/dL in massive bleeding1
  • Basic metabolic panel: Assess renal function, electrolytes1
  • Coagulation studies: PT/INR, PTT if anticoagulated or bleeding disorder suspected1
  • Type and crossmatch: Prepare for transfusion in active bleeding1

Imaging Studies

  • CT Angiography: Sn 86%, Sp 95%, detects bleeding ≥ 0.4 mL/min — initial test for hemodynamically unstable patients23
  • Tagged RBC scan: Sn 93%, Sp 95%, detects bleeding as low as 0.1 mL/min, continuous imaging capability6
  • Catheter angiography: Requires bleeding rate 0.5-1.0 mL/min, allows therapeutic intervention2

Endoscopic Evaluation

  • Colonoscopy: Preferred initial diagnostic procedure, Sn up to 98%, allows therapeutic intervention21
  • Upper endoscopy: If NG aspirate positive or clinical suspicion of upper source26
  • Flexible sigmoidoscopy: For distal bleeding sources, hemorrhoids, proctitis5

Treatment

+

Emergency Department/Initial Stabilization1

  • IV fluid resuscitation: Two large-bore IVs, crystalloid for hypotension
  • Blood transfusion: Packed RBCs if Hgb ≤ 7 g/dL or symptomatic anemia
  • Reversal agents: For life-threatening bleeding on anticoagulants (VKA, DOACs)

Inpatient Non-ICU Management12

  • Risk stratification: Oakland score ≤ 8 identifies low-risk patients for early discharge
  • Colonoscopy timing: Non-urgent within 14 days as effective as within 24 hours for stable patients
  • Conservative management: >80% of lower GI bleeds stop spontaneously

ICU Management8

  • Hemodynamic monitoring: Continuous vital signs, frequent CBC monitoring
  • Massive transfusion protocol: For ongoing heavy bleeding with hemodynamic instability
  • Endoscopic therapy: Clips, band ligation, coagulation for active bleeding lesions
  • Interventional radiology: Transcatheter embolization within 90 minutes of positive CTA (98% success rate)

Expected Course

+
  • Spontaneous cessation: >80% of lower GI bleeds stop without intervention1
  • Mortality rate: Overall 2-4%, but up to 21% in massive bleeding1
  • Recurrence risk: Diverticular bleeding commonly recurs, requiring early identification1
  • Hospital stay: Increasing complexity with longer lengths of stay in recent years18

Follow-up

+
  • Anticoagulation resumption: Resume after bleeding cessation to decrease thromboembolism risk18
  • Colonoscopy surveillance: For patients >40 years or concerning features5
  • Repeat bleeding evaluation: CT angiography or endoscopy for recurrent episodes12
  • Medication review: Assess need for antiplatelet agents, NSAIDs1

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Clinical Assessment

  • Hemodynamic stability assessment: Orthostatic vital signs, clinical appearance
  • Digital rectal examination: Essential component of Oakland score
  • Basic laboratory studies: CBC, basic metabolic panel if available

Conservative Management

  • IV fluid support: Crystalloid resuscitation for hypotension
  • Blood transfusion: If available, target restrictive strategy
  • Transfer criteria: Hemodynamic instability, ongoing bleeding, high-risk features
