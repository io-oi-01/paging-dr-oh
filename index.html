Status Epilepticus

Last verified: March 8, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Continuous seizure activity ≥5 minutes or recurrent seizures without recovery between episodes1
  • Convulsive SE: generalized tonic-clonic movements with mental status impairment1
  • Non-convulsive SE: altered mental status, behavioral changes, or confusion without obvious motor manifestations1
  • Focal motor SE: refractory motor activity of a limb or muscle group on one side ± loss of consciousness1
  • Myoclonic SE: repetitive, brief muscle jerks1

Exam Findings

  • Ongoing tonic-clonic activity in convulsive SE1
  • Impaired consciousness or altered mental status1
  • Subtle motor signs: nystagmic eye movements, facial twitching, extremity twitches in non-convulsive SE2
  • Post-ictal confusion and weakness (Todd paralysis) may occur6
  • Motor deficits ranging from quadriparesis to subtle soft palate paresis6

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Breakthrough seizures in known epilepsy patients (25% of cases)2
  • Ethanol-related seizures (25% of cases)2
  • Metabolic causes: hypoglycemia, hyponatremia, hypocalcemia, hepatic encephalopathy1
  • CNS infections: meningitis, encephalitis, intracranial abscess1
  • Medication non-compliance or irregular antiepileptic drug use2

Less Common

  • Stroke or intracranial hemorrhage1
  • Traumatic brain injury1
  • CNS tumors or mass lesions1
  • Autoimmune encephalitis1
  • Drug toxicity: tricyclic antidepressants, theophylline, sympathomimetics2

Mimics

  • Psychogenic non-epileptic seizures2
  • Movement disorders2
  • Syncope with convulsive movements2
  • Catatonia or other psychiatric conditions2
  • Hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy2

Diagnostic Tests

+

Laboratory Tests (STAT)22

  • Blood glucose: immediate point-of-care testing22
  • Complete blood count and basic metabolic panel22
  • Serum calcium (total and ionized), magnesium22
  • Antiepileptic drug levels if patient on chronic therapy22
  • Toxicology screen (urine and blood) for suspected ingestions22

Neuroimaging22

  • CT head (non-contrast): immediate for suspected structural lesion22
  • MRI brain: higher diagnostic yield based on focal findings2

Neurophysiology22

  • Continuous EEG monitoring: essential for non-convulsive SE diagnosis22
  • Urgent EEG: if non-convulsive SE suspected2

Additional Tests (if indicated)22

  • Lumbar puncture: if fever, immunosuppression, or CNS infection suspected2
  • Arterial blood gas, liver function tests, coagulation studies22
  • Autoimmune/paraneoplastic antibody panel for refractory cases11

Treatment

+

Immediate Stabilization (0-5 minutes)11

  • Airway, breathing, circulation assessment & support11
  • Supplemental oxygen via nasal cannula or non-invasive methods3
  • IV access & point-of-care glucose22
  • Rapid sequence intubation if impaired oxygenation/ventilation3

First-line Treatment (0-10 minutes)19

  • Lorazepam 4 mg IV (may repeat once) OR19
  • Midazolam 10 mg IM (preferred if no IV access) OR19
  • Diazepam 10 mg IV (may repeat once)19
  • Clinical context: Up to 2 doses of benzodiazepines within first 5-10 minutes12

Second-line Treatment (Established SE)19

  • Fosphenytoin 20 mg/kg phenytoin equivalent IV (max 1500 mg)19
  • Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg IV (max 4500 mg) - equivalent efficacy to fosphenytoin19
  • Valproate 20-40 mg/kg IV (max 3000 mg)19
  • Phenobarbital 15-20 mg/kg IV19
  • Clinical context: Administer immediately if seizures persist after benzodiazepines19

Refractory SE (ICU)19

  • Midazolam continuous infusion: load 0.2 mg/kg, then 0.1-2 mg/kg/hr19
  • Propofol continuous infusion: load 1-2 mg/kg, then 2-10 mg/kg/hr19
  • Pentobarbital continuous infusion: load 5-15 mg/kg, then 0.5-5 mg/kg/hr19
  • Ketamine: emerging evidence supports use in refractory cases19
  • Clinical context: Requires continuous EEG monitoring & mechanical ventilation19

Expected Course

+
  • Short-term mortality: 7.6-22% across all age groups, highest in elderly21
  • Pediatric mortality: 3-11% in retrospective series22
  • Refractory SE: develops in ~32% of cases despite appropriate treatment1
  • Neurological sequelae: risk increases with seizure duration & delayed treatment1
  • Clinical context: Outcomes depend heavily on underlying etiology & time to treatment initiation21

Follow-up

+
  • Neurology consultation: required for all patients with SE27
  • Long-term antiepileptic therapy: initiate in parallel with acute treatment27
  • EEG monitoring: continue for ongoing altered mental status or refractory cases27
  • Underlying etiology workup: comprehensive investigation as clinically indicated27
  • Specialist referral: consider epilepsy center for refractory or super-refractory cases28

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Medication Alternatives

  • Rectal diazepam when IV access unavailable30
  • Intranasal or buccal midazolam for prehospital treatment30
  • Phenytoin (rather than fosphenytoin) if cost-effective19

Monitoring

  • Clinical seizure cessation as primary endpoint when EEG unavailable19
  • Neurological examination for ongoing assessment2
  • Vital sign monitoring for treatment complications11

Aortic Dissection

Last verified: March 8, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Severe aortic pain: sudden onset, described as 'tearing,' 'ripping,' 'migrating,' or 'pulsating' pain most commonly in chest or back1,2
  • Sudden onset: characteristically presents with abrupt, severe chest or back pain1
  • Location: pain may migrate from anterior chest (ascending dissection) to posterior back (descending dissection)2
  • Clinical context: Pain severity rated as worst of patient's life in most cases
  • Subtle presentations often lead to missed diagnoses despite classic symptoms1

Exam Findings

  • Blood pressure differential ≥20 mmHg between arms (Type A dissection)1
  • Aortic regurgitation murmur (may occur if dissection extends to aortic valve)1
  • Malperfusion syndrome: organ ischemia from compromised branch vessels2
  • Pericardial tamponade: may occur from aortic rupture into pericardium1
  • Decreased pulses in upper extremities (if dissection affects arch branches)1
  • Neurologic deficits: stroke or spinal cord ischemia from vessel compromise1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Myocardial infarction: especially ST-elevation MI presenting with chest pain3
  • Pulmonary embolism: sudden chest pain, shortness of breath, hemodynamic instability3
  • Acute coronary syndrome: unstable angina or MI with radiating chest pain3

Less Common

  • Pericarditis: chest pain with pericardial friction rub
  • Pneumothorax: sudden chest pain with dyspnea
  • Esophageal rupture: severe chest pain after vomiting (Boerhaave syndrome)

Mimics

  • Intramural hematoma: bleeding within aortic wall without intimal tear2
  • Penetrating atherosclerotic ulcer: atherosclerotic plaque penetrating vessel wall2
  • Traumatic aortic injury: following blunt chest trauma3

Diagnostic Tests

+

First-Line Imaging4

  • CT angiography (CTA): ECG-gated contrast CT from neck to pelvis - first-line imaging technique4
  • Transesophageal echocardiography (TEE): real-time bedside imaging, useful for hemodynamically unstable patients3,5

Additional Imaging

  • Chest X-ray: may show mediastinal widening but has low sensitivity1
  • Transthoracic echocardiography: may detect aortic regurgitation and pericardial effusion1
  • MRI: when CT contraindicated or for surveillance imaging4

Risk Stratification

  • Aortic dissection detection risk score: multiparametric diagnostic algorithm recommended4
  • High-risk conditions: assess connective tissue disorders, family history4
  • High-risk pain features: sudden onset, severe, tearing quality4

Treatment

+

Emergency/ICU Management - Type A Dissection1,4

  • Immediate surgery: emergency surgical repair with resection of primary entry tear6
  • Blood pressure control: target SBP 100-120 mmHg, HR <60 bpm
  • Beta-blockers: esmolol or metoprolol for rate/pressure control
  • Pain management: adequate analgesia while avoiding hypotension

Emergency/ICU Management - Type B Dissection4,6

  • Complicated Type B: endovascular repair (TEVAR) recommended for rupture, malperfusion, rapid expansion4
  • Uncomplicated Type B: medical management initially, consider subacute TEVAR (IIa/B)4
  • Antihypertensive therapy: first-line treatment for uncomplicated acute dissection6
  • Beta-blockers: labetalol, esmolol, or metoprolol

Medical Management

  • Long-term beta-blockers: reduce aortic wall stress and prevent progression
  • ARBs/ACE inhibitors: for additional blood pressure control
  • Statin therapy: for cardiovascular risk reduction
  • Activity restriction: avoid heavy lifting, competitive sports initially

Expected Course

+
  • Type A dissection: extremely high mortality (>50% within 24 hours without surgery)1,7
  • Malperfusion complications: increase mortality by up to 39%7
  • Chronic dissection: defined as >2 weeks duration, slightly better prognosis1
  • Long-term surveillance: lifelong imaging required to monitor for aneurysm formation
  • Neurologic recovery: complete recovery possible with prompt surgical repair in type A6

Follow-up

+
  • Imaging surveillance: serial CT or MRI every 3-6 months initially, then annually4
  • Blood pressure monitoring: target <130/80 mmHg with home monitoring
  • Activity assessment: gradual return to activities based on aortic stability
  • Family screening: genetic testing and imaging of first-degree relatives recommended4
  • Specialist follow-up: cardiothoracic surgery and/or vascular medicine

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Limited Imaging Settings

  • Chest X-ray: may show mediastinal widening though insensitive1
  • Echocardiography: transthoracic may detect aortic regurgitation, pericardial effusion
  • Clinical suspicion: high index of suspicion based on symptoms and risk factors

Medical Management Priority

  • Blood pressure control: any available beta-blocker or antihypertensive
  • Pain control: adequate analgesia while monitoring blood pressure
  • Transfer planning: immediate transfer to surgical center for Type A dissection

Hospital-Acquired Pneumonia

Last verified: March 8, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Fever and temperature rise1
  • Cough with or without sputum production1
  • Purulent sputum production1
  • Chest pain (pleuritic)1
  • Dyspnea and respiratory distress1
  • Hypoxia and decline in oxygenation1

Exam Findings

  • Fever and tachypnea1
  • Pulmonary consolidation on examination1
  • Crackles on auscultation1
  • Leukocytosis on laboratory testing1
  • New lung infiltrate on chest imaging1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Community-acquired pneumonia — if onset <48 hours after admission1
  • Ventilator-associated pneumonia — if mechanically ventilated >48-72 hours1
  • Atelectasis with fever2
  • Pulmonary edema with infiltrates2

Less Common

  • Pulmonary embolism with infarction2
  • Drug-induced pneumonitis2
  • Aspiration pneumonia (witnessed aspiration)2
  • Viral pneumonia (influenza, COVID-19)2

Mimics

  • Central line-associated bloodstream infection2
  • Catheter-associated urinary tract infection2
  • Sinusitis in intubated patients2
  • Drug fever from antibiotics2

Diagnostic Tests

+

Respiratory Sampling1

  • Endotracheal aspiration with semiquantitative culture (preferred over invasive methods)2
  • Sputum culture if patient can expectorate1
  • Blood cultures (2 sets) before antibiotics1
  • Nasal MRSA PCR — excellent negative predictive value for MRSA pneumonia3

Imaging1

  • Chest X-ray — shows new infiltrate, confirms diagnosis1
  • CT chest if chest X-ray inconclusive1

Laboratory1

  • Complete blood count — leukocytosis common1
  • Procalcitonin — NOT recommended for diagnosis but may aid de-escalation at 48-72 hours if <0.3 or decreased ≥80%2,3
  • Arterial blood gas if hypoxia suspected1

Treatment

+

Low Risk HAP (No MRSA/Pseudomonas Risk Factors)1

  • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g IV q6h OR
  • Cefepime 2 g IV q8h OR
  • Levofloxacin 750 mg IV/PO daily OR
  • Imipenem 500 mg IV q6h OR
  • Meropenem 1 g IV q8h

High Risk HAP (MRSA/Pseudomonas Risk Factors Present)1,2

  • Anti-pseudomonal β-lactam: Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g IV q6h OR Cefepime 2 g IV q8h OR Meropenem 1 g IV q8h
  • PLUS Anti-MRSA agent: Vancomycin 15-20 mg/kg IV q8-12h (target trough 15-20 mcg/mL) OR Linezolid 600 mg IV/PO q12h
  • Consider dual anti-pseudomonal therapy for high resistance units or septic shock

Duration and De-escalation1,2

  • Standard duration: 7 days for most patients1
  • Extended duration: Consider longer for bacteremia, slow response, immunocompromise, empyema, or lung abscess1
  • De-escalation at 48-72 hours based on culture results and clinical response1,2
  • Discontinue vancomycin if nasal MRSA PCR negative3

Expected Course

+
  • Mortality rate: 20-50% for VAP, varies with patient factors1
  • Clinical improvement expected within 48-72 hours of appropriate therapy1
  • Poor prognostic factors: MDR pathogens, septic shock, ventilator support required, bacteremia1
  • Length of stay: Prolonged ICU and hospital stay, especially with VAP2

Follow-up

+
  • Clinical reassessment at 48-72 hours for response to therapy1,2
  • Repeat cultures if no clinical improvement1
  • Consider alternative diagnoses if no response after 72 hours1
  • Chest imaging to assess resolution (may lag clinical improvement)1
  • Surveillance cultures in high-risk units for MDR organisms2

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Diagnostic Approach

  • Clinical diagnosis when cultures unavailable — fever, infiltrate, purulent secretions1
  • Gram stain of respiratory secretions to guide initial therapy1

Treatment Options

  • Empiric broad-spectrum therapy based on local resistance patterns1
  • Amoxicillin-clavulanate plus fluoroquinolone for lower-risk patients1

Relevant Clinical Scores

qSOFA (Quick Sepsis-Related Organ Failure Assessment)

qSOFA (Quick Sepsis-Related Organ Failure Assessment) 0
Low risk. qSOFA negative. Continue standard monitoring.

STRONG-HF — Rapid Up-Titration After Acute HF

Lancet • 2022 • Mebazaa et al.
Read on WikiJournalClub →

Clinical Question

Does rapid up-titration of guideline-directed medical therapy (GDMT) before discharge reduce cardiovascular death or heart failure rehospitalization compared to usual care in patients with acute heart failure?

Study Design

Multicenter, open-label, randomized controlled trial conducted across 14 countries in Europe, Asia, and Australia. Patients were randomized 1:1 to high-intensity care with rapid GDMT up-titration or usual care within 24-72 hours of clinical stabilization.

Population

1,078 patients hospitalized for acute heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF ≤40%) who were clinically stabilized. Key exclusions: systolic BP <100 mmHg, eGFR <20 mL/min/1.73m², severe valve disease, or planned cardiac intervention. Median age 65 years, 75% male, median LVEF 25%.

Intervention

High-intensity care with rapid up-titration of ACE inhibitors/ARBs, beta-blockers, and MRAs to maximum tolerated evidence-based doses before discharge, supported by close outpatient follow-up including telemedicine and biomarker monitoring (NT-proBNP).

Comparator / Control

Usual care with standard discharge planning and routine outpatient follow-up, allowing physicians to optimize GDMT at their discretion according to local practice patterns.

Primary Endpoint

Composite of 180-day heart failure rehospitalization or cardiovascular death, analyzed as time-to-first-event.

Key Findings

Primary endpoint occurred in 15.8% (85/537) in the high-intensity group vs 23.3% (126/541) in usual care (HR 0.66, 95% CI 0.50-0.86, p=0.002). ARR = 7.5%, RRR = 32%, NNT = 13. 180-day cardiovascular death: 4.1% vs 8.3% (HR 0.47, 95% CI 0.27-0.81). HF rehospitalization: 12.3% vs 16.6% (HR 0.73, 95% CI 0.53-1.00). At 90 days, 55% of intervention patients achieved target doses vs 2% in usual care.

Strengths

Well-designed pragmatic trial with robust primary endpoint, international scope enhancing generalizability, high protocol adherence, comprehensive GDMT optimization strategy, and clinically meaningful effect size with low NNT of 13.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Open-label design introducing potential bias, relatively short 180-day follow-up, exclusion of patients with borderline hemodynamics limiting real-world applicability, and potential healthcare system differences affecting implementation across countries.

Practice Impact

Established rapid GDMT up-titration as standard of care for stable acute HF patients before discharge. Influenced 2023 ACC/AHA/HFSA heart failure guidelines emphasizing early optimization and close follow-up. Changed discharge practices to prioritize medication titration over early discharge.

Why It Matters Now

Provides evidence-based framework for HF discharge protocols and medication optimization strategies. Supports value-based care models focusing on reducing readmissions. Demonstrates importance of structured follow-up and telemedicine in HF management, particularly relevant for post-pandemic care delivery models.
