2 Minute Medicine • Fri, 06 Mar 2026 16:00:09 +000
Based on: PubMed abstract
Placebo may be inferior compared to acetaminophen when given with morphine for the management of acute pain in the emergency department
Understanding optimal pain management combinations in emergency settings is crucial given the ongoing opioid crisis and need for effective multimodal analgesia.
Design: Prospective cohort study comparing patients who received intravenous acetaminophen with opioids versus those who received opioids alone • Outcome: Opioid requirement measured in Morphine Equivalent Units during Emergency Department stay
Population: Adult Emergency Department patients aged 18 years and older treated with opioids for painful conditions
Intervention: Intravenous acetaminophen plus opioids
Comparator: Opioids alone
Key Results
  • No significant difference in opioid consumption during ED stay between groups (10.0 MEU vs 10.0 MEU, p=0.53)
  • Trend toward decreased opioid requirement at 24h post-discharge (15.0 MEU vs 30.0 MEU, p=0.059)
  • Both groups achieved similar pain reduction (median NRS from 9.0 to 4.0) and patient satisfaction (>80%)
  • Nine minor adverse events recorded, equally distributed between groups
Clinical Interpretation
  • IV acetaminophen did not provide opioid-sparing effects during ED stay, contrary to expectations for multimodal analgesia
  • The trend toward reduced post-discharge opioid use suggests potential delayed benefits
  • Both approaches achieved similar pain control and patient satisfaction
Limitations
  • Single-center study
  • Non-randomized design
  • Relatively small sample size (116 patients)
Bottom Line: IV acetaminophen does not reduce opioid requirements during ED stay but may decrease post-discharge opioid use.
High confidence 📊 Informative

ROCKET AF — Rivaroxaban in Atrial Fibrillation

NEJM • 2011 • Patel et al. / ROCKET AF Steering Committee

Clinical Question

Is rivaroxaban noninferior to warfarin for stroke and systemic embolism prevention in patients with nonvalvular atrial fibrillation at moderate-to-high stroke risk?

Study Design

Multicenter, randomized, double-blind, double-dummy, noninferiority trial conducted across 1,178 sites in 45 countries. Patients randomized 1:1 to rivaroxaban 20mg daily (15mg if CrCl 30-49 mL/min) versus dose-adjusted warfarin (target INR 2.0-3.0). Event-driven trial with median follow-up of 707 days.

Population

N=14,264 patients with nonvalvular atrial fibrillation and moderate-to-high stroke risk (CHADS2 score ≥2). Mean age 73 years, 40% female, mean CHADS2 score 3.5. Key exclusions: mechanical heart valves, hemodynamically significant mitral stenosis, conditions increasing bleeding risk, CrCl <30 mL/min.

Intervention

Rivaroxaban 20mg once daily (15mg daily if creatinine clearance 30-49 mL/min) plus warfarin placebo

Comparator / Control

Dose-adjusted warfarin (target INR 2.0-3.0) plus rivaroxaban placebo. Warfarin group achieved mean time in therapeutic range (TTR) of 55%.

Primary Endpoint

Composite of stroke (ischemic or hemorrhagic) and systemic embolism analyzed in the per-protocol population for noninferiority (margin 1.46)

Key Findings

Primary endpoint: Rivaroxaban 1.7% vs warfarin 2.2% per year (HR 0.79, 95% CI 0.66-0.96, p<0.001 for noninferiority, p=0.12 for superiority). ARR 0.5% per year, RRR 21%, NNT 200 per year. Major bleeding similar: 3.6% vs 3.4% per year (HR 1.04, 95% CI 0.90-1.20, p=0.58). Critical bleeding reduction: 0.5% vs 0.7% per year (HR 0.69, 95% CI 0.46-1.05, p=0.08). Intracranial hemorrhage: 0.5% vs 0.7% per year (HR 0.67, 95% CI 0.47-0.93, p=0.02). Fatal bleeding: 0.2% vs 0.5% per year (HR 0.50, 95% CI 0.31-0.79, p=0.003).

Strengths

Large international trial with robust double-blind design. Included higher-risk AF population (mean CHADS2 3.5) than other DOAC trials. Fixed-dose rivaroxaban simplified dosing compared to warfarin monitoring. Comprehensive safety evaluation including bleeding subtypes.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Suboptimal warfarin control (TTR 55% vs >65% in RE-LY and ARISTOTLE). Open-label warfarin run-in period may have selected warfarin-tolerant patients. Higher baseline stroke risk population limits generalizability. Per-protocol analysis for primary endpoint may overestimate benefit.

Practice Impact

Established rivaroxaban as effective alternative to warfarin for AF stroke prevention, particularly in patients with poor INR control or warfarin intolerance. Contributed to 2014 AHA/ACC/HRS AF guidelines recommending DOACs over warfarin for most patients. Simplified anticoagulation management with fixed dosing.

Why It Matters Now

Remains foundational evidence for rivaroxaban use in AF. Real-world studies confirm effectiveness despite lower TTR in trial. Current guidelines favor DOACs as first-line therapy. Ongoing relevance for patients requiring twice-daily dosing alternative or those with renal impairment requiring dose adjustment.

Iron Deficiency Anemia

Last verified: March 13, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Fatigue and dyspnea on exertion1
  • Nonspecific complaints often presenting as most common symptoms1
  • Clinical context: Weakness, shortness of breath with activity
  • Pica and pagophagia (ice cravings)4
  • Clinical context: Restless leg syndrome, irritability

Exam Findings

  • Pallor of mucous membranes1
  • Koilonychia (spoon-shaped nails)10
  • Glossitis (inflammation of tongue) and cheilitis2
  • Clinical context: Tachycardia, altered mental status with severe anemia
  • Hypothermia and increased infection risk4

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Thalassemia trait — normal/high RBC count, target cells, basophilic stippling2
  • Anemia of chronic disease — functional iron deficiency with adequate but sequestered stores1
  • Lead poisoning — basophilic stippling, ringed sideroblasts in bone marrow2

Less Common

  • Sideroblastic anemia — elevated serum iron and transferrin with ringed sideroblasts2
  • IRIDA (iron refractory iron deficiency anemia) due to TMPRSS6 mutation3
  • SLC11A2 mutation — rare inherited condition3

Mimics

  • Fibromyalgia, chronic fatigue syndrome — similar fatigue presentation3
  • Depression/mood disorders — overlapping symptoms of fatigue and weakness3
  • Hypothyroidism — can present with similar fatigue pattern3

Diagnostic Tests

+

Initial Laboratory Assessment1

  • Complete Blood Count: microcytic, hypochromic red blood cells1
  • Serum ferritin < 30 ng/mL (or < 100 ng/mL if transferrin saturation < 20%)7
  • Transferrin saturation < 20% indicates iron deficiency7

Iron Studies2

  • Decreased serum iron with increased TIBC and transferrin levels2
  • Elevated soluble transferrin receptor2
  • Clinical context: Note ferritin can be elevated in inflammation despite iron deficiency

Morphology Assessment2

  • Corrected reticulocyte count = percent reticulocytes x (patient's HCT/normal HCT)2
  • Peripheral smear shows microcytosis and hypochromia1

Treatment

+

Outpatient Management7

  • Oral iron 100-200 mg elemental iron daily divided in 2 doses7
  • Ferrous sulfate (20% elemental iron), ferrous gluconate (12% elemental), ferrous fumarate (33% elemental)4
  • Continue for ≥ 3 months after normalization to restore iron stores2

Intravenous Iron Therapy7

  • Indicated for severe deficiency, oral intolerance, or impaired absorption7
  • Iron dextran, iron sucrose, ferric carboxymaltose available formulations8
  • Most common side effect: hypotension7

Special Populations3

  • Pregnancy: oral iron first trimester, IV iron for severe anemia or malabsorption3
  • IBD patients: IV iron preferable due to absorption issues3
  • Bariatric surgery: IV iron often required3

Expected Course

+
  • Nutritional deficiencies have good prognosis if treated early and adequately2
  • Iron stores must be restored for 3+ months after hemoglobin normalization2
  • Untreated anemia can lead to multiorgan failure and death2
  • Clinical context: Hemoglobin typically rises 1-2 g/dL per month with adequate therapy

Follow-up

+
  • Monitor CBC and iron studies at 4-6 weeks after initiating therapy2
  • Continue iron replacement for minimum 3 months after normalization2
  • Investigate underlying cause especially in men >55 and postmenopausal women2
  • Clinical context: Consider GI evaluation if no obvious source of blood loss identified

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Dietary Interventions6

  • Heme iron sources: meats, poultry, seafood have higher bioavailability6
  • Non-heme iron sources: beans, nuts, dark chocolate, spinach, fortified grains6
  • Vitamin C enhances iron absorption when taken with iron-rich foods

Prevention Strategies6

  • Iron fortification of foods for at-risk populations6
  • Address underlying causes of blood loss when possible
  • Education on dietary sources and absorption enhancers
