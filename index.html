  • Only univariate trends identified for some presentations due to small sample sizes
Bottom Line: CT angiography of head and neck has highest yield in stroke codes with abnormal CT, focal deficits, or new symptoms, and can be guided by specific clinical predictors.

ESCAPE — Pulmonary Artery Catheter in Decompensated HF

JAMA • 2005 • Binanay et al. / NHLBI Heart Failure Clinical Research Network

Clinical Question

Does pulmonary artery catheter-guided therapy improve clinical outcomes compared to clinical assessment alone in patients hospitalized with severe symptomatic heart failure?

Study Design

Multicenter, randomized, controlled trial. Open-label design with randomization to PAC-guided therapy vs. clinical assessment alone. Conducted at 26 sites across the United States. No blinding due to nature of intervention.

Population

433 patients hospitalized with severe symptomatic heart failure (NYHA Class IV or Class III with recent deterioration). Key inclusion: symptoms at rest or minimal exertion, at least one sign of congestion, systolic BP <125 mmHg or on vasodilators. Exclusions: recent MI, severe valvular disease, anticipated discharge within 3 days.

Intervention

Pulmonary artery catheter insertion with therapy guided by hemodynamic parameters (n=215). Goals included PCWP 15-18 mmHg and maintenance of adequate cardiac output and perfusion pressure.

Comparator / Control

Clinical assessment alone without invasive hemodynamic monitoring (n=218). Therapy guided by clinical signs, symptoms, and standard non-invasive measures.

Primary Endpoint

Days alive and out of hospital during first 6 months after randomization.

Key Findings

No significant difference in primary endpoint: PAC group 133±1.8 days vs control 135±1.9 days, p=0.35. 6-month mortality: PAC 35% vs control 31%, HR 1.26 (95% CI 0.78-1.29), p=0.49. Hospital length of stay similar: 8.7±0.8 vs 8.3±0.8 days, p=0.27. Increased adverse events in PAC group: 21.9% vs 11.5%, p=0.04, ARR 10.4%, NNH=10. More days on IV therapy in PAC group: 4.2 vs 3.0 days, p=0.04.

Strengths

Well-designed multicenter RCT addressing important clinical question. Adequate sample size with good follow-up. Pragmatic real-world study design. Clear primary endpoint. Standardized protocols for both arms.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Open-label design introduced potential bias. Primary endpoint composite and difficult to interpret clinically. Relatively short follow-up period. Did not stratify by heart failure etiology or severity. Learning curve effects for PAC management not addressed.

Practice Impact

Led to dramatic decline in PAC use in heart failure management. Challenged routine use of invasive hemodynamic monitoring. Shifted focus toward clinical assessment and non-invasive monitoring. Influenced heart failure guidelines to recommend against routine PAC use.

Why It Matters Now

Established foundation for evidence-based approach to heart failure monitoring. Relevant as newer devices (CardioMEMS) show benefits through different mechanisms. Demonstrates importance of validating invasive monitoring strategies. Continues to inform discussions about hemodynamic monitoring in advanced heart failure and cardiogenic shock.

Hypercalcemia

Last verified: March 18, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Neurologic symptoms: Altered mental status, confusion, headache, depression1
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, constipation1
  • Musculoskeletal symptoms: Weakness, myalgias, arthralgias1
  • Genitourinary symptoms: Polyuria, polydipsia, nocturia1
  • Severe cases: Coma and cardiac arrhythmias, death1
  • Classic symptoms: "Bones, stones, abdominal moans, and psychic groans"2

Exam Findings

  • Cardiovascular findings: Hypertension, bradycardia1
  • Neurologic findings: Hyperreflexia, tongue fasciculations1
  • ECG changes: Shortened QT interval, T wave flattening or inversion, J waves, prolongation of the PR and QRS intervals1
  • Severe hypercalcemia (>15 mg/dL): Central nervous system dysfunction2

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Primary hyperparathyroidism: Accounts for 90% of cases with malignancy3
  • Malignancy-related hypercalcemia: Excessive production of parathyroid hormone-related protein (PTHrP)1
  • Parathyroid adenoma: Most common cause of primary hyperparathyroidism4
  • Osteolytic metastases: Metastatic disease to bone increasing osteoclast activity1

Less Common

  • Granulomatous diseases: Sarcoidosis and tuberculosis causing increased 1,25-dihydroxy vitamin D production1
  • Hematologic malignancies: Hodgkin or non-Hodgkin lymphoma1
  • Vitamin D toxicity: Increased levels of 25-hydroxy vitamin D1
  • Hyperthyroidism: Clinical context: Can cause hypercalcemia through increased bone resorption
  • Immobilization: Clinical context: Prolonged bed rest leading to bone demineralization

Mimics

  • Familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia (FHH): Genetic condition with mild hypercalcemia in asymptomatic individuals5
  • Milk-alkali syndrome: Third leading cause after malignancy and primary hyperparathyroidism6
  • Laboratory error/pseudohypercalcemia: Dehydration or hemoconcentration falsely elevating albumin levels7
  • Medication-induced: Thiazide diuretics and lithium3

Diagnostic Tests

+

Laboratory Studies1

  • Serum calcium (corrected for albumin): Mild (10-12 mg/dL), moderate (12-14 mg/dL), severe (>14 mg/dL)8
  • Ionized calcium: Should be measured when hypercalcemia suspected8
  • Parathyroid hormone (PTH): Should be checked to rule out hyperparathyroidism1
  • 25-hydroxyvitamin D: Clinical context: Essential to assess vitamin D status
  • 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D: Clinical context: Evaluate for granulomatous disease
  • PTH-related peptide (PTHrP): Most common cause of malignancy-related hypercalcemia8

Additional Tests1

  • 24-hour urine calcium: Differentiates PHPT from FHH; Ca/Cr clearance ratio <0.01 favors FHH, >0.02 supports PHPT9
  • Renal function: Check creatinine to evaluate for nephrogenic causes1
  • Thyroid function: TSH to evaluate endocrine origins1
  • Serum phosphate and magnesium: Consider electrolyte abnormalities1

Imaging Studies1

  • Radiographic evaluation: CT chest, breast imaging, kidney evaluation for malignancy1
  • Bone density scan (DXA): Evaluate bone mineral density at lumbar spine, hip, and distal radius9
  • Neck ultrasound: Clinical context: Identify parathyroid adenomas or thyroid pathology

Treatment

+

Acute Severe Hypercalcemia (ICU/Inpatient)10,11

  • IV normal saline: 2-4 L initial resuscitation, then maintenance
  • Zoledronic acid: 4 mg IV preferred over pamidronate6
  • Calcitonin: 4-8 units/kg SC/IM q12h for <72 hours (tachyphylaxis)12
  • Denosumab: 120 mg SC if bisphosphonate contraindicated or refractory12
  • Loop diuretics: Only after adequate volume repletion

Moderate Hypercalcemia (Inpatient Non-ICU)10,11

  • IV normal saline: 1-2 L then maintenance based on volume status
  • Bisphosphonate therapy: Zoledronic acid 4 mg IV, can repeat after 7 days6
  • Address underlying cause: Treat malignancy, discontinue offending medications
  • Denosumab alternative: 120 mg SC, repeat at 1, 2, 4 weeks then monthly6

Mild Hypercalcemia (Outpatient)1

  • Treat underlying condition: Address pathogenetic causes to manage serum calcium13
  • Discontinue contributing medications: Thiazides, lithium, calcium supplements, vitamin D
  • Adequate hydration: Encourage fluid intake
  • Monitor closely: Serial calcium, renal function, bone health assessments
  • Surgical consultation: For primary hyperparathyroidism management

Expected Course

+
  • Variable prognosis: HCM mortality declined with effective chemotherapy14
  • Treatment response: Bisphosphonates: 48-72 hours onset; Denosumab: 3-10 days onset6
  • Primary hyperparathyroidism: Parathyroidectomy achieves >95% cure rates in experienced centers9
  • FHH: Usually benign, lacks severe long-term consequences5

Follow-up

+
  • Serial monitoring: Serum calcium, renal function, PTH levels as clinically indicated
  • Bone health assessment: DXA scanning for bone density monitoring9
  • Malignancy surveillance: Ongoing oncology care for cancer-related hypercalcemia
  • Medication review: Reassess need for calcium-affecting medications
  • Specialist referral: Endocrinology for complex cases, surgery evaluation for hyperparathyroidism

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Essential Diagnostics

  • Serum calcium (total, corrected for albumin if available)
  • Basic chemistry panel: Creatinine, electrolytes, phosphate
  • PTH level: If available, helps differentiate PTH-mediated vs non-PTH causes

Treatment Priorities

  • IV fluid resuscitation: Normal saline as first-line therapy
  • Loop diuretics: If bisphosphonates unavailable (after volume repletion)
  • Prednisolone: 40-60 mg daily for granulomatous disease or lymphoma
  • Discontinue offending agents: Calcium, vitamin D supplements, thiazides
