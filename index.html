Based on: PubMed abstract
Delaying pegfilgrastim administration reduces bone pain in breast cancer patients
Pegfilgrastim-induced bone pain is a common side effect that can lead to treatment discontinuation and compromised chemotherapy effectiveness.
Design: Phase III double-blind placebo-controlled randomized clinical trial • Outcome: Area under the curve (AUC) for pain for days 1 through 5
Population: 510 patients at 17 sites; mean age 55.6 years, 86% female, 68% with breast cancer, 10% with lung cancer
Intervention: Naproxen 500 mg twice daily
Comparator: Placebo
Key Results
  • Mean AUC for pain was 7.71 for placebo vs 6.04 for naproxen group (P = 0.037)
  • Naproxen reduced maximum pain from 3.40 to 2.59 (P = 0.005)
  • Pain incidence reduced from 71.3% to 61.1% (P = 0.020)
  • Pain duration reduced from 2.40 to 1.92 days (P = 0.009)
  • Severe pain (>5/10) reduced from 27.0% to 19.2% (P = 0.048)
  • Pain peaked at 3 days for both groups
Clinical Interpretation
  • Prophylactic naproxen provides modest but clinically meaningful reduction in pegfilgrastim-induced bone pain
  • The intervention is safe and practical for routine clinical use
Limitations
  • Risk factors for pain could not be identified
  • Single-agent prophylaxis tested
  • Limited to specific naproxen dosing regimen
Bottom Line: Prophylactic naproxen 500 mg twice daily significantly reduces pegfilgrastim-induced bone pain incidence and severity.

IMPACT — Triple Therapy in COPD

NEJM • 2018 • Lipson DA, et al.

Clinical Question

In patients with COPD, does once-daily single-inhaler triple therapy (inhaled glucocorticoid, LAMA, and LABA) decrease moderate or severe exacerbations vs once-daily single-inhaler dual therapy (either inhaled glucocorticoid-LABA or LABA-LAMA)?

Study Design

Multicenter, double-blind, parallel-group, randomized phase 3 trial conducted in 37 countries between June 2014 through July 2017. 52-week follow-up with intention-to-treat analysis. Patients used electronic diaries to log symptoms daily, with investigators notified if worsening symptoms occurred over 2 days.

Population

N=10,355 patients ≥40 years with symptomatic COPD (CAT score ≥10). Required either FEV1 <50% predicted with ≥1 moderate/severe exacerbation in previous year, OR FEV1 50-80% predicted with ≥2 moderate or ≥1 severe exacerbation in previous year. Mean age 65.3 years, 34% female, mean FEV1 45.5% predicted, mean CAT score 20.1. Excluded asthma-only patients, active pulmonary diseases, recent exacerbations, and high-risk pneumonia patients.

Intervention

Triple therapy: Fluticasone Furoate-Umeclidinium-Vilanterol (FF-UMEC-VI) delivered via ELLIPTA dry powder inhaler (n=4,151)

Comparator / Control

Two dual therapy arms: Fluticasone Furoate-Vilanterol (FF-VI, n=4,134) or Umeclidinium-Vilanterol (UMEC-VI, n=2,070), both delivered via ELLIPTA dry powder inhaler

Primary Endpoint

Annual rate of moderate or severe COPD exacerbations during treatment period (moderate = requiring antibiotics or systemic corticosteroids; severe = requiring hospitalization or resulting in death)

Key Findings

Triple therapy reduced annual exacerbation rate vs dual therapy: 0.91 vs 1.07 events/year (FF-VI comparison, rate ratio 0.85, 95% CI 0.80-0.90, 15% difference, P<0.001) and vs 1.21 events/year (UMEC-VI comparison, rate ratio 0.75, 95% CI 0.70-0.81, 25% difference, P<0.001). Triple therapy improved trough FEV1 by 94mL vs -3mL with FF-VI and 40mL with UMEC-VI. However, higher pneumonia incidence occurred with inhaled glucocorticoid groups, especially triple therapy.

Strengths

Large, well-powered multicenter RCT with robust design. Double-blinding with identical delivery devices. Real-world electronic symptom monitoring. Intention-to-treat analysis. Addressed important clinical question about step-up therapy to triple therapy from dual bronchodilator therapy - the first study to do so systematically.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Higher pneumonia risk with inhaled corticosteroids, particularly in triple therapy group. Included some asthma-COPD overlap patients which may confound results. See source for details on other limitations and criticisms.

Practice Impact

This was the first major trial supporting step-up from dual bronchodilator therapy to triple therapy, providing evidence that influenced GOLD guidelines. Previously, evidence for triple therapy escalation was lacking, and GOLD guidelines had specifically called for more research on this approach. The trial supported triple therapy for symptomatic COPD patients with exacerbation history.

Why It Matters Now

Established the evidence base for current COPD triple therapy recommendations in guidelines. The risk-benefit profile shown here (reduced exacerbations but increased pneumonia risk) continues to inform clinical decision-making. The trial's methodology using single-inhaler triple therapy became a model for subsequent COPD combination therapy studies.

Myasthenia Gravis Crisis

Last verified: March 28, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Respiratory failure — acute deterioration of respiratory muscle function requiring mechanical ventilation1,2
  • Bulbar weakness — dysphagia, dysarthria, difficulty managing secretions1,3
  • Rapidly progressive generalized weakness — especially proximal muscle groups and diaphragm1,3
  • Ocular symptoms — ptosis, diplopia (may be initial presentation)1
  • Aspiration risk — due to pharyngeal and laryngeal muscle weakness3

Exam Findings

  • Respiratory compromise — hypoxemia (PO2 < 8 kPa), hypercapnia (PCO2 > 6.65 kPa)4
  • Fatigable weakness — worse with repetitive muscle testing, improves with rest1
  • MuSK-positive patients — facial atrophy, triple-furrowed tongue, prominent bulbar involvement5
  • Normal reflexes — preserved deep tendon reflexes (distinguishes from other neuromuscular disorders)1
  • Normal sensation — no sensory deficits (helps distinguish from Guillain-Barré syndrome)1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Cholinergic crisis — from excessive anticholinesterase medication; fasciculations present2
  • Guillain-Barré syndrome — ascending paralysis, areflexia, sensory involvement2
  • Lambert-Eaton myasthenic syndrome — proximal weakness, autonomic dysfunction, reflexes improve with exercise2

Less Common

  • Botulism — foodborne, wound, or infantile; autonomic symptoms, descending paralysis2
  • Critical illness myopathy — ICU setting, prolonged paralysis after neuromuscular blockade2
  • Organophosphate poisoning — cholinergic toxicity, recent exposure history2

Mimics

  • Acute respiratory failure — from other causes (COPD, pneumonia, pulmonary edema)2
  • Conversion disorder — fluctuating symptoms, inconsistent exam findings2
  • Periodic paralysis — episodic weakness, family history, potassium abnormalities2

Diagnostic Tests

+

Antibody Testing

  • Anti-AChR antibodies — positive in ~85% of generalized MG patients1
  • Anti-MuSK antibodies — present in 5-10% of generalized MG; associated with severe bulbar weakness1
  • Anti-LRP4 antibodies — newer marker, less commonly tested1

Pulmonary Function

  • Vital capacity — <20 mL/kg or <1L indicates high risk for intubation6
  • Negative inspiratory force — <-30 cmH2O suggests respiratory muscle weakness6
  • Blood gas analysis — PO2 < 8 kPa and PCO2 > 6.65 kPa defines crisis4

Additional Studies

  • Chest CT — evaluate for thymoma (10-15% of MG patients)1
  • EMG/NCS — decremental response ≥10% at 3 Hz stimulation1
  • Edrophonium test — rarely used; can distinguish myasthenic vs. cholinergic crisis7

Treatment

+

ICU Management2,3

  • Immediate intubation — if vital capacity <15-20 mL/kg or clinical deterioration
  • Non-invasive ventilation — may be tried in stable patients without bulbar involvement
  • Aspiration precautions — NPO if significant bulbar weakness present
  • DVT prophylaxis — sequential compression devices, anticoagulation if appropriate

Rapid Immunotherapy (choose one)8,9

  • Plasmapheresis — 250-300 mL/kg over 5 sessions; faster onset (2-3 days)8,9
  • IVIG — 2 g/kg divided over 2-5 days (typically 400 mg/kg/day × 5 days)8,9
  • Clinical context: Plasmapheresis preferred for rapid response; IVIG if contraindications to PLEX
  • Clinical context: Equal long-term efficacy but PLEX may have faster short-term improvement

Immunosuppression5,10

  • Corticosteroids — delay initiation 2-3 days; start prednisone 20 mg daily, increase gradually5
  • Steroid warning — may initially worsen symptoms in 50% of patients within 5-10 days5
  • Continue prior immunosuppressants — azathioprine, mycophenolate, if already established10

Symptomatic Treatment1,5

  • Pyridostigmine — 30-60 mg PO q4-6h; avoid in MuSK-positive patients1,5
  • Neostigmine — 0.5-2.5 mg IV/IM q2-4h if unable to take oral medications1
  • MuSK-positive patients — avoid anticholinesterases; may cause fasciculations and worsen symptoms5

Expected Course

+
  • Mortality rate — reduced from 50-80% to ~4.5% with modern intensive care11
  • Plasmapheresis response — improvement typically seen by 2nd-3rd session5
  • IVIG response — improvement usually within 1 week, lasting 3-6 weeks12
  • Ventilator duration — median 7-14 days; longer in MuSK-positive patients4
  • ICU length of stay — typically 10-21 days depending on severity and complications4

Follow-up

+
  • Serial pulmonary function — vital capacity and NIF every 4-6 hours initially6
  • MG-specific scores — monitor with MGFA-QMG, MG-ADL scales13
  • Weaning parameters — VC >15 mL/kg, NIF >-30 cmH2O before extubation6
  • Thymectomy evaluation — consider in patients <60 years with generalized MG14
  • Long-term immunosuppression — optimize maintenance therapy to prevent future crises10

Low-Resource Considerations

+

When PLEX/IVIG unavailable

  • High-dose corticosteroids — methylprednisolone 1000 mg IV daily × 3-5 days
  • Supportive care — mechanical ventilation, infection prevention, nutritional support
  • Anticholinesterase optimization — maximize pyridostigmine dosing (avoid in MuSK+)

Prevention Focus

  • Trigger identification — treat infections promptly, avoid contraindicated medications
  • Medication compliance — ensure adherence to maintenance immunosuppression
  • Early recognition — educate patients on warning signs for prompt medical attention
