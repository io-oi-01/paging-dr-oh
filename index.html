SURMOUNT-1 — Tirzepatide for Obesity

NEJM • 2022 • Jastreboff et al.

Clinical Question

Is tirzepatide effective for weight management in adults with obesity or overweight without type 2 diabetes?

Study Design

Phase 3, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial conducted at 119 sites across 9 countries. Participants were randomized 1:1:1:1 to placebo or tirzepatide 5mg, 10mg, or 15mg weekly subcutaneous injections with dose escalation over 20 weeks and 52 weeks of treatment with lifestyle intervention.

Population

Adults with BMI ≥30 kg/m² or ≥27 kg/m² with weight-related complication, excluding diabetes (HbA1c <6.5%). Mean age 45 years, 75% female, mean baseline weight 104.8 kg, mean BMI 38.0 kg/m². N=2,539 participants.

Intervention

Tirzepatide 5mg, 10mg, or 15mg subcutaneous weekly injections plus lifestyle intervention (reduced-calorie diet and increased physical activity).

Comparator / Control

Matching placebo subcutaneous weekly injections plus lifestyle intervention.

Primary Endpoint

Percentage change in body weight from baseline to week 72 and proportion achieving ≥5% weight reduction.

Key Findings

Mean weight reduction: placebo -2.4% vs tirzepatide 5mg -15.0% (difference -12.6%, 95% CI -13.4 to -11.9, p<0.001), 10mg -19.5% (difference -17.1%, 95% CI -17.9 to -16.3, p<0.001), 15mg -22.5% (difference -20.1%, 95% CI -20.8 to -19.3, p<0.001). Weight loss ≥5%: placebo 35% vs 5mg 85% (ARR 50%, NNT=2), 10mg 89% (ARR 54%, NNT=2), 15mg 91% (ARR 56%, NNT=2). Weight loss ≥20%: placebo 1.3% vs 15mg 57% (ARR 55.7%, NNT=2).

Strengths

Large sample size, robust randomization, excellent retention (88.5% completed), clinically meaningful primary endpoints, dose-response relationship demonstrated, comprehensive safety monitoring, and standardized lifestyle intervention across all arms.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Limited racial/ethnic diversity (76% white), short 72-week duration for chronic obesity management, high discontinuation due to GI adverse events (16.1% vs 3.1% placebo), cost considerations not addressed, and unknown long-term cardiovascular outcomes.

Practice Impact

Led to FDA approval of tirzepatide (Zepbound) for chronic weight management in May 2023. Established new benchmark for pharmacological obesity treatment with unprecedented weight loss magnitude. Changed treatment algorithms by providing option approaching surgical weight loss efficacy.

Why It Matters Now

With obesity affecting >40% of US adults, tirzepatide provides the most effective non-surgical weight loss option available. Ongoing SURMOUNT program trials are evaluating long-term safety, cardiovascular outcomes, and effectiveness in diabetes prevention, potentially expanding clinical applications beyond current obesity treatment paradigm.

Diabetic Ketoacidosis

Last verified: April 18, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain (common early symptoms)1
  • Polyuria and polydipsia: Osmotic diuresis from hyperglycemia1
  • Unintentional weight loss: Due to dehydration and catabolism1
  • Fatigue and weakness: From metabolic derangements1
  • Altered mental status: Ranging from confusion to coma in severe cases1

Exam Findings

  • Kussmaul respirations: Deep, labored breathing to compensate for metabolic acidosis1
  • Fruity breath odor: From acetone accumulation1
  • Dehydration signs: Dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor, hypotension1
  • Tachycardia: Compensatory response to dehydration1
  • Abdominal tenderness: May mimic acute abdomen1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Alcoholic ketoacidosis: β-hydroxybutyrate:acetoacetate ratio up to 8:1 vs 3:1 in DKA, glucose rarely >250 mg/dL2
  • Starvation ketoacidosis: Dietary intake <500 kcal/day, normal or low glucose3
  • Hyperglycemic hyperosmolar state: Glucose >600 mg/dL, minimal ketosis, pH >7.33

Less Common

  • Lactic acidosis: Often with sepsis/dehydration, absence of ketonemia3
  • Uremic acidosis: Acute/chronic kidney disease with metabolic acidosis3
  • Salicylate poisoning: Mixed respiratory alkalosis and metabolic acidosis3

Mimics

  • Toxic alcohol ingestion: Methanol, ethylene glycol causing high anion gap acidosis3
  • Ketosis of pregnancy: Hyperemesis gravidarum with ketone formation3
  • Acute pancreatitis: May present with abdominal pain and hyperglycemia1

Diagnostic Tests

+

2024 ADA Diagnostic Criteria (All 3 Required)3

  • Hyperglycemia: ≥200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L) or history of diabetes regardless of glucose3
  • Ketonemia: β-hydroxybutyrate ≥3.0 mmol/L or urine ketones ≥2+3
  • Metabolic acidosis: pH <7.3 and/or bicarbonate <18 mmol/L3

Laboratory Tests1

  • Blood glucose: Usually >250 mg/dL (may be normal in euglycemic DKA)1
  • Anion gap: Typically >12 mEq/L1
  • Arterial blood gas: pH <7.3, bicarbonate <15 mEq/L1
  • Electrolytes: Check sodium, potassium, chloride, phosphate1

Ketone Measurement1

  • Blood β-hydroxybutyrate: Preferred method, real-time assessment1
  • Urine ketones: Less accurate, reflects past concentrations1

Treatment

+

Emergency Department/ICU3

  • Fluid resuscitation: 0.9% NaCl 1L/hour initially, replace 50% deficit in 8-12 hours3
  • Insulin therapy: Regular insulin 0.1 U/kg/hour IV continuous infusion3
  • Potassium replacement: 20-30 mmol/L IV fluid when K+ <5.0 mmol/L, delay insulin if K+ <3.5 mmol/L3
  • Dextrose: Add 5-10% dextrose when glucose <250 mg/dL to prevent hypoglycemia3

Step-Down Unit (Mild-Moderate DKA)3

  • Subcutaneous insulin: May use rapid-acting insulin every 2 hours for uncomplicated cases3
  • Close monitoring: Blood glucose and ketones every 2-4 hours3
  • Fluid management: Oral fluids if tolerated, IV as needed3

Resolution Criteria3

  • Glucose: <200 mg/dL3
  • Bicarbonate: ≥15 mmol/L3
  • pH: >7.33
  • β-hydroxybutyrate: <3.0 mmol/L3

Expected Course

+
  • Mortality: <1% in experienced centers, higher in elderly and with comorbidities1
  • Resolution time: Usually 12-24 hours with appropriate treatment1
  • Complications: Cerebral edema (rare in adults), hypoglycemia, hypokalemia1
  • Precipitating factors: Infection (40%), insulin non-adherence (25%), new-onset diabetes (15%)1

Follow-up

+
  • Diabetes education: Insulin management, sick-day rules, when to check ketones1
  • Endocrinology referral: For diabetes management optimization1
  • SGLT2 inhibitor counseling: Risk of euglycemic DKA, ketone monitoring1
  • Mental health screening: Address potential intentional insulin omission1

Low-Resource Considerations

+

When IV Pumps Unavailable2

  • Subcutaneous insulin: Rapid-acting insulin every 2 hours for mild DKA2
  • Urine ketones: If blood ketone measurement unavailable2

Limited Laboratory Access1

  • Bedside glucose/ketone meter: Point-of-care testing essential1
  • Clinical assessment: Monitor mental status, vital signs, urine output1

Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis

Last verified: April 18, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Fever, chills, malaise — present in majority of patients1
  • Abdominal pain or discomfort — occurs in ~50% of patients but usually not severe or focal1
  • Altered mental status — hepatic encephalopathy may be precipitated1
  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, ileus — gastrointestinal symptoms common1
  • Asymptomatic presentation — approximately 30% of patients have no symptoms at diagnosis1
  • Dyspnea, early satiety — from worsening ascites2

Exam Findings

  • Abdominal distension — from underlying ascites2
  • Shifting dullness, fluid wave — signs of ascites2
  • Absence of peritoneal signs — unlike secondary bacterial peritonitis3
  • Stigmata of chronic liver disease — spider angiomata, palmar erythema, jaundice2
  • Hepatosplenomegaly — from portal hypertension2
  • Signs of decompensated cirrhosis — ascites, encephalopathy1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Secondary bacterial peritonitis — from perforated viscus or surgical source3
  • Culture-negative neutrocytic ascites — same clinical significance as SBP3
  • Hepatic encephalopathy — may present with altered mental status alone1
  • Hepatorenal syndrome — may be precipitated by or coexist with SBP4

Less Common

  • Tuberculous peritonitis — requires acid-fast staining and PCR5
  • Peritoneal carcinomatosis — cytology needed for diagnosis5
  • Pancreatitis with ascites — elevated ascitic amylase3
  • Spontaneous bacterial empyema — pleural equivalent of SBP6

Mimics

  • Empyema with diaphragmatic irritation — can mimic peritoneal irritation1
  • Cystitis or pyelonephritis — peritoneum-adjacent pain1
  • Acute-on-chronic liver failure — systemic inflammation without infection7
  • Portal hypertensive gastropathy bleeding — may coexist with SBP1

Diagnostic Tests

+

Ascitic Fluid Analysis1,3

  • Neutrophil count ≥250 cells/μL — most sensitive indicator for SBP diagnosis1
  • Ascitic fluid culture — inoculate into blood culture bottles at bedside, positive in ~60%3
  • Total protein <1.0 g/dL — associated with increased SBP risk3
  • SAAG >1.1 g/dL — confirms portal hypertension as cause of ascites1

Laboratory Studies1

  • Complete blood count — leukocytosis common but not universal1
  • Comprehensive metabolic panel — assess renal and hepatic function1
  • Lactate level — marker of tissue perfusion and sepsis severity1
  • Blood cultures — positive in ~75% of culture-positive SBP cases3

Imaging Studies3

  • CT abdomen/pelvis — to exclude secondary bacterial peritonitis if suspected3
  • Abdominal ultrasound — assess ascites volume and guide paracentesis5

Treatment

+

First-Line Antibiotic Therapy6,8

  • Cefotaxime 2 g IV every 8 hours — preferred first-line agent6,8
  • Ceftriaxone 2 g IV daily — alternative first-line option8
  • Duration: 5 days minimum — based on clinical response8

Alternative Antibiotics8,9

  • Ofloxacin 400 mg PO twice daily — for selected low-risk patients9
  • Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g IV every 6 hours — if resistant organisms suspected8
  • Meropenem 1 g IV every 8 hours — for MDR organisms or treatment failure8

Adjunctive Therapy6,8

  • Albumin 1.5 g/kg IV on day 1 — reduces risk of hepatorenal syndrome8
  • Albumin 1.0 g/kg IV on day 3 — improves survival in SBP8
  • Hold diuretics during acute episode — prevent further renal impairment8

Secondary Prophylaxis8

  • Norfloxacin 400 mg PO daily — after first SBP episode8
  • Ciprofloxacin 750 mg PO weekly — alternative prophylactic regimen8
  • Consider in high-risk patients — ascitic protein <1.5 g/dL8

Expected Course

+
  • Mortality rate: 30-70% — in patients with end-stage liver disease2
  • Clinical response expected within 48-72 hours — with appropriate antibiotic therapy8
  • Repeat paracentesis in 48 hours — if no clinical improvement8
  • High recurrence risk — ~70% develop recurrent SBP within 1 year without prophylaxis8
  • Associated complications — hepatorenal syndrome in ~30%, hepatic encephalopathy4

Follow-up

+
  • Repeat paracentesis if treatment failure — neutrophil count should decrease by 25%8
  • Monitor for hepatorenal syndrome — creatinine rise >50% or >133 μmol/L4
  • Liver transplant evaluation — SBP indicates decompensated cirrhosis7
  • Long-term antibiotic prophylaxis — consider in survivors of first episode8
  • Regular monitoring — CBC, metabolic panel, liver function tests2

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Diagnostic Adaptations

  • Manual cell count if automated unavailable — focus on neutrophil predominance3
  • Gram stain — may help guide initial therapy when culture unavailable3
  • Clinical diagnosis — treat empirically if high suspicion and paracentesis not possible1

Treatment Modifications

  • Ampicillin-sulbactam 3 g IV every 6 hours — if cephalosporins unavailable8
  • Oral fluoroquinolone alternatives — if IV access limited9
  • Normal saline for volume expansion — if albumin not available8
