ARDS

Last verified: March 11, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Dyspnea: Common presenting symptom, usually mild initially, escalating to severe within 12 to 24 hours requiring mechanical ventilation1
  • Progressive respiratory distress: Dyspnea and hypoxemia progressively worsening within 6 to 72 hours of inciting event1
  • Acute onset: Symptoms start within seven days of the inciting event1

Exam Findings

  • Hypoxemia: PaO2/FiO2 ratio < 300 mm Hg; classified as mild (200-300), moderate (100-200), or severe (≤100 mm Hg)1
  • Bilateral pulmonary infiltrates: Bilateral lung infiltrates on chest radiography or CT scan of non-cardiac origin1
  • Non-compliant lungs: Characterized by poor oxygenation and non-compliant or "stiff" lungs1
  • Pulmonary hypertension: Varying degrees of pulmonary artery vasoconstriction and may develop pulmonary hypertension1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Cardiogenic pulmonary edema: Assessment of left ventricular function crucial to differentiate from congestive heart failure1
  • Pneumonia: Pulmonary infection as risk factor1
  • Aspiration pneumonitis: Aspiration as extra-pulmonary source1

Less Common

  • Pulmonary embolism: Consider with acute hypoxemia1
  • Interstitial lung disease: May present with bilateral infiltrates1
  • Fat embolism: Fat embolism as extra-pulmonary trigger1

Mimics

  • Acute chest syndrome: In sickle cell disease with ARDS-like presentation1
  • High-altitude pulmonary edema: Non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema1
  • Transfusion-related acute lung injury (TRALI): Leading cause of death related to blood transfusion, 1 per 5000 units of packed RBCs1

Diagnostic Tests

+

Arterial Blood Gas1

  • PaO2/FiO2 ratio: Diagnostic criterion < 300 mm Hg with severity stratification1
  • A-a gradient calculation: Help determine if abnormality due to hypoventilation, shunt, V/Q mismatch, or impaired diffusion1

Imaging1

  • Chest X-ray: Bilateral lung infiltrates of non-cardiac origin required for diagnosis1
  • CT chest: Helpful for pneumothorax, pleural effusion, mediastinal lymphadenopathy, or barotrauma1

Cardiac Assessment1

  • Echocardiography: Assess left ventricular function to differentiate from cardiogenic causes1
  • Pulmonary artery catheter: Invasive hemodynamic assessment when indicated1

Treatment

+

ICU - Mechanical Ventilation (Lung Protective Strategy)2,3

  • Tidal volume: 4-8 mL/kg predicted body weight (target 6.8 ± 1.2 mL/kg PBW)2
  • Plateau pressure: Limit to < 30 cm H2O after 0.5-s inspiratory pause2
  • PEEP strategy: Higher PEEP without lung recruitment maneuvers for moderate-severe ARDS (4 trials, 1,162 patients; RR 0.77, 95% CI 0.60-0.96)3
  • Driving pressure: Monitor driving pressure (plateau pressure - PEEP) as predictor of outcome2

ICU - Positioning and Rescue Therapies2,3

  • Prone positioning: Moderate-severe ARDS for 16+ consecutive hours if PaO2/FiO2 < 150 mmHg after stabilization2 50% reduction in 28-day mortality in PROSEVA trial2
  • Neuromuscular blockade: Suggested for early severe ARDS (ATS conditional recommendation)3
  • Corticosteroids: Conditional recommendation for ARDS patients (moderate certainty)3
  • VV-ECMO: Severe ARDS with refractory hypoxemia at specialized centers (RR 0.76, 95% CI 0.60-0.95)3

ICU - Contraindicated Therapies2,3

  • High-frequency oscillatory ventilation: NOT recommended for moderate-severe ARDS3
  • Prolonged recruitment maneuvers: NOT recommended (≥ 35 cmH2O for ≥ 1 minute)3
  • Extracorporeal CO2 removal: NOT recommended (high level evidence)3

Expected Course

+
  • High mortality: ARDS carries high mortality with few effective therapeutic modalities1 Mortality rate 35-46%1
  • Severity-dependent outcomes: Mortality and ventilator-free days increase with severity classification1
  • Three-phase progression: Exudative (first 7-10 days), proliferative (repair phase), and fibrotic phase (associated with increased mortality)1
  • Ventilator dependence: Frequently requiring mechanical ventilation and ICU-level care1

Follow-up

+
  • Daily assessment: Monitor oxygenation, ventilator parameters, and compliance with lung-protective ventilation1
  • Prone positioning evaluation: Early evaluation of PaO2/FiO2 ratio to discuss neuromuscular blockade and prone positioning4
  • Liberation screening: Dead-space-to-tidal-volume ratio useful for extubation decisions in ventilated patients1
  • Long-term outcomes: Monitor for persistent respiratory impairment and functional recovery1

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Basic Supportive Care

  • Lung-protective ventilation: Low tidal volumes 4-8 mL/kg PBW shown to reduce mortality in ARDSNet trial5
  • PEEP optimization: PEEP identified as most efficacious intervention when ARDS first described5
  • Awake prone positioning: ESICM suggests awake prone to avoid intubation in COVID-19 patients, valuable for limited resource centers6

Resource Considerations

  • ECMO limitations: ECMO challenging in low-resource settings including Brazil where less available in public healthcare6
  • Conservative fluid management: Additional adjuvant strategy for specific clinical situations5

Sepsis and Septic Shock

Last verified: March 11, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Fever >38°C or hypothermia <36°C1
  • Chills and rigors (highly suggestive when present)1
  • Altered mental status (confusion, agitation, delirium)1
  • Dyspnea and increased respiratory rate1
  • Decreased urine output (oliguria)1
  • Clinical context: Nausea, vomiting, and abdominal pain may occur

Exam Findings

  • Tachycardia >90 bpm1
  • Tachypnea ≥22 breaths/min2
  • Hypotension (SBP ≤100 mmHg)2
  • Warm shock: warm extremities, flash capillary refill (<1 second), bounding pulses3
  • Cold shock: cool extremities, delayed capillary refill (>3 seconds), thready pulses3
  • Hyperthermia >38.3°C or hypothermia <36°C1
  • Source-specific findings (rash, lung consolidation, dysuria, peritonitis)4

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Hypovolemic shock — trauma, GI bleeding, severe dehydration
  • Cardiogenic shock — MI, CHF exacerbation, arrhythmias
  • Anaphylactic shock — drug or food allergy
  • Non-infectious SIRS — trauma, burns, pancreatitis

Less Common

  • Neurogenic shock — spinal cord injury
  • Obstructive shock — pulmonary embolism, tension pneumothorax
  • Toxic shock syndrome — S. aureus or streptococcal toxins
  • Adrenal crisis — steroid withdrawal or insufficiency

Mimics

  • Drug-induced shock — benzodiazepines, beta-blockers, CCBs5
  • Severe metabolic disorders — DKA, thyrotoxicosis
  • Massive transfusion reaction
  • Heat stroke — especially in elderly patients

Diagnostic Tests

+

Laboratory Studies6

  • Blood cultures (2 sets, aerobic/anaerobic) before antibiotics6
  • Lactate level — elevated ≥2 mmol/L suggests tissue hypoperfusion1
  • Procalcitonin: Sn 77%, Sp 79% for sepsis in critically ill patients7
  • Complete blood count with differential (bandemia >10%)1
  • Comprehensive metabolic panel (creatinine, liver enzymes, bilirubin)6

Scoring Systems2,8

  • qSOFA: RR ≥22, SBP ≤100, altered mental status; ≥2 points suggests poor outcomes2
  • SOFA score: increase ≥2 points indicates sepsis; SOFA >11 has Sn 100%, Sp 79%8
  • SIRS criteria: fever, tachycardia, tachypnea, leukocytosis; ≥2 criteria1

Source Identification6

  • Chest X-ray or CT for pneumonia6
  • Urinalysis and urine culture for UTI/pyelonephritis6
  • CT abdomen/pelvis for intra-abdominal infections6
  • Wound cultures from any suspected soft tissue infections6

Treatment

+

Initial Resuscitation (Hour-1 Bundle)9

  • Crystalloid fluid: 30 mL/kg IV within 3 hours for hypotension or lactate ≥4 mmol/L9
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour of recognition9
  • Vasopressors if hypotensive during or after fluid resuscitation (MAP ≥65 mmHg)9
  • Remeasure lactate if initially >2 mmol/L9

ICU Management10

  • Norepinephrine first-line vasopressor; can initiate peripherally10
  • Hydrocortisone 200 mg/day IV if ongoing vasopressor requirement10
  • Balanced crystalloids preferred over normal saline10
  • Central venous catheter for vasopressor infusion and monitoring3
  • Mechanical ventilation for respiratory failure10

Antimicrobial Therapy7

  • Empiric therapy: broad-spectrum within 1 hour9
  • Procalcitonin-guided duration: may reduce antibiotic exposure7
  • De-escalation based on culture results and clinical improvement6
  • Source control (drainage, debridement) as soon as possible11

Expected Course

+
  • Septic shock mortality: 40-50% hospital mortality2,12
  • Early recognition and treatment within 1 hour improves outcomes1
  • Organ dysfunction may progress despite appropriate treatment1
  • Recovery phase: often prolonged with persistent physical and cognitive impairments13
  • Clinical context: Multi-organ failure is the leading cause of death in non-coronary ICU patients

Follow-up

+
  • ICU discharge planning: include information about sepsis, treatments, and common impairments13
  • Post-sepsis care: follow-up with clinicians able to manage long-term sequelae13
  • Physical rehabilitation for functional impairments13
  • Cognitive assessment for post-sepsis syndrome13
  • Clinical context: Family involvement in goals-of-care discussions is important

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Basic Assessment

  • Vital signs monitoring with focus on fever, tachycardia, hypotension
  • Basic laboratory: CBC, blood cultures if available
  • Clinical scoring: qSOFA for risk assessment

Treatment Priorities

  • Early antibiotics — most critical intervention
  • IV fluid resuscitation with available crystalloids
  • Source control when feasible (drainage, debridement)

Acute Kidney Injury

Last verified: March 11, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Often asymptomatic in early stages1
  • Decreased urine output (oliguria < 0.5 mL/kg/h for ≥6 hours)1
  • Nausea, vomiting, malaise due to uremic toxins10
  • Fatigue, weakness and confusion10
  • Anorexia and headache10
  • Flank pain (may occur with acute interstitial nephritis)10

Exam Findings

  • Fluid overload: Edema, pulmonary crackles, elevated JVP1
  • Volume depletion signs: Hypotension, tachycardia, poor skin turgor4
  • Signs of underlying etiology: Fever, rash, lymphadenopathy10
  • Palpable bladder or enlarged prostate (post-renal causes)2
  • Skin rash and eosinophilia in drug-induced interstitial nephritis10

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Prerenal AKI: Hypovolemia, heart failure, sepsis, renal artery stenosis1,2
  • Acute tubular necrosis (ATN): Ischemic or nephrotoxic injury8
  • Post-renal obstruction: Urolithiasis, BPH, malignancy2
  • Drug-induced AKI: NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, aminoglycosides, contrast agents1

Less Common

  • Acute interstitial nephritis: Drug-induced, infection-related, autoimmune10
  • Acute glomerulonephritis: RPGN, post-infectious GN1
  • Hepatorenal syndrome in patients with liver disease9
  • Rhabdomyolysis with myoglobin-induced nephropathy1

Mimics

  • Chronic kidney disease with acute decompensation1
  • Pseudorenal failure: Massive muscle breakdown, high protein intake1
  • Laboratory error: Sample hemolysis, analytical interference1

Diagnostic Tests

+

Serum Markers1

  • Serum creatinine: Rise ≥0.3 mg/dL in 48h or ≥1.5x baseline in 7 days1
  • BUN: Elevated, BUN:Cr ratio >20:1 suggests prerenal4
  • BUN:Cr ratio ≤12 suggests acute interstitial nephritis10

Urine Studies1,4

  • Urine output: <0.5 mL/kg/h for ≥6 hours diagnostic1
  • Urinalysis: Proteinuria, hematuria, RBC/WBC casts1
  • Urine sodium: <20 mEq/L (prerenal), >40 mEq/L (intrinsic)4
  • Fractional excretion of sodium (FENa): <1% prerenal, >2% intrinsic4

Imaging1

  • Renal ultrasound: Rule out obstruction, assess kidney size1
  • CT without contrast: Evaluate for stones, masses1
  • Doppler ultrasound: Assess renal blood flow if vascular cause suspected1

Treatment

+

Prevention1

  • Volume optimization: Avoid hypovolemia and fluid overload1
  • Nephrotoxin avoidance: Hold ACEi/ARBs, NSAIDs during illness1
  • Contrast prevention: Pre/post-hydration with isotonic saline1

General Management1

  • Fluid balance: Isotonic crystalloids for hypovolemia1
  • Medication adjustment: Dose reduction for renally cleared drugs1
  • Electrolyte monitoring: Manage hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia1
  • Metabolic acidosis: Sodium bicarbonate if pH <7.21

Specific Therapies1,8,9

  • Prerenal AKI: Volume resuscitation, vasopressor support if needed4
  • Post-renal AKI: Urgent decompression (catheter, nephrostomy)2
  • Drug-induced AIN: Discontinue offending agent, consider steroids10
  • Hepatorenal syndrome: Vasoconstrictors (terlipressin), albumin9

Renal Replacement Therapy1

  • Indications: Severe hyperkalemia, fluid overload, uremia, acidosis1
  • Timing: Initiate when conservative management insufficient1
  • Modality: Continuous vs intermittent based on hemodynamic stability1

Expected Course

+
  • Recovery varies by etiology: Prerenal AKI recovers rapidly with treatment1
  • ATN recovery: May take days to weeks, often incomplete8
  • Mortality risk: 7% hospital admissions, up to 67% ICU patients develop AKI1
  • Long-term outcomes: Increased risk of CKD progression and cardiovascular events8
  • Complete recovery: Possible if cause reversible and treatment prompt5

Follow-up

+
  • 3-month evaluation: Assess for CKD development or progression1
  • Serial monitoring: Creatinine, proteinuria, blood pressure1
  • Nephrology referral: Consider for Stage 3+ AKI or incomplete recovery1
  • Long-term follow-up: Monitor for hypertension and cardiovascular risk7
  • Medication review: Ensure appropriate dosing adjustments1

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Diagnostic Approach1

  • Clinical assessment: Focus on volume status and drug history1
  • Basic laboratory: Serum creatinine, urine dipstick1
  • Point-of-care ultrasound: If available, to rule out obstruction1

Treatment Priority1

  • Volume management: Oral rehydration if tolerated1
  • Drug cessation: Stop nephrotoxic medications immediately1
  • Simple interventions: Bladder catheter for retention1
  • Peritoneal dialysis: Consider if hemodialysis unavailable23

Clinical Question

Does intensive glucose control (target 81-108 mg/dL) reduce mortality compared to conventional glucose control (target <180 mg/dL) in critically ill patients?

Study Design

Multicenter, multinational, randomized controlled trial conducted in 42 ICUs across Australia, New Zealand, and Canada. Open-label design with computer-generated randomization stratified by site.

Population

Adult patients expected to require ICU care for ≥3 consecutive days. N=6,104 patients enrolled. Mean age 60.4 years, 64% male, mean APACHE II score 21.0. Excluded patients with diabetes requiring insulin, those expected to die within 24 hours.

Intervention

Intensive glucose control targeting blood glucose 81-108 mg/dL (4.5-6.0 mmol/L) using continuous insulin infusion.

Comparator / Control

Conventional glucose control with insulin initiated when blood glucose exceeded 180 mg/dL (10.0 mmol/L), targeting 144-180 mg/dL (8.0-10.0 mmol/L).

Primary Endpoint

Death from any cause within 90 days of randomization.

Key Findings

90-day mortality: 27.5% (829/3,016) intensive vs 24.9% (751/3,014) conventional control. ARR: -2.6% (increased mortality with intensive control). RRR: -10.4% (harm). HR 1.14 (95% CI 1.02-1.28, p=0.02). NNH: 38 patients. Severe hypoglycemia (<40 mg/dL): 6.8% vs 0.5% (p<0.001). Mean glucose achieved: 115 mg/dL vs 144 mg/dL.

Strengths

Large sample size, multicenter international design, pragmatic real-world setting, robust statistical power, clear separation of glucose levels between groups, comprehensive follow-up.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Open-label design, heterogeneous ICU populations, potential differences in insulin protocols between sites, limited to predominantly medical ICUs, short-term follow-up only.

Practice Impact

Definitively ended the era of tight glycemic control in ICUs. Led to widespread adoption of more liberal glucose targets (140-180 mg/dL) and influenced major society guidelines including ADA, ESICM, and SCCM recommendations.

Why It Matters Now

Remains the definitive evidence against intensive glucose control in critically ill patients. Current ICU glucose management protocols worldwide are based on these findings. Reinforces importance of avoiding hypoglycemia while maintaining reasonable glycemic control in critical care.
