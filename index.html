Hypertensive Emergency

Last verified: March 10, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Headache — often severe and associated with hypertensive encephalopathy1
  • Visual disturbances — blurred vision, visual field defects1
  • Altered mental status — confusion, lethargy, agitation1
  • Chest pain — may indicate cardiac ischemia or aortic dissection1
  • Shortness of breath — associated with acute pulmonary edema1
  • Nausea and vomiting — particularly with neurologic involvement1
  • Oliguria — decreased urine output from acute renal failure1
  • Hematuria — blood in urine from renal damage1

Exam Findings

  • Severely elevated blood pressure — typically SBP > 180 or DBP > 110-120 mmHg1
  • Papilledema on fundoscopy — indicative of malignant hypertension2
  • Retinal hemorrhages and exudates — grade III-IV hypertensive retinopathy2
  • Pulmonary edema findings — rales, elevated JVD, S3 gallop1
  • Neurologic deficits — focal weakness, altered consciousness1
  • Arteriovenous nicking on fundoscopy2

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Hypertensive urgency — elevated BP without target organ damage1
  • Secondary hypertension — renal or endocrine causes1
  • Medication noncompliance — most common precipitant in known hypertensives1
  • Sympathomimetic use — cocaine, amphetamines1

Less Common

  • Pheochromocytoma — catecholamine-producing tumor1
  • Renovascular disease — renal artery stenosis1
  • Primary aldosteronism — especially with hypokalemia1
  • Thyrotoxicosis — hyperthyroid crisis1

Mimics

  • Anxiety disorders — can cause elevated BP and symptoms1
  • Pain-related hypertension — reactive BP elevation1
  • White coat hypertension — measurement artifact1

Diagnostic Tests

+

Laboratory Studies

  • Complete blood count — assess for microangiopathic hemolytic anemia1
  • Comprehensive metabolic panel — evaluate renal function, electrolytes1
  • Urinalysis — proteinuria, hematuria, RBC casts1
  • Troponin — assess for cardiac ischemia1
  • LDH — elevated in microangiopathy1

Imaging and Other Tests

  • Electrocardiogram — assess for ischemia, LV hypertrophy1
  • Chest X-ray — evaluate for pulmonary edema1
  • Echocardiogram — assess LV function if heart failure suspected1
  • Fundoscopy — grade retinopathy, assess for papilledema2
  • CT head — if neurologic symptoms present1

Treatment

+

Hypertensive Emergency - ICU Setting1

  • Labetalol 10-20 mg IV bolus q10min (max 300 mg/24h) or 0.5-2 mg/min infusion1
  • Nicardipine 5-15 mg/h IV infusion, titrate by 2.5 mg/h q15min1
  • Esmolol 0.5-1 mg/kg loading dose, then 50-300 mcg/kg/min infusion1
  • Target reduction: 10-20% in first hour, 25% in first 24 hours1
  • Avoid sublingual nifedipine and clonidine due to unpredictable effects1

Specific Conditions1

  • Acute stroke: Permissive hypertension (SBP < 220 mmHg) unless thrombolysis candidate1
  • Aortic dissection: Beta-blocker first (esmolol), target SBP 100-120 mmHg1
  • Acute MI: Nitroglycerin or esmolol preferred1
  • Pulmonary edema: Nitroglycerin or clevidipine1

Asymptomatic Elevated BP - Inpatient1

  • No routine IV antihypertensives recommended for asymptomatic patients1
  • Resume home medications if held perioperatively1
  • Optimize existing regimen if inadequately controlled1
  • Outpatient follow-up within 1-2 weeks for medication adjustment1

Expected Course

+
  • Mortality ranges from 0.2-11% depending on severity and organ involvement1
  • Neurologic recovery may take days to weeks after BP control1
  • Renal function often improves but may not return to baseline1
  • Retinal changes may persist despite adequate BP control2
  • Clinical context: Long-term prognosis depends on achieving sustained BP control and preventing recurrence

Follow-up

+
  • ICU monitoring during acute phase with frequent BP checks1
  • Outpatient follow-up within 1-2 weeks after discharge1
  • Monthly visits until BP control achieved with stable regimen1
  • Annual screening for target organ damage (echo, ophthalmology, renal function)1
  • Medication adherence counseling to prevent recurrence1

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Limited ICU Access

  • Oral agents: Immediate-release nifedipine 10 mg q30min (avoid sublingual)1
  • Hydralazine 10-20 mg IV q20-30min if other agents unavailable1
  • Frequent monitoring with manual BP cuffs if automated unavailable1

Diagnostic Limitations

  • Clinical assessment for target organ damage when imaging limited1
  • Basic urinalysis can identify proteinuria and hematuria1
  • ECG changes may indicate cardiac involvement1

Malignant Spinal Cord Compression

Last verified: March 10, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Back pain: Present in 95% of patients, often the first symptom, progressive and unremitting1
  • Neurologic symptoms: Bilateral arm or leg symptoms, weakness, numbness1
  • Bladder dysfunction: Urinary retention, urinary incontinence1
  • Bowel dysfunction: Stool incontinence, constipation1
  • Gait abnormalities: Difficulty walking, ataxia1
  • Red flag pain features: Localised cervical or thoracic pain, pain aggravated by coughing/sneezing/defecation, severe unremitting pain2
  • Radicular pain: May precede myelopathic symptoms by weeks2

Exam Findings

  • Motor deficits: Progressive weakness, muscle atrophy, fasciculations1
  • Sensory deficits: Loss of sensation in dermatomal distribution, sensory level1
  • Hyperreflexia: Increased deep tendon reflexes below level of compression1
  • Pathological reflexes: Babinski sign, clonus1
  • Sphincter dysfunction: Loss of anal tone, reduced perianal sensation1
  • Functional status: Ambulatory vs paretic vs paraplegic (critical for prognosis)3

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Degenerative spondylosis with myelopathy1
  • Spinal epidural abscess1
  • Primary spinal cord tumors (ependymoma, astrocytoma)4
  • Herniated disc with cord compression1

Less Common

  • Spinal epidural hematoma (spontaneous or iatrogenic)1
  • Lymphomatous infiltration of epidural space1
  • Vertebral osteomyelitis with epidural extension1
  • Arteriovenous malformation1

Mimics

  • Guillain-Barré syndrome (ascending paralysis)1
  • Transverse myelitis1
  • Multiple sclerosis acute exacerbation1
  • Cauda equina syndrome from other causes1

Diagnostic Tests

+

Imaging5

  • MRI spine (whole spine T1, T2, STIR): Sn 44-100%, Sp 90-93% for spinal cord compression5
  • CT with 3-plane reconstruction: For spinal stability assessment and surgical planning5
  • CT myelography: When MRI contraindicated5

Laboratory Studies6

  • Complete blood count: Evaluate for hematologic malignancy6
  • PSA (men): Screen for prostate cancer6
  • Myeloma screen: Serum protein electrophoresis, immunofixation6
  • Tumor markers: Based on suspected primary (CEA, CA 19-9, etc.)6

Treatment

+

Emergency Management7

  • Dexamethasone 10-16 mg IV immediately, then oral taper over 10-14 days7
  • PPI prophylaxis with corticosteroids (especially if on NSAIDs)7
  • Immediate neurosurgical consultation if surgical candidate2
  • Immobilization until spinal stability assessed7

Definitive Treatment - Inpatient8

  • Surgery + radiotherapy: For ambulatory patients with single-level disease and ≥3 months life expectancy8
  • Radiotherapy alone: 8 Gy single fraction or 20-30 Gy in fractions, start within 24 hours3
  • Chemotherapy: For chemosensitive tumors (lymphoma, multiple myeloma)2
  • Vertebroplasty/kyphoplasty: For vertebral compression fractures5

Supportive Care9

  • DVT prophylaxis: Mechanical and/or pharmacologic as appropriate9
  • Bladder management: Foley catheter or intermittent catheterization9
  • Physiotherapy: Early mobilization when appropriate6
  • Pain management: Multimodal approach including opioids as needed6

Expected Course

+
  • Prognosis: Median survival 2-3 months, 17% alive at 1 year, 10% at 18 months10
  • Functional recovery: Pre-treatment ambulatory status is strongest predictor of post-treatment function2
  • Time-sensitive: Treatment within 24 hours of symptom onset optimizes functional preservation2
  • Primary tumor dependent: Hematologic malignancies and prostate cancer have better survival than lung cancer10

Follow-up

+
  • Neurologic monitoring: Serial neurologic exams to assess function9
  • Pain assessment: Regular pain scores and analgesic adjustment9
  • Rehabilitation: Physical and occupational therapy for functional restoration6
  • Oncology follow-up: Primary tumor management and palliative care coordination2
  • Monitoring for complications: DVT, infections, steroid-related side effects9

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Imaging Alternatives5

  • CT myelography: When MRI unavailable5
  • Plain X-rays: Limited utility but may show vertebral collapse5

Treatment Modifications7

  • Oral dexamethasone: When IV access limited7
  • Single-fraction radiotherapy: 8 Gy when multiple fractions not feasible3

Antiphospholipid Syndrome

Last verified: March 10, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Clinical context: History of recurrent venous or arterial thrombosis1
  • Clinical context: Recurrent pregnancy losses, especially in 2nd or 3rd trimester1
  • Clinical context: Unexplained stroke in young adults (<50 years)2
  • Clinical context: History of pulmonary embolism without obvious risk factors2
  • Clinical context: Livedo reticularis or skin necrosis3

Exam Findings

  • Clinical context: Livedo reticularis - net-like purplish skin discoloration3
  • Clinical context: Digital ischemia or gangrene3
  • Clinical context: Evidence of valvular heart disease (Libman-Sacks endocarditis)4
  • Clinical context: Signs of acute thrombosis (DVT, PE, stroke)2

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Clinical context: Inherited thrombophilias (Factor V Leiden, Protein C/S deficiency)5
  • Clinical context: Systemic lupus erythematosus1
  • Clinical context: Malignancy-associated thrombosis5

Less Common

  • Clinical context: Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia5
  • Clinical context: Catastrophic antiphospholipid syndrome6
  • Clinical context: Thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura5

Mimics

  • Clinical context: Vasculitis with thrombotic complications5
  • Clinical context: Atherosclerotic disease in young patients2

Diagnostic Tests

+

Antiphospholipid Antibodies1

  • Lupus anticoagulant: Most specific test for APS1
  • Anticardiolipin antibodies (IgG/IgM): Medium-high titer (>40 GPL/MPL units)1
  • Anti-β2 glycoprotein I antibodies (IgG/IgM): >99th percentile1
  • Clinical context: Must be positive on 2 occasions ≥12 weeks apart1

Supportive Laboratory Tests7

  • Complete blood count: May show thrombocytopenia (50,000-150,000/μL)7
  • PT/aPTT: Prolonged aPTT that doesn't correct with mixing study1
  • D-dimer: Elevated during acute thrombotic events2

Imaging Studies2

  • Doppler ultrasound: First-line for suspected DVT2
  • CT pulmonary angiogram: For suspected pulmonary embolism2
  • Echocardiogram: Evaluate for valvular abnormalities4

Treatment

+

Acute Thrombosis - Inpatient8

  • Unfractionated heparin: 80 units/kg IV bolus, then 18 units/kg/hr infusion8
  • Low molecular weight heparin: Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg SC q12h8
  • Clinical context: Bridge to warfarin therapy8

Long-term Anticoagulation - Outpatient8

  • Warfarin: Target INR 2.0-3.0 for venous thrombosis8
  • Warfarin: Target INR 3.0-4.0 for recurrent thrombosis or arterial events8
  • Clinical context: DOACs may be less effective than warfarin in APS9
  • Duration: Indefinite anticoagulation for definite APS8

Pregnancy Management10

  • Aspirin: 81 mg daily starting preconception10
  • Prophylactic heparin: LMWH throughout pregnancy10
  • Clinical context: Avoid warfarin due to teratogenicity10

Expected Course

+
  • Clinical context: Chronic condition requiring lifelong anticoagulation8
  • Clinical context: 30% risk of recurrent thrombosis within 10 years without treatment1
  • Clinical context: Improved pregnancy outcomes with appropriate treatment10

Follow-up

+
  • INR monitoring: Weekly initially, then monthly once stable8
  • Repeat antiphospholipid antibodies: Not routinely recommended once diagnosed1
  • Clinical context: Monitor for bleeding complications on anticoagulation8
  • Pregnancy counseling: Specialized care with maternal-fetal medicine10

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Essential Diagnostics

  • Clinical context: Focus on lupus anticoagulant testing if limited resources1
  • Clinical context: Clinical diagnosis based on thrombotic history + single positive test1

Treatment Alternatives

  • Clinical context: Aspirin 325 mg daily if warfarin unavailable8
  • Clinical context: Unfractionated heparin SC if LMWH unavailable8

Relevant Clinical Scores

Wells Score for Pulmonary Embolism

Wells Score for Pulmonary Embolism 0
Low probability (1.3% PE incidence). Consider D-dimer; if negative, PE effectively excluded.

Wells Score for DVT

Wells Score for DVT 0
Low probability (5%). D-dimer recommended; if negative, DVT excluded.

EMPA-KIDNEY — Empagliflozin in CKD

NEJM • 2023 • EMPA-KIDNEY Collaborative Group

Clinical Question

Does empagliflozin reduce kidney disease progression or cardiovascular death in patients with chronic kidney disease, including those with and without diabetes?

Study Design

Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, international multicenter trial. Participants randomized 1:1 to empagliflozin 10mg daily vs placebo. Event-driven trial with planned follow-up until 1,070 primary outcome events occurred.

Population

6,609 adults with CKD (eGFR 20-45 mL/min/1.73m² or eGFR 45-90 with urine albumin ≥200 mg/g). Mean age 63.8 years, 33.6% female, 46.4% with diabetes. Mean eGFR 37.5 mL/min/1.73m², median UACR 412 mg/g. Excluded patients with polycystic kidney disease or recent immunosuppression.

Intervention

Empagliflozin 10 mg once daily (N=3,304)

Comparator / Control

Matching placebo once daily (N=3,305)

Primary Endpoint

Composite of kidney disease progression (sustained decline in eGFR ≥40%, end-stage kidney disease, sustained eGFR <10 mL/min/1.73m², or kidney death) or cardiovascular death

Key Findings

Primary endpoint: 13.1% empagliflozin vs 16.9% placebo (HR 0.72, 95% CI 0.64-0.82, p<0.001). ARR = 3.8%, RRR = 28%, NNT = 26 over median 2.0 years. Kidney-specific component: HR 0.71 (95% CI 0.62-0.81). CV death: HR 0.78 (95% CI 0.56-1.07). Hospitalization for heart failure: HR 0.73 (95% CI 0.54-0.98). All-cause death: HR 0.93 (95% CI 0.78-1.11).

Strengths

Large, well-powered study with robust primary endpoint. Broad CKD population including non-diabetic patients. Consistent benefit across prespecified subgroups including diabetes status, baseline eGFR, and UACR levels. Low dropout rate (2.1%). Independent adjudication of outcomes.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Relatively short median follow-up (2.0 years). Primary endpoint driven largely by kidney outcomes rather than CV death. Limited racial diversity. Unclear long-term safety beyond 2 years. Post-hoc nature of some subgroup analyses for lower proteinuria levels.

Practice Impact

Established SGLT2 inhibitors as standard of care for CKD progression regardless of diabetes status. Led to FDA approval of empagliflozin for CKD in adults. Updated major guidelines (KDIGO, ADA/ESC) to recommend SGLT2 inhibitors in CKD patients with eGFR ≥20 mL/min/1.73m².

Why It Matters Now

Represents paradigm shift toward early, aggressive nephroprotection in CKD. Complements DAPA-CKD findings with dapagliflozin. Ongoing questions about optimal timing, combination with other nephroprotective agents (finerenone), and cost-effectiveness in healthcare systems. Real-world implementation studies are validating trial benefits.
