Emergency department screening tool predicts firearm violence risk
Emergency departments see high-risk populations for firearm violence, and effective screening tools could enable targeted prevention interventions.
Design: Prospective longitudinal study at 4 level 1 emergency departments in 3 cities • Outcome: Firearm violence (shooting someone or being shot) at 12 months
Population: Adults aged 18 to 24 years seen in an ED for any reason (1506 participants, 61.4% female, mean age 21.3 years)
Intervention: SaFETy score screening tool (4-item, 10-point scale)
Comparator: Baseline covariates and comprehensive covariate set
Key Results
  • Among 1122 participants with follow-up data, 73 (6.5%) had 12-month firearm violence
  • 12-month firearm violence rates by SaFETy score: 0 points (1.8%), 1-5 points (12.1%), 6+ points (25.0%)
  • Area under ROC curve was 0.78 (95% CI 0.72-0.83)
  • Optimal cut point >0 had sensitivity 83.1% and specificity 62.4%
  • Adding SaFETy score to full covariate set improved AUC from 0.81 to 0.84 (p=0.025)
Clinical Interpretation
  • The SaFETy score provides a practical screening tool for identifying young adults at high risk for firearm violence
  • The strong risk gradient suggests potential utility for targeted interventions, though implementation would require robust prevention programs
Limitations
  • Outcomes were primarily self-reported
  • Highest-risk subsamples were more likely to have missing data
  • 25.5% loss to follow-up
Bottom Line: The SaFETy score effectively stratifies firearm violence risk in young ED patients and could guide prevention efforts.

This addresses a longstanding clinical dilemma about timing of valve replacement in asymptomatic severe aortic stenosis, with potential to change practice guidelines.
Design: Randomized controlled trial with 10-year follow-up comparing early surgery versus conservative care in asymptomatic patients • Outcome: Composite of operative mortality or death from cardiovascular causes during 10-year follow-up
Population: 145 asymptomatic patients with very severe aortic stenosis (aortic-valve area ≤0.75 cm² with peak aortic jet velocity ≥4.5 m/s)
Intervention: Early surgical valve replacement (n=73)
Comparator: Conservative care (n=72)
Key Results
  • Primary endpoint occurred in 2/73 (3%) early surgery vs 17/72 (24%) conservative care (HR 0.10, 95% CI 0.02-0.43, P=0.002)
  • At 10 years, cumulative incidence of operative mortality or cardiovascular death was 1% early surgery vs 19% conservative care
  • All-cause mortality: 11/73 (15%) early surgery vs 23/72 (32%) conservative care (HR 0.42, 95% CI 0.21-0.86)
Clinical Interpretation
  • This provides compelling evidence for early intervention in asymptomatic very severe aortic stenosis, with substantial long-term survival benefit that challenges current watchful waiting approaches
Limitations
  • Single-center study
  • Relatively small sample size
  • Surgical techniques and patient selection may limit generalizability
Bottom Line: Early surgical valve replacement significantly reduces 10-year cardiovascular mortality in asymptomatic very severe aortic stenosis compared to conservative management.

SHIFT — Ivabradine in Heart Failure

Lancet • 2010 • Swedberg K et al.

Clinical Question

In patients with symptomatic HFrEF (NYHA II-IV; LVEF ≤ 35%) with hospitalization for heart failure within the preceding year and resting heart rate of ≥ 70bpm despite maximal medical therapy including beta blockers, does the addition of the sinus node inhibitor ivabradine result in improved heart failure-related mortality or hospitalization compared to placebo?

Study Design

Randomized placebo-controlled study with median follow-up of nearly 2 years. See source for details.

Population

6,558 patients with symptomatic HFrEF (NYHA II-IV; LVEF ≤ 35%) with hospitalization for heart failure within the preceding year and resting HR ≥ 70bpm despite maximally-tolerated medical therapy (ACE/ARB, beta blocker, aldosterone antagonist). Only 26% of patients were on guideline-based target doses of beta blocker and only 56% were on at least half-target doses at inclusion.

Intervention

Ivabradine, a sinus node modifying agent that lowers heart rate by inhibiting the 'funny current' (If) channel in sinoatrial node pacemaking cells

Comparator / Control

Placebo

Primary Endpoint

Heart failure-related hospitalization or mortality

Key Findings

Ivabradine resulted in a 5% absolute reduction in the primary endpoint of heart failure mortality or hospitalization at 2 years. This benefit was driven by both a 5% absolute reduction in heart failure hospitalization and a 2% absolute reduction in heart failure mortality, with trends toward improved all-cause mortality and cardiovascular mortality. Benefits were greatest in patients with resting HR > 75bpm and good HR response to ivabradine.

Strengths

Large sample size (N=6,558), contemporary medical therapy background, well-tolerated intervention with only modest increase in symptomatic bradycardia, demonstrated dose-response relationship with heart rate reduction

Weaknesses / Limitations

Only 26% of patients were on guideline-based target doses of beta blocker at inclusion, and benefits were somewhat attenuated in the subgroup on at least half-dose beta blocker therapy, raising questions about benefit in patients already on target dose beta blocker therapy

Practice Impact

Led to ESC guideline recommendation for ivabradine as additional therapy for patients with HFrEF (LVEF ≤ 35%), persistent NYHA II-IV symptoms, and resting HR ≥ 70bpm despite maximal medical therapy. Also recommended for consideration in patients with HFrEF and beta blocker intolerance. FDA approved ivabradine for heart failure in the US in 2015.

Why It Matters Now

Established ivabradine as an important option for heart rate control in HFrEF patients who remain symptomatic despite optimal medical therapy, particularly those with HR > 75bpm. The trial demonstrated that targeted heart rate reduction through If channel inhibition provides clinical benefits beyond traditional beta-blocker therapy, though questions remain about its role in patients already on optimal beta-blocker doses.

Cardiac Tamponade

Last verified: April 8, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Chest pain - vague, may be present1
  • Palpitations1
  • Shortness of breath (dyspnea)1
  • Dizziness in more severe cases1
  • Syncope in more severe cases1
  • Altered mental status in more severe cases1

Exam Findings

  • Beck's triad: hypotension, jugular venous distension (JVD), and muffled heart sounds1
  • Pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg - decrease in systolic blood pressure by more than 10 mmHg with inspiration1
  • Tachycardia - compensatory mechanism to overcome reduced cardiac output1
  • Kussmaul sign - paradoxical elevated JVP and pressure during inspiration (sometimes seen)1
  • Signs of cardiogenic or obstructive shock1
  • Clinical context: Pulsus paradoxus may be absent in patients with ASD, elevated diastolic pressures, pulmonary hypertension, and aortic regurgitation

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Cardiogenic shock from other causes (MI, severe heart failure)1
  • Obstructive shock from other causes (massive PE, tension pneumothorax)1
  • Large pericardial effusion without tamponade physiology1

Less Common

  • Restrictive cardiomyopathy8
  • Constrictive pericarditis8
  • Right ventricular infarction8

Mimics

  • Severe asthma with pulsus paradoxus8
  • Acute coronary syndrome with hemodynamic instability10
  • Aortic dissection with hemodynamic compromise10

Diagnostic Tests

+

Echocardiography1

  • Right atrial collapse: Sn 50-100%, Sp 33-100%11
  • Right ventricular collapse: Sn 48-100%, Sp 72-100%11
  • Any chamber collapse: Sn 90%, Sp 65%12
  • Venous flow Doppler: Sn 75%, Sp 91%12
  • Plethoric inferior vena cava with minimal respiratory variation15

Electrocardiography1

  • Low voltage complexes: Sn 56%, Sp 74%, PPV 81%14
  • Electrical alternans: Sn 23%, Sp 98%, PPV 95% (classic but rare finding)14
  • Sinus tachycardia: Sn 76%, Sp 60%, PPV 79% (most common ECG finding)14
  • Any ECG abnormality: Sn 89%, Sp 47%14

Imaging1

  • Chest X-ray: May show enlarged cardiac silhouette3
  • CT chest: Can detect pericardial effusion3

Treatment

+

Emergency Management1

  • Immediate pericardiocentesis for hemodynamically unstable patients4
  • IV fluid resuscitation may provide temporary improvement in hypovolemic patients28
  • Avoid preload reduction (diuretics, nitroglycerin)4
  • Inotropic support may be needed for severe cases10

Pericardiocentesis4

  • Echocardiographic guidance preferred when available4
  • Fluoroscopic guidance alternative approach4
  • Subxiphoid approach most commonly used4
  • Catheter drainage 3±2 days to prevent recurrence28

Surgical Management4

  • Surgical drainage when pericardiocentesis not feasible or unsuccessful10
  • Pericardial window for recurrent effusions4
  • Emergency pericardiotomy for refractory cases10

Specific Etiologies1

  • Uremic tamponade: Emergent pericardiocentesis + intensive dialysis6
  • Malignant effusion: Pericardiocentesis + systemic antineoplastic therapy29
  • Post-cardiac surgery: Often requires surgical exploration10

Expected Course

+
  • Immediate relief of symptoms with successful pericardiocentesis4
  • Recurrence rate 6% with extended catheter drainage vs higher rates with single drainage28
  • Mortality depends on underlying etiology and rapidity of treatment1
  • Clinical context: Prognosis varies significantly based on cause - malignancy (32%), infection (24%), idiopathic (16%)8

Follow-up

+
  • Serial echocardiography for first 48 hours post-procedure to monitor for reaccumulation10
  • Pericardial fluid analysis for cytology, culture, and biochemistry when etiology unclear4
  • Treatment of underlying condition when identified1
  • Monitor for constrictive pericarditis in long-term follow-up of certain etiologies1

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Clinical Diagnosis1

  • Beck's triad assessment - hypotension, JVD, muffled heart sounds1
  • Pulsus paradoxus measurement using blood pressure cuff8
  • Point-of-care ultrasound if available for pericardial effusion detection1

Management1

  • IV fluid resuscitation as temporizing measure28
  • Blind pericardiocentesis via subxiphoid approach if imaging unavailable4
  • Immediate surgical referral when percutaneous drainage not feasible4
