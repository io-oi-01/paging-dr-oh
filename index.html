Lower GI Bleeding

Last verified: March 10, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Hematochezia: Bright red blood per rectum — most common presentation1
  • Melena: Dark, tarry stools (less common in lower GI bleeding)1
  • Iron deficiency anemia: Fatigue, dyspnea, palpitations with occult bleeding1
  • Painless bleeding in diverticular disease (over 40% of lower GI bleeds)1
  • Systemic symptoms: Syncope, weakness, dizziness1

Exam Findings

  • Hemodynamic instability: SBP ≤ 90 mmHg, HR > 100/min in massive bleeding1
  • Orthostatic vital signs: May indicate volume depletion1
  • Pallor and signs of anemia1
  • Abdominal exam: Assess for masses, tenderness, distention1
  • Digital rectal exam: Evaluate for hemorrhoids, masses, blood1

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Diverticulosis: Over 40% of lower GI bleeds, painless hematochezia1
  • Angiodysplasia: Most frequent cause of small bowel bleeding in patients >60 years4
  • Ischemic colitis: 20% of lower GI bleeds, more prevalent in elderly1
  • Hemorrhoids: Bleeding with/without defecation, swelling, discomfort6
  • Inflammatory bowel disease: Ulcerative colitis and Crohn disease6

Less Common

  • Colon cancer: 3.4% of rectal bleeding cases, friable pathologic vasculature6
  • Anal fissures: Painful with defecation6
  • Proctitis: May be infectious or inflammatory6
  • Small bowel bleeding: 5-10% of all GI bleeds, includes Meckel diverticulum8
  • Anticoagulant-related bleeding1

Mimics

  • Upper GI bleeding: 1/3 of presumed lower GI bleeds with heavy bleeding are actually upper GI1
  • Hemosuccus pancreaticus: Rare, presents with melena and abdominal pain9
  • Hemobilia: Bleeding from biliary tree9

Diagnostic Tests

+

Laboratory Studies1

  • Complete blood count: Hemoglobin ≤ 6 g/dL in massive bleeding1
  • Basic metabolic panel: Assess volume status and renal function1
  • Coagulation studies: PT/INR, aPTT if on anticoagulants1
  • Type and crossmatch: Prepare for transfusion1

Endoscopic Studies1,6

  • Colonoscopy: Gold standard for lower GI bleeding evaluation and treatment6
  • Flexible sigmoidoscopy: For distal bleeding sources6
  • Upper endoscopy: Consider if melena or upper GI symptoms1
  • Capsule endoscopy: For small bowel bleeding after negative colonoscopy8

Imaging Studies7

  • CT angiography: Detects bleeding ≥ 0.5-1.0 mL/min2
  • 99mTc-RBC scan: Sn 93%, Sp 95%, detects bleeding ≥ 0.1 mL/min7
  • Mesenteric angiography: For active bleeding, allows therapeutic embolization2

Treatment

+

Emergency Department/Initial Management1

  • IV access: Two large-bore IVs, crystalloid resuscitation1
  • Blood transfusion: PRBC if hemodynamically unstable or severe anemia1
  • Anticoagulant reversal: Consider reversal agents for life-threatening bleeding12
  • Risk stratification: Use Oakland score (≤8 low risk) or SHA2PE score12,20

Inpatient Non-ICU Management1,2

  • Hemodynamic monitoring: Vital signs, urine output2
  • Serial CBC monitoring: Every 6-8 hours if actively bleeding1
  • Colonoscopy: Within 24 hours for severe bleeding, within 14 days for stable patients12
  • Conservative management: >80% of lower GI bleeds stop spontaneously1

ICU Management2,4

  • Intensive monitoring: Continuous cardiac monitoring, frequent vitals2
  • Airway protection: Intubation if obtunded with active bleeding4
  • Massive transfusion protocol: If ongoing severe hemorrhage2
  • Urgent intervention: Emergent colonoscopy, angiography, or surgery2

Endoscopic Therapy12,20

  • Diverticular bleeding: Through-the-scope clips, band ligation, coagulation20
  • Angiodysplasia: Thermal coagulation, hemostatic clips4
  • Injection therapy: Epinephrine for hemostasis2

Expected Course

+
  • Self-limited bleeding: >80% of lower GI bleeds stop spontaneously1
  • Overall mortality: 2-4% for lower GI bleeding1
  • Massive bleeding mortality: Up to 21% in patients >65 years1
  • Recurrent bleeding: Diverticular bleeding commonly recurs1

Follow-up

+
  • Outpatient colonoscopy: If not performed during admission12
  • Iron supplementation: For iron deficiency anemia1
  • Anticoagulation management: Risk-benefit assessment for resumption12
  • Primary care follow-up: Monitor hemoglobin, symptom recurrence1

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Basic Evaluation

  • Hemoglobin/hematocrit: Point-of-care testing if available1
  • Vital signs assessment: Orthostatic measurements1
  • Digital rectal exam: Assess for obvious bleeding source1

Management

  • IV fluid resuscitation: Normal saline or Ringer lactate1
  • Oral iron therapy: For chronic occult bleeding1
  • Clinical context: Blood typing essential if transfusion anticipated

Community-Acquired Pneumonia

Last verified: March 10, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Cough: Productive or non-productive, present in majority of patients1
  • Sputum production: May be purulent, bloody, or rusty-colored1
  • Fever: Often >38°C (100.4°F) with chills and rigors1
  • Pleuritic chest pain: Sharp, stabbing pain worsened by deep inspiration1
  • Dyspnea: Shortness of breath, may progress to respiratory failure1
  • Constitutional symptoms: Fatigue, malaise, headache, myalgias1
  • Clinical context: Atypical presentations in elderly (confusion, falls) and immunocompromised patients

Exam Findings

  • Vital signs: Tachypnea (>20/min), tachycardia, fever, hypotension1
  • Pulmonary: Crackles, decreased breath sounds, dullness to percussion1
  • Consolidation signs: Bronchial breath sounds, egophony, tactile fremitus1
  • Hypoxemia: Oxygen saturation <90% on room air indicates severity2
  • Mental status changes: Confusion, especially in elderly patients2
  • Clinical context: Physical exam has limited sensitivity for pneumonia diagnosis

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Viral pneumonia: Influenza, SARS-CoV-2, RSV, parainfluenza1
  • Atypical pneumonia: Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Legionella species1
  • Acute bronchitis: Cough without infiltrate on chest imaging1
  • Congestive heart failure: Bilateral infiltrates, BNP elevation1

Less Common

  • Pulmonary embolism: Pleuritic chest pain, dyspnea, D-dimer elevation1
  • Lung cancer: Mass lesion, weight loss, smoking history1
  • Aspiration pneumonia: History of dysphagia, altered mental status3
  • Drug-induced pneumonitis: Medication exposure history1

Mimics

  • Pulmonary contusion: Trauma history, chest wall injury1
  • Acute respiratory distress syndrome: Bilateral infiltrates, severe hypoxemia1
  • Organizing pneumonia: Subacute course, steroid responsive1
  • Vasculitis: Systemic symptoms, autoimmune markers1

Diagnostic Tests

+

Imaging

  • Chest X-ray: First-line imaging, shows pulmonary infiltrates1
  • CT chest: More sensitive than CXR, useful when CXR normal but clinical suspicion high1

Laboratory Studies

  • Complete blood count: May show leukocytosis or leukopenia2
  • Basic metabolic panel: Assess for renal function, electrolyte abnormalities2
  • Blood cultures: Positive in 20-25% of S. pneumoniae pneumonia cases4
  • Arterial blood gas: If hypoxemia suspected or severity assessment needed2

Microbiologic Studies

  • Sputum culture: If adequate specimen (>25 PMNs, <10 epithelial cells per hpf)2
  • Urinary antigens: Streptococcus pneumoniae and Legionella pneumophila serogroup 12
  • Influenza testing: Molecular testing preferred when influenza circulating2
  • Procalcitonin: May help differentiate bacterial from viral etiology2

Treatment

+

Outpatient - No Comorbidities2

  • Amoxicillin 1g PO TID
  • Doxycycline 100mg PO BID
  • Azithromycin 500mg PO day 1, then 250mg daily × 4 days
  • Clarithromycin 500mg PO BID

Outpatient - With Comorbidities2

  • Amoxicillin/clavulanate 875/125mg PO BID PLUS macrolide
  • Cephalexin 500mg PO QID PLUS macrolide
  • Levofloxacin 750mg PO daily
  • Moxifloxacin 400mg PO daily

Inpatient Non-ICU2

  • β-lactam PLUS macrolide: Ampicillin/sulbactam 1.5-3g IV q6h + azithromycin 500mg IV daily
  • Ceftriaxone 1-2g IV daily + azithromycin 500mg IV daily
  • Cefotaxime 1-2g IV q8h + azithromycin 500mg IV daily
  • Respiratory fluoroquinolone: Levofloxacin 750mg IV daily OR moxifloxacin 400mg IV daily

ICU - Severe CAP2

  • β-lactam PLUS macrolide: Ceftriaxone 1-2g IV daily + azithromycin 500mg IV daily
  • β-lactam PLUS fluoroquinolone: Ceftriaxone + levofloxacin 750mg IV daily
  • Add MRSA coverage if risk factors: Vancomycin 15mg/kg IV q12h (adjust by levels) OR linezolid 600mg IV q12h
  • Add Pseudomonas coverage if risk factors: Piperacillin/tazobactam 4.5g IV q6h

Expected Course

+
  • Clinical improvement: Expected within 48-72 hours of appropriate antibiotic therapy2
  • Radiographic resolution: May lag behind clinical improvement by weeks1
  • Mortality rates: Overall 8th leading cause of death; ICU mortality up to 23%
  • Duration of therapy: Typically 5-7 days for most patients2
  • Clinical context: Elderly and immunocompromised patients may have slower recovery

Follow-up

+
  • Outpatient follow-up: 48-72 hours for clinical response assessment2
  • Chest imaging follow-up: Not routinely recommended if clinically improving2
  • Smoking cessation counseling: For all tobacco users2
  • Pneumococcal vaccination: Ensure appropriate vaccination status2
  • Clinical context: Consider repeat imaging in high-risk patients or those with poor response

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Limited Laboratory Access

  • Clinical diagnosis: Based on symptoms and physical exam findings
  • Oxygen saturation: Pulse oximetry if available for severity assessment
  • Basic vital signs: Focus on respiratory rate, heart rate, blood pressure

Limited Imaging

  • Clinical scoring: Use CURB-65 for severity assessment without imaging
  • Empirical treatment: Start antibiotics based on clinical presentation
  • Response to therapy: Monitor clinical improvement as diagnostic confirmation

Antibiotic Selection

  • Amoxicillin: First-line in resource-limited settings per WHO guidelines
  • Doxycycline: Alternative broad-spectrum option
  • Local resistance patterns: Consider regional antibiogram data if available

Relevant Clinical Scores

CURB-65 Severity Score

CURB-65 Severity Score 0
Low risk (0.6% 30-day mortality). Consider outpatient treatment.

Takotsubo Cardiomyopathy

Last verified: March 10, 2026

Read on StatPearls →

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Chest pain and dyspnea are the most common presenting symptoms, similar to acute coronary syndrome1
  • Nausea, palpitations, syncope, and vomiting may occur1
  • Symptoms typically occur in the mid-afternoon (unlike ACS which often occurs in early morning hours)1
  • Preceding emotional or physical stressor reported in ~72% of cases (emotional 28%, physical 36%, both 8%)2
  • May present with complications: heart failure, cardiogenic shock, or cardiac arrest3

Exam Findings

  • Nonspecific findings similar to heart failure: pulmonary rales, S3 gallop, systolic murmur of mitral regurgitation1
  • May have signs of cardiogenic shock or heart failure depending on severity3
  • Hypotension and signs of cardiac tamponade may occur with rare complications like cardiac rupture4

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Acute myocardial infarction (ST-elevation or non-ST-elevation) - most important differential3
  • Acute myocarditis - requires cardiac MRI with LGE for differentiation3
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy with apical ballooning - differentiated by thickened myocardium at apex on imaging1

Less Common

  • Spontaneous coronary artery dissection (SCAD)3
  • Peripartum cardiomyopathy3
  • Pheochromocytoma with catecholamine excess5
  • Thyrotoxicosis6

Mimics

  • Cardiac tamponade6
  • Heavy metal toxicity6
  • Beriberi (thiamine deficiency)6
  • Late-stage hypertrophic cardiomyopathy6

Diagnostic Tests

+

Electrocardiogram1

  • Stage 1: ST deviation (not necessarily localized to territory) in first few hours1
  • Stage 2: QTc prolongation and widespread T-wave inversion in precordial, lateral leads over 1-3 days1
  • May show ST-elevation, T-wave inversion, or left bundle branch block1

Cardiac Biomarkers7

  • Troponin: Mild elevation (lower than typical MI), may rarely be negative7
  • NT-proBNP/BNP: Significantly elevated, more useful diagnostic marker than troponin7

Imaging Studies1,3

  • Transthoracic Echocardiogram: Shows characteristic regional wall motion abnormalities (apical, midventricular, basal, or focal)1
  • Coronary angiography with left ventriculography: Gold standard - normal/mild CAD with perfusion-contraction mismatch7
  • Cardiac MRI with LGE: Recommended within 5 days to confirm diagnosis and exclude mimics3

InterTAK Diagnostic Score3

  • Scoring system: Uses 7 clinical variables; score ≥40 identifies TTS with 81.8% Sn, 100% Sp8
  • Parameters: Female sex, emotional/physical triggers, absence of ST depression, psychiatric/neurologic disorders, QT prolongation3

Treatment

+

Acute Management9

  • Supportive care similar to acute heart failure management
  • ACE inhibitors/ARBs for heart failure symptoms
  • Beta-blockers may be used but evidence for preventing recurrence is limited9
  • Diuretics for volume overload as clinically indicated

Complications Management1,4

  • LVOT obstruction: Avoid inotropes, consider beta-blockers and volume expansion
  • Ventricular arrhythmias: Monitor for torsades de pointes during 24-48 hours, manage QT prolongation1
  • Anticoagulation may be considered for wall motion abnormalities in certain situations5

Long-term Management9

  • Clinical context: No established guidelines exist; treatment based on expert consensus
  • Follow-up imaging to document recovery (typically within weeks to months)
  • Risk factor modification and stress management counseling
  • Recurrence monitoring - occurs in small percentage of patients9

Expected Course

+
  • Recovery: Left ventricular function typically recovers within days to weeks in most patients1,5
  • Long-term outcomes: Previously considered benign, but recent studies show persistent subtle dysfunction and limiting symptoms9
  • Mortality: In-hospital mortality comparable to STEMI and non-STEMI patients5
  • Recurrence: Occurs in small percentage; "super recurrence" (≥2 episodes) in ~1.8% of patients9

Follow-up

+
  • Serial echocardiography to document LV function recovery1
  • ECG monitoring: T-wave changes and QTc prolongation may persist weeks to months after LV recovery7
  • Stress management and psychological support as appropriate2
  • Long-term cardiology follow-up given potential for persistent dysfunction and recurrence9

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Essential Diagnostics

  • 12-lead ECG and basic echocardiography for initial assessment
  • Troponin testing if available (though may be normal)
  • Clinical scoring using InterTAK criteria when advanced imaging unavailable

Management Priorities

  • Rule out MI as primary concern with available resources
  • Supportive care for heart failure symptoms
  • Identify and address potential triggers or stressors

CLOVERS — Liberal vs Restrictive Early Fluids in Sepsis

NEJM • 2023 • Shapiro NI, et al.
Read on WikiJournalClub →

Clinical Question

In patients with sepsis-induced hypotension who have already received 1-3L of IV fluids, is a strategy that prioritizes early use of vasopressors superior to one that prioritizes fluid boluses?

Study Design

Multicenter, unblinded, randomized controlled trial with intention-to-treat analysis. Study was halted by DSMB at second interim analysis for futility in primary outcome. Trial protocols applied for 24 hours post-randomization.

Population

N=1,563 adults >18 years with sepsis-induced hypotension (SBP <100 after >1L IVF). Mean age 59, 47% female, mean SOFA score 3.4, mean SBP 93.5 mmHg. 92% randomized in ER, median time to randomization 61 minutes, median pre-randomization fluid volume 2050 mL.

Intervention

Restrictive group (n=782): Prioritized early vasopressor use, halted maintenance/bolus fluids, maximum 2L total fluids including pre-randomization volume. Started norepinephrine for MAP <65 or SBP <90 to achieve MAP ≥65. 'Rescue fluids' allowed for severe hypotension, refractory shock, lactate >4 and rising, or clinical deterioration.

Comparator / Control

Liberal group (n=781): Prioritized fluid resuscitation with 2L bolus at randomization, then 500cc boluses for MAP <65, SBP <90, lactate >4 and rising, UOP <30cc/hr, HR >110, or vasopressor requirement. 'Rescue vasopressors' allowed for severe hypotension, rising lactate after 2 hours, fluid overload, or >5L total fluids.

Primary Endpoint

Death from any cause before discharge home at 90 days

Key Findings

No significant difference in 90-day mortality: 14% restrictive vs 14.9% liberal (difference -0.9%, 95% CI -4.4% to 2.6%, p=0.61). Secondary outcomes showed no significant differences in organ-free days, ventilator-free days (23.4 vs 22.8 days), renal replacement therapy-free days, vasopressor-free days, ICU-free days, or hospital-free days.

Strengths

Large multicenter RCT with pragmatic design reflecting real-world ED practice. Early randomization (median 61 minutes) in predominantly ED setting (92%). Included safety data on peripheral vasopressor administration. Well-defined protocols with rescue options for both arms.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Unblinded design, stopped early for futility. Significant protocol deviations likely occurred given unblinded nature. Median fluid volume already substantial (2050 mL) before randomization, potentially limiting ability to detect differences between strategies.

Why It Matters Now

Adds to growing evidence from trials like CLASSIC that restrictive fluid strategies don't improve mortality in sepsis. Provides safety data supporting early peripheral vasopressor use and challenges traditional fluid-first approaches in septic shock management.
