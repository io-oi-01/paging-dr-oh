Smallpox therapy tecovirimat not efficacious in treating clade II mpox
Tecovirimat is the only FDA-approved antiviral for poxviruses and was widely used during the 2022 mpox outbreak, making its clinical efficacy crucial for treatment decisions.
Design: Phase 3, international, double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial with 2:1 randomization to tecovirimat or placebo for 14 days • Outcome: Clinical resolution assessed in time-to-event analysis in participants with active skin or mucosal lesions
Population: 412 adults with laboratory-confirmed clade II mpox (344 confirmed, 336 with active skin/mucosal lesions included in primary analysis)
Intervention: Oral tecovirimat for 14 days
Comparator: Placebo for 14 days
Key Results
  • By day 29, estimated cumulative incidence of clinical resolution was 83% with tecovirimat vs 84% with placebo (competing-risks hazard ratio 0.98, 95% CI 0.74-1.31, P=0.89)
  • No substantial between-group differences in pain reduction among participants with severe pain (difference 0.1 point, 95% CI -0.8 to 1.0)
  • No difference in complete lesion healing (competing-risks hazard ratio 0.97, 95% CI 0.75-1.26) or viral DNA clearance
  • Similar incidence of grade 3+ adverse events (4% tecovirimat vs 3% placebo)
Clinical Interpretation
  • This well-designed trial definitively shows tecovirimat provides no clinical benefit for clade II mpox, challenging its routine use
  • The lack of efficacy despite animal model success highlights the limitation of the FDA Animal Rule pathway
Limitations
  • Limited to clade II mpox, may not apply to other clades
  • Most participants likely had self-limiting disease course
Bottom Line: Tecovirimat shows no clinical benefit for clade II mpox treatment and should not be routinely prescribed.
High confidence 📊 Practice-changing

Computed tomography angiography of the head and neck may inform emergency management in patients with suspected stroke
Understanding when CT angiography is most likely to change acute stroke management can improve resource utilization and reduce unnecessary imaging while ensuring appropriate care.
Design: Retrospective cohort study of 1,445 ED patients at an academic Level 1 trauma center who underwent CTAHN in 2023 for 5 common neurologic presentations • Outcome: CTAHN findings resulting in acute management change
Population: Emergency department patients with acute neurologic symptoms (stroke code, headache, dizziness, altered mental status, vision changes)
Intervention: CT angiography of head and neck (CTAHN)
Key Results
  • 216 of 1,445 CTAHN studies (14.9%) resulted in acute management change
  • Highest yield for stroke codes (21.2%) and vision changes (14.7%), lowest for dizziness (4.0%)
  • For stroke codes, predictors of management change included abnormal CT head (OR 2.84), focal neurologic deficit (OR 2.2), new vs recurring symptoms (OR 2.39), maximal symptom severity at onset (OR 3.95), and tobacco use (OR 2.02)
  • NIHSS scores of 10-14 had highest likelihood of CTAHN acute management change
  • For headache, abnormal CT head (OR 4.98) and thunderclap presentation (OR 5.31) predicted management change
Clinical Interpretation
  • These clinical predictors can help emergency physicians and neurologists make more informed decisions about when CTAHN is likely to be high-yield, potentially reducing unnecessary imaging while maintaining quality care
Limitations
  • Retrospective single-center design
  • Only univariate trends identified for some presentations due to small sample sizes
Bottom Line: CT angiography of head and neck has highest yield in stroke codes with abnormal CT, focal deficits, or new symptoms, and can be guided by specific clinical predictors.

CASSINI — Rivaroxaban for Cancer-Associated VTE Prophylaxis

NEJM • 2019 • Khorana et al.

Clinical Question

Does rivaroxaban prophylaxis reduce venous thromboembolism in ambulatory cancer patients initiating systemic therapy?

Study Design

Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial. Multicenter international study with 1:1 randomization to rivaroxaban 10mg daily vs placebo for up to 180 days. Primary analysis by intention-to-treat and on-treatment.

Population

Adult ambulatory cancer patients initiating systemic therapy with Khorana Risk Score ≥2. Key exclusions: therapeutic anticoagulation, high bleeding risk, brain metastases. N=841 randomized (420 rivaroxaban, 421 placebo).

Intervention

Rivaroxaban 10 mg orally once daily for up to 180 days

Comparator / Control

Matching placebo once daily for up to 180 days

Primary Endpoint

Composite of objectively confirmed proximal DVT, PE, asymptomatic proximal DVT on screening ultrasonography, VTE-related death, or arterial thromboembolism during intervention period

Key Findings

ITT analysis: 25/420 (6.0%) rivaroxaban vs 37/421 (8.8%) placebo (HR 0.66, 95% CI 0.40-1.09, p=0.10). On-treatment analysis: 11/405 (2.6%) vs 27/404 (6.4%) (HR 0.40, 95% CI 0.20-0.80, p=0.007). ARR 3.8%, RRR 60%, NNT 26. Major bleeding: 8/420 (1.9%) vs 4/421 (1.0%) (HR 2.0, 95% CI 0.59-6.49).

Strengths

First large RCT of DOAC prophylaxis in ambulatory cancer patients. Well-designed with systematic VTE screening. Used validated Khorana Risk Score for enrollment. Appropriate statistical plan with pre-specified on-treatment analysis.

Weaknesses / Limitations

Failed to meet statistical significance in primary ITT analysis (p=0.10). Higher discontinuation rate (43.9%) than expected. Enrolled lower-risk patients than anticipated. Limited power for bleeding endpoints. Post-treatment period not evaluated.

Practice Impact

Led to FDA approval of rivaroxaban for VTE prophylaxis in ambulatory cancer patients. Influenced guidelines including NCCN and ASCO recommendations for thromboprophylaxis in selected high-risk ambulatory cancer patients.

Why It Matters Now

Established foundation for cancer-associated VTE prevention. Highlighted importance of patient selection and adherence. Subsequent trials (AVERT, PROTECHT) have further refined risk-benefit ratio and patient selection criteria for ambulatory cancer thromboprophylaxis.

Hepatorenal Syndrome

Last verified: March 21, 2026

Clinical Manifestations

+

Symptoms

  • Oliguria: Progressive decrease in urination with smaller volumes1
  • Fatigue and lethargy: Often multifactorial, may be contributed to by loss of appetite due to increasing nausea1
  • Progressive jaundice: Originating in the sclera and then spreading from head-to-toe1
  • Ascites: Very slow, gradual build-up of ascitic fluid in the peritoneum due to the intravascular loss of albumin1
  • Hepatic encephalopathy: Confusion due to hepatic encephalopathy is likely the last and most severe stage1

Exam Findings

  • Coagulopathy: The liver's synthetic function becomes affected, and patients become coagulopathic and more likely to bleed1
  • Signs of portal hypertension: Most patients have portal hypertension from alcoholic hepatitis, cirrhosis, or metastatic cancers1
  • Signs of advanced liver disease: Patients with hepatorenal syndrome present with signs and symptoms of severe liver failure1
  • Spider nevi: Clinical context: Common in patients with cirrhosis secondary to hyperestrogenemia

Differential Diagnosis

+

Common

  • Prerenal azotemia/hypovolemic AKI: Must be excluded with volume expansion2
  • Acute tubular necrosis: Essential to differentiate underlying etiologies of AKI3
  • Drug-induced nephrotoxicity: Clinical context: NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast agents

Less Common

  • Glomerulonephritis: Assessment of urine sediment and proteinuria should be initiated4
  • Obstructive nephropathy: Should be evaluated with abdominal ultrasonography4
  • Cholemic nephropathy: One of the underlying etiologies of AKI that should be differentiated3

Mimics

  • Bacterial infection-related AKI: The presence of bacterial infections should be investigated extensively4
  • Hypovolemic hyponatremia: Clinical context: Can mimic HRS but responds to volume replacement

Diagnostic Tests

+

Laboratory Criteria2,4

  • Serum creatinine rise: ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours or ≥50% increase from baseline2
  • Serum-ascites albumin gradient: ≥1.1 g/dL predicts portal hypertension with 97% accuracy5
  • Urine output: Not currently part of diagnostic criteria because patients with cirrhosis and ascites have low UO at baseline2

Novel Biomarkers6

  • Neutrophil gelatinase-associated lipocalin (NGAL): May be useful for differential diagnosis of AKI etiologies in cirrhosis6
  • Fractional excretion of sodium: Markers reflecting tubular functional integrity will likely be incorporated into future diagnostic criteria7

Treatment

+

First-line Therapy (Inpatient)1,8

  • Terlipressin: 1 mg IV bolus q6h initially, can increase to 2 mg q6h if <30% sCr reduction by day 48
  • Albumin: 1 g/kg body weight (maximum 100 g) daily for 2 days without clear evidence of hypovolemia4
  • Continuous terlipressin: Better tolerated than IV boluses with same efficacy9

Alternative Therapy (ICU)3,8

  • Norepinephrine: Often utilized where terlipressin is unavailable3
  • Octreotide + Midodrine: Combination indicated for HRS-AKI management but lower efficacy3
  • Renal replacement therapy: May be applied as bridging therapy to transplantation10

Definitive Therapy1,4

  • Liver transplantation: Definitive treatment for HRS-AKI8
  • Simultaneous liver-kidney transplant: May be necessary for patients not expected to recover kidney function post-transplantation2

Expected Course

+
  • Mortality: Median survival for HRS Type 1 and 2 is approximately 1 and 6.7 months, respectively9
  • Treatment response: Terlipressin effective in reversing HRS in only 40%-50% of patients9
  • Prognosis with ascites: Mortality reaches 15% at 1 year and 44% at 5 years5
  • Prevention importance: Early diagnosis and initiation of HRS-AKI-specific treatment can improve outcomes6

Follow-up

+
  • Response assessment: Response to terlipressin should be assessed on day 4 of therapy8
  • Treatment goal: Continue until HRS reversal (sCr ≤1.5 mg/dL on 2 consecutive days) or maximum 14 days8
  • Monitoring adverse effects: Monitor for abdominal pain, diarrhea, or signs/symptoms of mesenteric ischemia8
  • Transplant evaluation: Liver transplantation evaluation should be strongly considered8

Low-Resource Considerations

+

Prevention Strategies

  • Volume expansion during SBP: HRS can be prevented with volume expansion with albumin during spontaneous bacterial peritonitis9
  • Post-paracentesis albumin: Volume expansion after large volume paracentesis9
  • Antibiotic prophylaxis: In patients with advanced cirrhosis and low proteins in ascitic fluid9

Limited Resource Settings

  • Serum creatinine monitoring: Serum Cr alone is limited but still remains the best practical assessment of renal function9
  • Volume status assessment: Clinical context: Careful clinical assessment for hypovolemia before diagnosis
  • Basic supportive care: Clinical context: Infection prevention, careful fluid balance
